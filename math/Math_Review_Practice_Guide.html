AREA 1

Compound Interest as a Geometric Sequence

The big idea in one sentence: every year you earn interest on top of interest you already earned — so the balance grows by the same multiplier each period. That is exactly what a geometric sequence is.

Why it's geometric — $1,000 at 5% per year
Year Balance How you get it Ratio to prev year
Start$1,000.00you deposit this—
1$1,050.00$1,000 × 1.051.05
2$1,102.50$1,050 × 1.051.05
3$1,157.63$1,102.50 × 1.051.05
4$1,215.51$1,157.63 × 1.051.05
The ratio column is always 1.05 → constant ratio = geometric sequence. The "interest on interest" you hear about is just what happens when r > 1.
The key connection:
\[ A = P\cdot(1+r)^n \quad\longleftrightarrow\quad a_n = a_1 \cdot (\text{ratio})^{n-1} \] P = starting amount (= a1)  ·  (1+r) = common ratio  ·  n = number of periods
Worked Example 1 — Annual compounding
You deposit $500 at 6% annual interest. Write the geometric sequence for the first 4 year-end balances, then use the formula to find the year-4 balance.
1
Identify the pieces: P = $500  (= a1),  r = 0.06,  so common ratio = 1 + 0.06 = 1.06
2
Build the sequence by multiplying by 1.06 each time:
$500 → $530.00 → $561.80 → $595.51 → $631.24
3
Formula shortcut (skip straight to year 4):
\(A = 500 \times (1.06)^4 = 500 \times 1.2625 = \mathbf{\$631.24}\) ✓
4
Why n = 4? You want the balance after 4 years of growth, so you apply the ratio 4 times: \(a_1\cdot\text{ratio}^4\). (In the general formula \(a_n = a_1 r^{n-1}\), year-4 balance is term 5, but the compound-interest form \(A = P(1+r)^n\) counts periods directly, so n = 4.)
Worked Example 2 — Quarterly compounding (same money, more periods)
Same $500 and 6% annual rate, but now interest is added every quarter. What is the balance after 3 years? How does that compare to annual compounding?
1
Adjust r and n for quarterly periods.
Annual rate 6% ÷ 4 quarters = 1.5% per quarter = 0.015
3 years × 4 quarters/year = 12 periods
2
New common ratio = 1 + 0.015 = 1.015 (applied 12 times, not 3)
3
\(A = 500 \times (1.015)^{12} = 500 \times 1.19562 = \mathbf{\$597.81}\)
4
Compare to annual compounding (same 3 years):
Annual: \(500\times(1.06)^3 = \$595.51\)    Quarterly: \(\$597.81\)
Quarterly gives $2.30 more — more compounding periods = slightly more growth.
✓
The pattern: more frequent compounding → smaller r per period, more n periods, same nominal rate — always a bit better for the saver.
Quick check trick: multiply your starting amount by the ratio once per period and you should get the next term. If the ratio ever changes, it is not geometric — double-check your r.
Classic mistake: using the annual rate with quarterly n. If you see "compounded monthly / quarterly / semi-annually", you must divide r by the number of periods per year AND multiply n by that same number.
AREA 2

Sums Not Starting at i = 1

Standard summation formulas assume the index starts at 1. When it starts later, use the subtraction trick:

\[ \sum_{i=m}^{n} f(i) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(i) \;-\; \sum_{i=1}^{m-1} f(i) \] Big sum (1 to n) minus the part you don't want (1 to m−1)

The three special formulas you must know:

Sum Formula Quick example (n = 4)
\(\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} c\) \(c \cdot n\) \(\sum_{i=1}^{4} 5 = 5 \times 4 = 20\)
\(\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} i\) \(\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\) \(\sum_{i=1}^{4} i = \frac{4 \cdot 5}{2} = 10\)
\(\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} i^2\) \(\dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}\) \(\sum_{i=1}^{4} i^2 = \frac{4 \cdot 5 \cdot 9}{6} = 30\)
Worked Example
Evaluate \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=3}^{6}(2i^2 + i - 5)\)
1
Split by linearity: \(2\displaystyle\sum_{i=3}^{6} i^2 \;+\; \sum_{i=3}^{6} i \;-\; \sum_{i=3}^{6} 5\)
2
Use the subtraction trick on each piece: each = (sum from 1 to 6) minus (sum from 1 to 2)
3
\(\sum_{i=1}^{6} i^2 = \frac{6\cdot7\cdot13}{6} = 91\),   \(\sum_{i=1}^{2} i^2 = 1+4 = 5\)  →  \(\sum_{i=3}^{6} i^2 = 91-5 = 86\)
4
\(\sum_{i=1}^{6} i = 21\),   \(\sum_{i=1}^{2} i = 3\)  →  \(\sum_{i=3}^{6} i = 18\)
5
\(\sum_{i=3}^{6} 5\): there are \(6-3+1 = 4\) terms → \(5 \times 4 = 20\)
6
Final: \(2(86) + 18 - 20 = 172 + 18 - 20 = \mathbf{170}\)
Counting terms: From \(i = m\) to \(i = n\) there are \((n - m + 1)\) terms. For \(i = 3\) to \(6\): \(6 - 3 + 1 = 4\) terms.
AREA 3

Exponential Expressions → Scientific Notation

The core trick: rewrite everything as a power of 10, then combine exponents using the product rule \(10^a \cdot 10^b = 10^{a+b}\).

\[ 100 \cdot \left(\frac{1}{10}\right)^x = 10^2 \cdot 10^{-x} = 10^{(2-x)} \]
Worked Example 1 — Pure powers of 10
Simplify \(1000 \cdot (0.1)^x\), then evaluate for \(x = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4\).
1
Rewrite: \(1000 = 10^3\) and \(0.1 = 10^{-1}\), so \((0.1)^x = 10^{-x}\)
2
Combine: \(10^3 \cdot 10^{-x} = \mathbf{10^{(3-x)}}\)
3
\(x=0 \to 10^3 = 1\times10^3\)  |  \(x=1 \to 10^2 = 1\times10^2\)  |  \(x=2 \to 10^1 = 1\times10^1\)
4
\(x=3 \to 10^0 = 1\)  |  \(x=4 \to 10^{-1} = 1\times10^{-1}\)
Worked Example 2 — Non-power-of-10 base
Simplify \(4.5 \times 10^3 \cdot (2 \times 10^{-1})^2\) and express in scientific notation.
1
Expand the square: \((2 \times 10^{-1})^2 = 2^2 \times (10^{-1})^2 = 4 \times 10^{-2}\)
2
Multiply: \((4.5 \times 10^3)\times(4\times10^{-2}) = (4.5\times4)\times10^{3+(-2)} = 18\times10^1\)
3
Adjust so coefficient is between 1 and 10: \(18\times10^1 = 1.8\times10^1\times10^1 = \mathbf{1.8\times10^2}\)
Three-step process: (1) Rewrite base as a power of 10 if possible → (2) Combine using \(10^a\cdot10^b=10^{a+b}\) → (3) Adjust so the coefficient is between 1 and 10.
AREA 4

Repeating Decimals → Fractions via Geometric Series

A repeating decimal is actually an infinite geometric series with \(|r| < 1\). Use the infinite sum formula:

\[ S = \frac{a_1}{1 - r} \qquad \text{(valid when } -1 < r < 1\text{)} \]
Worked Example 1
Convert \(0.818181\ldots\) to a fraction in simplest form.
1
Write as a series: \(0.81 + 0.0081 + 0.000081 + \cdots\)
2
First term \(a_1 = 0.81\), common ratio \(r = \frac{0.0081}{0.81} = 0.01\)
3
\(S = \dfrac{0.81}{1 - 0.01} = \dfrac{0.81}{0.99} = \dfrac{81}{99}\)
4
Simplify: \(\gcd(81, 99) = 9\) → \(\dfrac{81}{99} = \mathbf{\dfrac{9}{11}}\)
✓
Check: \(9 \div 11 = 0.818181\ldots\) ✓
Worked Example 2
Convert \(0.123123123\ldots\) to a fraction.
1
Repeating block is "123" (3 digits): \(a_1 = 0.123\), \(r = 0.001\)
2
\(S = \dfrac{0.123}{1-0.001} = \dfrac{0.123}{0.999} = \dfrac{123}{999}\)
3
\(\gcd(123, 999) = 3\) → \(\mathbf{\dfrac{41}{333}}\)
Shortcut: If \(k\) digits repeat, put the repeating block over \(k\) nines. \(0.\overline{81}\) → 2 repeating digits → \(\tfrac{81}{99} = \tfrac{9}{11}\). Works every time!
AREA 5

Sequences with Special Terms at the Beginning

Sometimes the first few terms don't follow the geometric or arithmetic pattern. Identify where the regular pattern begins, sum the "special" part by hand, then apply formulas to the regular part.

Worked Example 1 — Bouncing ball
A ball is dropped from 10 ft. Each bounce reaches 60% of the previous height. Find the total distance traveled after 5 bounces.
1
The special first term: the initial drop is 10 ft downward only — there is no "up" before it, so handle it separately.
2
Bounce heights (60% each time): 6 ft, 3.6 ft, 2.16 ft, 1.296 ft, 0.7776 ft
3
Each bounce = up + down = 2 × height
4
Bounce total = \(2(6 + 3.6 + 2.16 + 1.296 + 0.7776) = 2(13.8336) = 27.667\text{ ft}\)
5
Grand total = \(10 + 27.667 = \mathbf{37.667\text{ ft}}\)
Worked Example 2 — Repeated opening terms
The sequence is 3, 3, 12, 48, 192, … — terms 1–2 are both 3 (not yet geometric), then ratio 4 kicks in. Find the sum of the first 7 terms.
1
Special part (terms 1–2): \(3 + 3 = 6\)
2
Geometric part (terms 3–7): \(a_1 = 12\), \(r = 4\), 5 terms
3
\(S_5 = 12\cdot\dfrac{4^5 - 1}{4-1} = 12\cdot\dfrac{1023}{3} = 12\times341 = 4{,}092\)
4
Total = \(6 + 4{,}092 = \mathbf{4{,}098}\)
Strategy: (1) Find where the regular pattern starts → (2) Sum all "special" terms by hand → (3) Apply the geometric/arithmetic formula to the regular part → (4) Add the two results.

Problem 1
Area 1 — Compound interest
$3,500 is invested at 5% interest compounded semi-annually. (a) How much is in the account after 3 years? (b) After how many full semi-annual periods does the account first exceed $4,500?
1
Rate per period = \(5\%\div2 = 2.5\% = 0.025\). Periods in 3 years = 6.
2
(a) \(A = 3500\times(1.025)^6 = 3500\times1.15969 = \mathbf{\$4{,}058.91}\)
3
(b) Solve \(3500\times(1.025)^n > 4500\) → \((1.025)^n > 1.2857\)
4
Take logs: \(n > \dfrac{\log 1.2857}{\log 1.025} = \dfrac{0.10926}{0.01072} \approx 10.19\)
5
First whole period: \(\mathbf{n = 11}\) semi-annual periods (= 5.5 years)
Problem 2
Area 2 — Sum of squares, not starting at i = 1
Evaluate: \[\sum_{i=3}^{8} i^2\]
1
\(\displaystyle\sum_{i=3}^{8}i^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{8}i^2 - \sum_{i=1}^{2}i^2\)
2
\(\sum_{i=1}^{8}i^2 = \dfrac{8\cdot9\cdot17}{6} = \dfrac{1224}{6} = 204\)
3
\(\sum_{i=1}^{2}i^2 = 1^2+2^2 = 1+4 = 5\)
4
Answer: \(204 - 5 = \mathbf{199}\)
✓
Check: \(9+16+25+36+49+64 = 199\) ✓
Problem 3
Area 3 — Scientific notation, general base
Simplify \(5\times10^2\cdot(2\times10^{-1})^3\) and express the answer in proper scientific notation.
1
Expand the cube: \((2\times10^{-1})^3 = 2^3\times(10^{-1})^3 = 8\times10^{-3}\)
2
Multiply: \((5\times10^2)\times(8\times10^{-3}) = (5\times8)\times10^{2+(-3)} = 40\times10^{-1}\)

Adjust: \(40\times10^{-1} = 4.0\times10^1\times10^{-1} = \mathbf{4.0\times10^0 = 4}\)
Problem 4
Area 4 — Mixed repeating decimal
Convert \(0.3272727\ldots\) to a fraction. Note: only the "27" repeats; the leading "3" does not.

Split: \(0.3\) (non-repeating) + \(0.0\overline{27}\) (repeating)

Repeating part: \(a_1 = 0.027,\; r = 0.01\) → \(S = \dfrac{0.027}{0.99} = \dfrac{27}{990} = \dfrac{3}{110}\)

Non-repeating: \(0.3 = \dfrac{3}{10}\)
4
Total: \(\dfrac{3}{10}+\dfrac{3}{110} = \dfrac{33}{110}+\dfrac{3}{110} = \dfrac{36}{110} = \mathbf{\dfrac{18}{55}}\)

A ball is dropped from 16 feet. Each bounce reaches 75% of the previous height. Find the total distance traveled when the ball hits the ground for the 4th time (i.e., after exactly 3 bounces).

2
Bounce heights: 1st = 12 ft, 2nd = 9 ft, 3rd = 6.75 ft
3

Mixed challenge — some problems combine two focus areas.
Problem 1
Areas 1 + 3 — Exponential growth meets scientific notation
A scientist places 1 bacterium in a dish. The population triples every hour. (a) Write a formula for the population \(P(x)\) after \(x\) hours. (b) Find \(P(8)\) in scientific notation. (c) Explain why this has the same mathematical structure as compound interest.
1
(a) \(P(x) = 1\cdot3^x = \mathbf{3^x}\)   (geometric, \(a_1=1,\; r=3\))
2
(b) \(P(8) = 3^8 = 6{,}561 = \mathbf{6.561\times10^3}\)
3
(c) Compound interest: \(A = P(1+r)^n\). Bacteria: \(P=1\cdot3^x\). Identical structure — both multiply by a fixed ratio each period. Only the ratio differs (1.06 vs 3).
Problem 2
Area 2 — Mixed special sum
Evaluate: \[\sum_{i=2}^{5}(i^2 - 3i + 1)\]
1
Split: \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=2}^{5}i^2 \;-\; 3\sum_{i=2}^{5}i \;+\; \sum_{i=2}^{5}1\)
2
\(\sum_{i=1}^{5}i^2 = \frac{5\cdot6\cdot11}{6} = 55\),   \(\sum_{i=1}^{1}i^2 = 1\) → \(\sum_{i=2}^{5}i^2 = 54\)
3
\(\sum_{i=1}^{5}i = 15\),   \(\sum_{i=1}^{1}i = 1\) → \(\sum_{i=2}^{5}i = 14\)
4
\(\sum_{i=2}^{5}1 = 4\) terms \(\times\,1 = 4\)
5
Answer: \(54 - 3(14) + 4 = 54 - 42 + 4 = \mathbf{16}\)
✓
Check: \((-1)+1+5+11 = 16\) ✓
Problem 3
Area 3 — Finding the term index
The sequence is defined by \(a_n = 6.4\times10^5\cdot(0.5)^n\). Find \(a_4\) in proper scientific notation. Then find the smallest \(n\) for which \(a_n < 1{,}000\).
1
\(a_4 = 6.4\times10^5\times(0.5)^4 = 640{,}000\times0.0625 = 40{,}000 = \mathbf{4.0\times10^4}\)
2
Solve \(6.4\times10^5\cdot(0.5)^n < 1000\) → \((0.5)^n < \dfrac{1000}{640000} \approx 0.001563\)
3
\(n > \dfrac{\log(0.001563)}{\log(0.5)} = \dfrac{-2.806}{-0.301} \approx 9.32\)
4
Smallest integer: \(\mathbf{n = 10}\)
Problem 4
Area 4 — Prove an identity
A student claims \(0.9999\ldots = 1\) exactly (not "approximately"). Use the infinite geometric series formula to prove or disprove this claim. Show all steps.
1
Write as series: \(0.9 + 0.09 + 0.009 + \cdots\)  →  \(a_1 = 0.9,\; r = 0.1\)
2
\(|r| = 0.1 < 1\), so the infinite sum formula applies.
3
\(S = \dfrac{0.9}{1-0.1} = \dfrac{0.9}{0.9} = \mathbf{1}\)
4
The student is correct. \(0.\overline{9} = 1\) exactly — this is a proven mathematical fact, not an approximation.
Problem 5
Area 5 — Special start, real-world context
A social media post gets 2 shares on Day 1 and 2 shares on Day 2 (spreading slowly). Starting Day 3, each day's shares are exactly triple the previous day's. Find the total number of shares over all 7 days.
1
Special part (Days 1–2): \(2+2 = 4\) shares
2
Geometric part starts Day 3: \(a_1 = 2\times3 = 6,\; r=3\), and there are 5 terms (Days 3–7)
3
\(S_5 = 6\cdot\dfrac{3^5-1}{3-1} = 6\cdot\dfrac{242}{2} = 6\times121 = 726\)
4
Total = \(4+726 = \mathbf{730\text{ shares}}\)
✓
Sequence: 2, 2, 6, 18, 54, 162, 486  →  sum = 730 ✓
