Trigonometric Ratios and Functions (Right Triangles, Radians, Any Angle)

SECTION 8.1 — Right Triangle Trigonometry

Right Triangle Anatomy

In a right triangle, the side opposite the right angle is the hypotenuse (longest side). The other two sides are legs. For an acute angle θ inside the triangle, the leg across from θ is the opposite side; the leg touching θ (that isn't the hypotenuse) is the adjacent side.

Pythagorean Theorem: a² + b² = c² (c = hypotenuse)

The Six Trig Ratios (SOH-CAH-TOA + reciprocals)

sin θ = opp / hyp csc θ = 1 / sin θ = hyp / opp cos θ = adj / hyp sec θ = 1 / cos θ = hyp / adj tan θ = opp / adj cot θ = 1 / tan θ = adj / opp

Reciprocal pairs: sin↔csc, cos↔sec, tan↔cot. These ratios are valid only for acute angles (0° < θ < 90°) inside a right triangle.

Pythagorean Triples (memorize)

(3, 4, 5) (5, 12, 13) (7, 24, 25) (8, 15, 17) — and any multiple of these.

Solving a Right Triangle

"Solve △ABC" means find every unknown side and angle. With a right angle at C, you generally need either two sides OR one side + one acute angle. Strategy:

  • The two acute angles add to 90° (so ∠A + ∠B = 90°).
  • Pick a trig ratio whose two sides are (one known, one unknown).
  • For unknown sides: multiply or divide. Example: cos 34° = 13/c → c = 13 / cos 34°.
  • Always confirm with Pythagoras when possible.

SECTION 8.2 — Angles and Radian Measure

Standard Position

An angle is in standard position when its vertex is at the origin and its initial side lies along the positive x-axis. A positive angle rotates counterclockwise; a negative angle rotates clockwise. The ray you end on is the terminal side.

Coterminal Angles

Two angles are coterminal if they share the same terminal side. To find coterminal angles, add or subtract any multiple of 360°.

Coterminal with θ: θ + 360°·n, where n is any integer

Examples: 30° and 390° (= 30 + 360) and −330° (= 30 − 360) are all coterminal.

Radians

A radian is the angle that subtends an arc length equal to one radius. Definition:

θ (in radians) = arc length / radius = ℓ / r

A full circle has circumference 2πr, so a full rotation = 2π rad = 360°. A half rotation = π rad = 180°.

Conversion (key fact): 180° = π radians
  Degrees → Radians: multiply by π / 180°
  Radians → Degrees: multiply by 180° / π

Useful approximation: 1 rad ≈ 57.3°.

Arc Length and Sector Area

For a sector of a circle with radius r and central angle θ in radians:

Arc length: ℓ = θ · r Sector area: A = (1/2) · θ · r²

If θ is given in degrees, first convert to radians (multiply by π/180°) before using these formulas.

SECTION 8.3 — Trigonometric Functions of Any Angle

General Definitions

Let (x, y) be any point (other than the origin) on the terminal side of an angle θ in standard position, and let r be the distance from the origin to (x, y):

r = √x² + y² (always positive)
sin θ = y / r csc θ = r / y (y ≠ 0) cos θ = x / r sec θ = r / x (x ≠ 0) tan θ = y / x (x ≠ 0) cot θ = x / y (y ≠ 0)

These extend the right-triangle definitions to any angle θ (positive, negative, or larger than 90°).

The Unit Circle

The circle x² + y² = 1 has center (0, 0) and radius r = 1. On the unit circle, sin θ = y and cos θ = x, which simplifies many evaluations.

Quadrantal Angles

A quadrantal angle is one whose terminal side lies on an axis (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°, 360°, ...). At these angles, x or y equals 0, so some trig functions are undefined (division by zero).

Worked Example (Section 8.3, Ex 1)

Let (8, −6) be on the terminal side of θ. Find r, then all six trig functions.

  • r = √8² + (−6)² = √64 + 36 = √100 = 10
  • sin θ = y/r = −6/10 = −3/5, csc θ = −5/3
  • cos θ = x/r = 8/10 = 4/5, sec θ = 5/4
  • tan θ = y/x = −6/8 = −3/4, cot θ = −4/3

Master Problem-Solving Checklist

  • Right triangle? (8.1) Identify hyp / opp / adj relative to the angle, choose SOH-CAH-TOA. For special triangles, recognize the ratios first.
  • Converting angle units? (8.2) Multiply by π/180 (deg → rad) or by 180/π (rad → deg). Keep π symbolic when possible.
  • Coterminal? (8.2) Add/subtract 360° (or 2π) until in the desired range.
  • Arc / sector? (8.2) Convert θ to radians first; then ℓ = θr and A = (1/2)θr².
  • Any angle? (8.3) Compute r = √x² + y², then apply general definitions. Check signs against the quadrant.
  • Quadrantal? (8.3) Identify (x, y) on the axis; watch for zeros that make tan, cot, sec, or csc undefined.
