10.1 Using Normal Distributions

Normal Distribution Properties

A normal distribution is symmetric and bell-shaped, defined by mean μ and standard deviation σ.

Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7):
68% of data within 1σ of mean (μ±σ)
95% of data within 2σ of mean (μ±2σ)
99.7% of data within 3σ of mean (μ±3σ)
z-score:   z = (x − μ)/σ

z-score = number of standard deviations from the mean.

Using z-scores and z-tables

A standard normal distribution has μ=0 and σ=1.

The z-table gives P(Z < z) — the area to the LEFT of z.

Finding probabilities:

  • P(X < a) = P(Z < z_a)
  • P(X > a) = 1 − P(Z < z_a)
  • P(a < X < b) = P(Z < z_b) − P(Z < z_a)
⚠ Always draw and shade the normal curve before computing.

Examples

Ex 1 — Test scores: μ=72, σ=8. What % score between 64 and 88?

64=72−8=μ−σ; 88=72+2σ

P(μ−σ<X<μ+2σ)=34%+34%+13.5%=81.5%

Ex 2 — Height: μ=67 in, σ=3 in. Find z-score for 73 in and P(X<73).

z=(73−67)/3=2.0; P(Z<2.0)=0.9772 (97.72%)

Ex 3 — P(X>70): μ=65, σ=5. Find z; use table.

z=(70−65)/5=1.0; P(Z>1.0)=1−0.8413=0.1587 (15.87%)

Ex 4 — P(60<X<75): μ=65, σ=5.

z₁=(60−65)/5=−1; z₂=(75−65)/5=2

P=P(Z<2)−P(Z<−1)=0.9772−0.1587=0.8185 (81.85%)

10.2 Populations, Samples, and Hypotheses

Population vs. Sample

Population: the entire group being studied

Sample: a subset of the population used to draw conclusions

Parameter: a numerical summary of a population (e.g., μ, σ)

Statistic: a numerical summary of a sample (e.g., x̄, s)

Types of samples:

  • Simple random: every member equally likely to be chosen
  • Stratified: divide into groups (strata), randomly sample each
  • Systematic: choose every k-th member of a list
  • Cluster: randomly select groups (clusters), sample all within
  • Convenience: use whoever is easy to reach (often biased)

Hypotheses

Null hypothesis H₀: claims no effect or no difference; the "status quo."

Alternative hypothesis Hₐ: claims there IS an effect or difference.

Example:

H₀: The new drug has no effect on blood pressure.

Hₐ: The new drug reduces blood pressure.

Error types:

Type I (α): Reject H₀ when it's actually true (false positive)

Type II (β): Fail to reject H₀ when Hₐ is true (false negative)

10.3 Collecting Data

Study Types

Survey: collects data via questions; no treatment given.

Experiment: applies a treatment; can establish cause-and-effect.

Observational study: observes without interfering; can only show correlation, not causation.

Sources of Bias

  • Voluntary response: people opt in (self-selection)
  • Undercoverage: some groups excluded from sample
  • Nonresponse: chosen subjects don't respond
  • Wording bias: leading or misleading questions
  • Convenience sampling: only accessible people sampled

Simulation

A simulation uses a model to imitate a real process when direct data collection is impractical.

Steps: define model → assign random digits/outcomes → run trials → analyze results.

Example: flip a coin 50 times using a random-number generator.

10.4 Experimental Design

Elements of a Well-Designed Experiment

Experimental units: the subjects or objects being tested.

Treatment: the condition applied to experimental units.

Control group: receives no treatment (or a placebo).

Treatment group: receives the treatment being tested.

Randomization: randomly assign subjects to groups to reduce bias.

Replication: repeat the experiment to reduce variability.

Blinding: subjects don't know which group they're in.

Double-blinding: neither subjects nor researchers know assignments.

Placebo effect: improvement seen just from believing one is receiving treatment.

Confounding Variables & Blocking

A confounding variable is a variable that affects the response but is NOT the factor being studied. It can create a false appearance of cause-and-effect.

Blocking: divide subjects into groups (blocks) based on a known variable (e.g., gender, age), then randomize within each block.

10.5 Making Inferences from Sample Surveys

Margin of Error & Confidence Intervals

Margin of Error (95% CI): ME = 1/√n
Confidence interval: p̂ ± ME = p̂ ± 1/√n

Interpretation: "We are 95% confident the true population proportion p is within the interval [p̂−ME, p̂+ME]."

Sample size: larger n → smaller ME → more precise estimate

To halve ME, quadruple sample size: ME ∝ 1/√n

Examples

Ex 5 — Survey: 400 people; 52% prefer Brand A. Find 95% CI.

ME = 1/√400 = 1/20 = 0.05 (5%)

CI: 0.52 ± 0.05 = [0.47, 0.57] or 47% to 57%

Ex 6 — To achieve ME ≤ 2%, what sample size is needed?

1/√n ≤ 0.02 → √n ≥ 50 → n ≥ 2500

At least 2500 respondents

10.6 Making Inferences from Experiments

Statistical Significance

A result is statistically significant when it is unlikely to have occurred by chance alone.

Significance level α: typically 0.05. If the probability of observing a result as extreme as the one obtained (the p-value) is less than α, we reject H₀.

p-value < α → Reject H₀ (statistically significant)
p-value ≥ α → Fail to reject H₀ (not significant)

Comparing Results Using Simulations

To determine if a difference between treatment and control groups is significant:

  1. Assume H₀: no difference (any difference is due to chance)
  2. Simulate many re-randomizations of subjects
  3. Find what proportion of simulated differences ≥ observed difference
  4. If that proportion < 5%, the result is significant

A result in the outer 5% of the simulated distribution is considered statistically significant at the 0.05 level.
