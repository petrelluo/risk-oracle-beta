How to Read the Map of an Atom

Electron configuration is like a secret map. It tells us exactly how a specific atom's electrons are arranged around its nucleus. Think of the nucleus as a stage, and the electrons as the audience sitting in very specific sections and rows.

1. The Seats (Shells & Subshells)

Electrons sit in layers called Shells (numbered 1, 2, 3...). Shell 1 is closest to the nucleus and has the lowest energy. Inside each shell are different types of "sections" called Subshells, labeled by letters (s, p, d, f). They have specific shapes and hold a maximum number of electrons.

🤔 Why do they hold exactly 2, 6, 10, or 14 electrons?

It all comes down to Orbitals (individual seats inside the section) and a rule called the Pauli Exclusion Principle. This rule states that a single exact seat (an orbital) can hold exactly 2 electrons maximum (one spinning "up" ↑, and one spinning "down" ↓).

Because of 3D geometry and quantum physics, each subshell has a strictly odd number of available orbitals. You just multiply the number of orbitals by 2:

  • s subshell: has 1 orbital × 2 electrons = 2 max
  • p subshell: has 3 orbitals × 2 electrons = 6 max
  • d subshell: has 5 orbitals × 2 electrons = 10 max
  • f subshell: has 7 orbitals × 2 electrons = 14 max

2. The Rules of Filling Seats

  1. The Aufbau Principle: Electrons are "lazy"—they always sit in the lowest available energy seat first. They follow the diagonal path shown in the diagram to the right.
  2. The Pauli Exclusion Principle: A single exact seat (an orbital box) can hold exactly 2 electrons, but they must spin in opposite directions.
  3. Hund's Rule (The Bus Seat Rule): When electrons are filling a section with multiple seats (like the 'p' subshell), they prefer to have their own seat. They will spread out into empty seats first before pairing up!
Example - Oxygen (Atomic Number 8, 8 Electrons):
1. Fill 1s with 2 electrons (6 left) → 1s²
2. Fill 2s with 2 electrons (4 left) → 2s²
3. Put remaining 4 into 2p (which holds 6) → 2p⁴
Result: 1s² 2s² 2p⁴
