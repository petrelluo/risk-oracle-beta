How to Read the Map of an Atom

Electron configuration is like a seating chart for an atom. It tells us exactly where the electrons "sit" around the nucleus.

The Seats (Shells & Subshells)

Electrons sit in layers called Shells (1, 2, 3...). Inside each shell are sections called Subshells (s, p, d, f).

  • s subshell: Holds up to 2 electrons.
  • p subshell: Holds up to 6 electrons.
  • d subshell: Holds up to 10 electrons.
  • f subshell: Holds up to 14 electrons.

The 3 Golden Rules

  1. The Aufbau Principle: Electrons are lazy. They always fill the lowest energy seats first (1s, then 2s, then 2p...).
  2. The Pauli Exclusion Principle: A single exact seat (an orbital box) can hold exactly 2 electrons, but they must spin in opposite directions (one up ↑, one down ↓).
  3. Hund's Rule: When electrons sit in a section with multiple seats (like 'p' which has 3 boxes), they prefer to sit alone first! They put one electron in each empty box before they start pairing up.

Example - Oxygen (8 Electrons):
Fill 1s (2) → Fill 2s (2) → Remaining 4 go into 2p.
Result: 1s² 2s² 2p⁴
