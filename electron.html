How to Read the Map of an Atom

Electron configuration is like a secret map. It tells us exactly how a specific atom's electrons are arranged around its nucleus. Think of the nucleus as a stage, and the electrons as the audience sitting in very specific sections and rows.

1. The Seats (Shells & Subshells)

Electrons sit in layers called Shells (numbered 1, 2, 3...). Shell 1 is closest to the nucleus and has the lowest energy. Inside each shell are different types of "sections" called Subshells, labeled by letters (s, p, d, f). They have specific shapes and hold a maximum number of electrons.

2. The Rules of Filling Seats

  1. The Aufbau Principle: Electrons are "lazy"—they always sit in the lowest available energy seat first. They follow the diagonal path shown in the diagram to the right.
  2. The Pauli Exclusion Principle: A single exact seat (an orbital box) can hold exactly 2 electrons, but they must spin in opposite directions (represented as one up ↑, one down ↓).
  3. Hund's Rule (The Bus Seat Rule): When electrons are filling a section with multiple seats (like the 'p' subshell, which has 3 boxes), they prefer to have their own seat. They will spread out into empty seats first before pairing up!

Aufbau Diagonal Rule

Example - Oxygen (Atomic Number 8, so 8 Electrons):
1. Fill 1s with 2 electrons (6 left) → 1s²
2. Fill 2s with 2 electrons (4 left) → 2s²
3. Put remaining 4 into 2p (which can hold up to 6) → 2p⁴
Result: 1s² 2s² 2p⁴
