Part 1: Why Do Atoms Bond?

Atoms crave stability! For most atoms, being "stable" means having a full outer shell of 8 electrons (the Octet Rule). If they don't have 8, they will steal, give away, or share electrons with other atoms to get there. The magnetic attraction that holds them together afterward is called a Chemical Bond.

The Octet Rule
Atoms want 8 outer electrons.

1. Ionic Bonds (The "Give & Take")

Who: Metals + Non-Metals.

How: A metal (like Sodium) completely gives an electron to a non-metal (like Chlorine). This makes the metal Positive (+) and the non-metal Negative (-). Like magnets, they stick together!

2. Covalent Bonds ("Sharing is Caring")

Who: Non-Metals + Non-Metals.

How: When both atoms need electrons, neither will give one away. Instead, they overlap their outer shells and share electrons to build a bridge.

3. Metallic Bonds (The "Communal Sea")

Who: Metals + Metals.

How: Metal atoms let their outer electrons flow away freely. The solid metal is held together by a rigid grid of positive nuclei sitting in a flexible "sea" of shared, floating electrons. This is why metals bend without snapping and conduct electricity!

4. Polyatomic Ions (The Hybrid)

Sometimes you get both! A polyatomic ion is a cluster of atoms joined by covalent bonds, but the entire cluster has gained or lost an extra electron, giving it a charge. Because of that charge, the whole group can now form ionic bonds with other atoms.

Part 2: Molecular Shapes (VSEPR Theory)

When atoms share electrons (covalent bonds), they form a molecule. But they don't sit flat! Because electrons are all negative, they repel each other. Bonds and lone pairs push as far apart from each other as physically possible in 3D space. This creates specific shapes.

Part 3: Polarity & Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

Polar Molecules (Unequal Sharing)

In a covalent bond, sometimes one atom is "greedier" than the other. Oxygen, for example, pulls shared electrons closer to itself than Hydrogen does. This makes the Oxygen side slightly negative (δ-) and the Hydrogen side slightly positive (δ+). This creates a Polar Molecule—a tiny molecular magnet!

Intermolecular Forces (The Glue Between Molecules)

Chemical bonds hold atoms together inside a molecule. IMFs are the magnetic pulls between separate molecules. When you boil water, you aren't breaking the H and O apart; you are using heat to break the IMFs so the molecules can float away as gas.

  • London Dispersion Forces: Weakest. Happens in all molecules. Easy to boil.
  • Dipole-Dipole: Medium strength. The (+) end of one polar molecule attracts the (-) end of another.
  • Hydrogen Bonding: Strongest! Only happens when Hydrogen bonds to greedy N, O, or F. It acts like molecular super-velcro. This is why Water (H₂O) takes so much heat to boil!
