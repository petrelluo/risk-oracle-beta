Part 1: Why Do Atoms Bond?

Atoms crave stability! For most atoms, being "stable" means having a full outer shell of 8 electrons (the Octet Rule). If they don't have 8, they will steal, give away, or share electrons with other atoms. The magnetic attraction that holds them together afterward is called a Chemical Bond.

1. Ionic Bonds (The "Give & Take")

Who: Metals + Non-Metals.

How: A metal (like Sodium) completely gives an electron to a non-metal (like Chlorine). This makes the metal Positive (+) and the non-metal Negative (-). Like magnets, they stick together!

2. Covalent Bonds ("Sharing is Caring")

Who: Non-Metals + Non-Metals.

How: When both atoms need electrons, neither will give one away. Instead, they overlap their outer shells and share electrons to build a bridge.

Single, Double, and Triple Bonds

Atoms can share more than one pair of electrons if they are really desperate to reach 8!

Hydrogen gas (H₂)

3. Metallic Bonds (The "Communal Sea")

Who: Metals + Metals.

How: Metal atoms let their outer electrons flow away freely into a "sea" of shared electrons. This is why metals bend without snapping and conduct electricity!

Part 2: Molecular Shapes (VSEPR Theory)

Because electrons are all negatively charged, they repel each other. The bonds and lone pairs (unshared electrons) around a central atom will push as far apart as physically possible in 3D space to avoid each other. This creates specific geometric shapes!

Part 3: Polarity & Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

Polar Molecules (The Electron Tug of War)

In a covalent bond, atoms share electrons, but rarely equally! Electronegativity is how "greedy" an atom is for electrons. If a greedy atom (like Fluorine) bonds with a weak atom (like Hydrogen), the greedy atom pulls the shared electrons closer to itself.

This creates a Polar Molecule: One side is slightly negative (δ-), and the other side is slightly positive (δ+). It acts just like a tiny magnet!

Intermolecular Forces (The Glue Between Molecules)

Chemical bonds hold atoms together inside a molecule. IMFs are the magnetic pulls between separate molecules. When you boil water, you break the IMFs, allowing the molecules to float away as gas.

1. London Dispersion

Weakest. Temporary shifting of electron clouds in all molecules.

2. Dipole-Dipole

Medium strength. The permanent (+) end attracts the (-) end.

3. Hydrogen Bonding

Strongest! Super-velcro when H bonds directly to greedy N, O, or F.
