1. Start by identifying the type of compound

This is the fastest way to choose the right naming rule.

What you noticeLikely typeMain rule
Metal + nonmetalIonic compoundName cation first, then anion
Metal + polyatomic ionTernary ionic compoundKeep polyatomic ion name unchanged
Nonmetal + nonmetalMolecular / covalent compoundUse Greek prefixes
Starts with H and acts as an acidAcidUse acid naming rules
Contains OH-BaseName metal + hydroxide

2. Binary ionic compounds: the anion ends in -ide

These contain only two elements, usually a metal and a nonmetal. Name the metal first. Change the nonmetal ending to -ide.

FormulaNameWhy
NaClSodium chlorideChlorine becomes chloride
MgOMagnesium oxideOxygen becomes oxide
CaSCalcium sulfideSulfur becomes sulfide
AlBr3Aluminum bromideBromine becomes bromide

Common -ide endings: oxide, sulfide, nitride, phosphide, fluoride, chloride, bromide, iodide, hydride, selenide.

3. Transition metals: use Roman numerals when the metal can have more than one charge

If the metal can form different ions, show the metal charge with a Roman numeral. The numeral applies only to the metal.

FormulaNameMetal charge
FeOIron(II) oxideFe2+
Fe2O3Iron(III) oxideFe3+
CuClCopper(I) chlorideCu+
CuCl2Copper(II) chlorideCu2+
SnS2Tin(IV) sulfideSn4+

No Roman numeral is usually needed for Group 1 metals, Group 2 metals, Al3+, Zn2+, Ag+, and Cd2+.

4. Polyatomic ions: keep the ion name together

When you see a known polyatomic ion, do not rename part of it to -ide. Treat the whole ion as one unit.

FormulaNamePolyatomic ion
NaNO3Sodium nitrateNO3- = nitrate
CaCO3Calcium carbonateCO32- = carbonate
KOHPotassium hydroxideOH- = hydroxide
(NH4)2SO4Ammonium sulfateNH4+ and SO42-

Very common polyatomic ions: nitrate, nitrite, sulfate, sulfite, carbonate, phosphate, phosphite, hydroxide, acetate, cyanide, chlorate, chlorite, perchlorate, permanganate, chromate, ammonium.

5. Understanding -ate and -ite

For related oxyanions, -ate usually means more oxygen, and -ite means less oxygen.

More oxygenLess oxygenExample pair
NitrateNitriteNO3- vs NO2-
SulfateSulfiteSO42- vs SO32-
PhosphatePhosphitePO43- vs PO33-
ChlorateChloriteClO3- vs ClO2-

Memory idea: ate has a bit more oxygen than ite.

6. Prefix families for oxyanions: per- and hypo-

Some oxyanion families use extra prefixes to show the relative number of oxygens.

NameMeaningExample
per-...-ateMost oxygenClO4- = perchlorate
...-ateNext most oxygenClO3- = chlorate
...-iteFewer oxygenClO2- = chlorite
hypo-...-iteLeast oxygenClO- = hypochlorite

This same pattern can appear in bromine and iodine oxyanion families too.

7. Binary molecular compounds: use Greek prefixes

These are usually nonmetal + nonmetal. Use prefixes to show how many atoms are present. The second element ends in -ide.

PrefixNumberExample
mono-1CO = carbon monoxide
di-2CO2 = carbon dioxide
tri-3N2O3 = dinitrogen trioxide
tetra-4CCl4 = carbon tetrachloride
penta-5P2O5 = diphosphorus pentoxide
hexa-6XeF6 = xenon hexafluoride
hepta-7Cl2O7 = dichlorine heptoxide
octa-8N2O8 = dinitrogen octoxide
nona-9I2O9 = diiodine nonoxide
deca-10S2F10 = disulfur decafluoride

Important: Do not usually use mono- on the first element. So CO is carbon monoxide, not monocarbon monoxide.

Sometimes the final vowel of a prefix is dropped to make the name easier to say.

9. Acids: connect the ion ending to the acid ending

Acid naming depends on the anion it comes from.

Anion endingAcid patternExample
-idehydro- root -ic acidHCl = hydrochloric acid
-ateroot -ic acidHNO3 = nitric acid
-iteroot -ous acidHNO2 = nitrous acid

Classic examples: H2SO4 = sulfuric acid, H2SO3 = sulfurous acid, HBr = hydrobromic acid, HClO4 = perchloric acid, HClO = hypochlorous acid.

10. Bases: most classroom bases contain hydroxide

If the compound contains OH-, name the cation first, then add hydroxide.

FormulaName
NaOHSodium hydroxide
Ca(OH)2Calcium hydroxide
Fe(OH)3Iron(III) hydroxide
CuOHCopper(I) hydroxide

Use this mini-checklist exactly like the decision tree so you can decide the naming system before you start naming.

Fast pattern reminders: binary ionic usually ends in -ide; oxyanion families often use -ate / -ite / per- / hypo-; molecular compounds use prefixes such as di-, tri-, tetra-.

12. Formula writing tips for practice

When converting a name to a formula:

StepWhat to doExample

2Balance total positive and negative charge2 Fe3+ with 3 SO42-
3Use parentheses if more than one polyatomic ion is neededFe2(SO4)3
4Reduce subscripts to lowest whole-number ratioCaO, not Ca2O2

14. Common mistakes to avoid

Use these checks while practicing:

  • Do not use prefixes for ionic compounds.
  • Do not change polyatomic ions into -ide names.
  • Do not forget Roman numerals for variable-charge metals.
  • Do not put hydro- on oxyacids such as HNO3 or H2SO4.
  • Do not use -ic acid for anions ending in -ite; those become -ous acid.
  • For molecular compounds, remember the second element always ends in -ide.

15. Very common polyatomic ions to memorize

These ions appear constantly in ionic compounds, bases, and acids. Memorizing them makes naming much faster because their names usually stay unchanged inside larger compounds.
