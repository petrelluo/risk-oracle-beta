Covalent Bonding & IMFs

A Step-by-Step Interactive Tutorial

Step 1: Bonding Definitions & Bond Types

Covalent Bonds form when nonmetal atoms share their valence electrons to achieve a stable outer shell. Because both atoms want electrons, they compromise by sharing them.

Ionic Bonds form when a metal atom completely transfers its valence electrons to a nonmetal atom. This creates oppositely charged ions that strongly attract each other.

We use Electronegativity (EN)—a measure of how strongly an atom pulls on electrons—to predict exactly how the electrons will behave. The yellow dots in the diagrams below represent the valence electrons being bonded.

How a Covalent Bond Forms: H₂ (Hydrogen)

Both hydrogen atoms share their single electron. Each now "sees" 2 electrons — a full, stable outer shell (duet). This shared pair is the covalent bond.

Step 2: Varieties of Covalent Bonds & Polyatomic Ions

Not all covalent bonds are exactly the same. They can vary based on how many electrons are shared, where those electrons come from, and the overall electrical charge of the bonded group.

  • 1. Single, Double, and Triple Bonds

    Atoms can share more than one pair of electrons to reach a stable state. As they share more electrons, the atoms are pulled closer together, making the bond shorter and stronger.

    • Single Bond: 1 pair (2 e⁻) shared. Longest and weakest. (Ex: H-H)
    • Double Bond: 2 pairs (4 e⁻) shared. Shorter and stronger. (Ex: O=O)
    • Triple Bond: 3 pairs (6 e⁻) shared. Shortest and strongest. (Ex: N≡N)

  • 2. Coordinate Covalent Bond (Dative Bond)

    A normal covalent bond forms when each atom contributes one electron. In a coordinate covalent bond, one atom generously contributes both of the shared electrons. Once formed, it behaves exactly like a normal covalent bond. (Ex: Carbon Monoxide, CO)

  • 3. Polyatomic Ions

    A tightly bound group of atoms held together by covalent bonds, but the entire group has an overall charge, meaning it behaves as a single ion. They form ionic bonds with other oppositely charged ions. (Ex: Ammonium NH₄⁺, Sulfate SO₄²⁻)

Step 3: Drawing Electron Dot (Lewis) Structures

A Lewis Structure is a 2D drawing that shows exactly how valence electrons are distributed in a molecule. To draw them perfectly every time, follow these 5 steps:

  1. Count total valence electrons: Add up the valence electrons for all atoms. If it's an anion (negative), add extra electrons. If it's a cation (positive), subtract electrons.
  2. Determine the Central Atom: Usually the least electronegative element. (Note: Hydrogen is NEVER in the center).
  3. Draw Single Bonds: Draw a line connecting the central atom to each outer atom. Each line represents 2 shared electrons. Subtract these from your total.
  4. Fill Outer Octets: Distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs (dots) around the outer atoms until they are happy (8 for an octet, 2 for H's duet).
  5. Check the Central Atom: Place any leftover electrons on the central atom. If the central atom does NOT have a full octet, erase a lone pair from an outer atom and turn it into a double or triple bond to share it.

Example 1: Water (H₂O)

2(1) + 6 = 8 e⁻ Total

After drawing single bonds, the remaining 4 electrons sit on Oxygen as lone pairs.

Example 2: Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

4 + 2(6) = 16 e⁻ Total

There aren't enough electrons to give Carbon an octet, so Oxygen shares more to make double bonds.

Example 3: Cyanide Ion (CN⁻)

4 + 5 + 1(ion) = 10 e⁻ Total

A triple bond is required to satisfy both octets. The brackets and minus sign denote the polyatomic ion.

Step 5: Molecular Polarity & Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

Part A: Molecular Polarity

Before we can determine how molecules interact with each other, we must figure out if the entire molecule is polar or nonpolar. This depends on both the bond types (Step 1) and the 3D shape (Step 4).

Nonpolar Molecule (Symmetrical)

Ex: Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

Even though the C=O bonds are polar, the linear shape is perfectly symmetrical. The dipole arrows pull in opposite directions and cancel out. The molecule is nonpolar.

Polar Molecule (Asymmetrical)

Ex: Water (H₂O)

The bent shape is asymmetrical. The polar bonds pull oxygen's way and do not cancel. This creates a net dipole (a permanently positive and negative end).

Part B: Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

Now that we know if a molecule is polar or nonpolar, we can determine its IMFs. While covalent bonds hold atoms together inside a molecule, IMFs are the weaker forces of attraction between separate molecules. There are three main types:

IMF Type Strength What causes it? Example
London Dispersion (LDF) Weakest Temporary shifting of electrons. Exists in ALL molecules, but is the only force in nonpolar molecules. Gets stronger with larger atoms. CH₄, Cl₂
Dipole-Dipole Medium Attraction between the permanent partial charges (δ⁺ and δ⁻) of asymmetrical, polar molecules. HCl, H₂S
Hydrogen Bonding Strongest A very strong dipole interaction when Hydrogen is directly bonded to a highly electronegative atom (N, O, or F). H₂O, NH₃

IMF Diagrams

London Dispersion (LDF)

Ex: Cl₂ molecules

A temporary, random shift of electrons creates a fleeting δ⁻/δ⁺ that induces the same in a nearby molecule. Present in all molecules.

Dipole-Dipole

Ex: HCl molecules

The permanent δ⁻ end of one polar molecule lines up and attracts the δ⁺ end of the next. Requires a permanently polar molecule.

Hydrogen Bonding

Ex: H₂O molecules

The tiny, bare H atom bonded to O creates an intense δ⁺. A lone pair on a neighbouring O is powerfully attracted to it — much stronger than regular dipole-dipole.

Step 6: How IMFs Impact Physical Properties

The strength of Intermolecular Forces directly controls the physical properties of a substance, especially its phase transitions (melting and boiling) and vapor pressure.

1. Melting Point (Direct Relationship)

In a solid, molecules are locked into a rigid crystal lattice by their IMFs. To melt the solid into a liquid, you must add thermal energy to overcome these attractions. The stronger the IMFs, the higher the melting point.

Melting Point vs. Dominant IMF

Notice how water's strong hydrogen bonds keep it solid (ice) at much higher temperatures than nonpolar methane.

2. Boiling Point (Direct Relationship)

To boil a liquid into a gas, you must add enough heat to completely pull the molecules apart from one another. Just like melting, stronger IMFs result in a higher boiling point.

Boiling Point vs. Dominant IMF

3. Vapor Pressure (Inverse Relationship)

Vapor Pressure is the pressure exerted by a gas that has evaporated from a liquid in a closed container. It measures how easily a substance evaporates (its volatility).

The Rule: IMFs and Vapor Pressure have an inverse relationship. Molecules with weak IMFs don't stick together well, allowing them to escape easily into the gas phase (HIGH vapor pressure). Molecules with strong IMFs hold onto each other tightly, preventing evaporation (LOW vapor pressure).

Vapor Pressure vs. Dominant IMF (at 25°C)

Notice how water, with its strong hydrogen bonds, has a very low vapor pressure compared to the weaker dipole interactions of acetone.
