Covalent Bonding & IMFs

A Step-by-Step Interactive Tutorial

Step 1: Electronegativity & Bond Types

A covalent bond forms when two nonmetals share electrons. However, atoms don't always share equally. We use Electronegativity (EN)—how strongly an atom pulls on electrons—to predict the exact bond type.

  • Nonpolar Covalent (EN diff 0.0 - 0.4): Electrons are shared equally.
  • Polar Covalent (EN diff 0.5 - 1.9): Electrons are shared unequally, creating partial charges (δ+ and δ-).
  • Ionic (EN diff ≥ 2.0): An electron is completely transferred.

Step 2: Molecule Shape & VSEPR Theory

To understand a molecule's properties, you must know its 3D shape. VSEPR Theory (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion) states that electron regions (bonds and lone pairs) around a central atom will repel each other to be as far apart as possible.

Step 3: Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

While covalent bonds hold atoms together inside a molecule, IMFs are the weaker forces of attraction between separate molecules. There are three main types:

IMF Type Strength What causes it? Example
London Dispersion (LDF) Weakest Temporary shifting of electrons. Exists in ALL molecules, but is the only force in nonpolar molecules. CH4, Cl2
Dipole-Dipole Medium Attraction between the permanent partial charges (δ+ and δ-) of polar molecules. HCl, H2S
Hydrogen Bonding Strongest A very strong dipole interaction when Hydrogen is directly bonded to a highly electronegative atom (N, O, or F). H2O, NH3

Step 4: How IMFs Impact Physical Properties

To melt or boil a substance, you must add enough heat energy to pull the molecules apart from one another. The stronger the IMFs, the more energy is needed, resulting in a higher boiling point.

Conversely, molecules with weak IMFs don't stick together well, allowing them to easily escape as a gas (high vapor pressure).
