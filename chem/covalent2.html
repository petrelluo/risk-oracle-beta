Single-element dot diagrams: why dots go where they go

The PowerPoint shows the dot diagrams for Period 2 elements — but does not explain why the dots are placed where they are, or how the placement predicts how many bonds the atom will form.

The rule behind the dots

Electron dot diagrams show only the valence electrons (outer-shell electrons). The placement follows two rules that come from how electrons actually fill orbitals:

★ The pairing rule (Hund's rule, simplified)

1. Spread out first. Place one dot on each of the four sides of the symbol (top, right, bottom, left) — one at a time — before pairing any up.

2. Pair up only when forced. Once all four sides have a single dot, additional electrons must double up.

Why? Electrons repel one another. Putting them on different sides keeps them apart, which is lower in energy and more stable.

The big idea: unpaired dots → bonds

▶ The connection

The number of unpaired (single) dots tells you how many covalent bonds the atom will form.

Each bond requires one electron from each atom. So an atom with one unpaired dot can share once (one bond), one with three unpaired dots can share three times (three bonds), and so on.

Worked example: nitrogen

Nitrogen has 5 valence electrons. Place them one at a time:

  • Dots 1, 2, 3, 4 → one on each side (top, right, bottom, left)
  • Dot 5 → must pair up. By convention, pair the top.

Result: nitrogen has 1 lone pair (the doubled top) and 3 unpaired electrons (right, bottom, left). Therefore nitrogen forms 3 bonds — exactly what we see in NH₃ and N₂.

Worked example: oxygen

Oxygen has 6 valence electrons. Place them:

  • Dots 1–4 → one on each side
  • Dot 5 pairs the top, dot 6 pairs the bottom (or right — convention varies)

Result: oxygen has 2 lone pairs and 2 unpaired electrons → forms 2 bonds (as in H₂O).

Memory trick

Bonds = 8 − valence electrons (for nonmetals in groups 14–17 only).

C: 8 − 4 = 4 bonds · N: 8 − 5 = 3 bonds · O: 8 − 6 = 2 bonds · F: 8 − 7 = 1 bond. This is just the octet rule re-stated: each atom needs to share enough electrons to reach 8 total.

A common student mistake

⚠ Watch out

Students often pair up dots too early — for example, drawing carbon as :C: with two pairs and zero unpaired electrons. This would predict carbon forms 0 bonds, which is wrong. Always spread one dot to each side first; only pair up after all four sides are occupied.

Gap 2 of 4

Why "burns easily" is a covalent property

Flammability is one of the classic properties of covalent (molecular) compounds — but this is rarely explained well. Here's the reasoning.

The general rule

★ Property summary

Many covalent compounds burn easily; ionic compounds generally do not burn.

Think about it: gasoline (covalent), candle wax (covalent), rubbing alcohol (covalent), natural gas (covalent), wood (mostly covalent) — all flammable. Salt, baking soda, chalk (all ionic) — not flammable.

Three reasons why

1. Covalent compounds usually contain C and H

Most everyday covalent compounds are organic — built from carbon and hydrogen (hydrocarbons, alcohols, fats, sugars). Combustion is the reaction of C and H with oxygen:

CₓHᵧ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy

The C–H and C–C bonds release a lot of energy when they break and reform as the much stronger C=O and O–H bonds. Ionic compounds don't have C–H bonds, so this reaction can't happen.

2. Covalent compounds vaporize easily

Burning is a gas-phase reaction: the fuel must be in vapor form to mix with O₂ and ignite. Because covalent compounds have weak intermolecular forces, they have low boiling points and evaporate readily — so they can reach the gas phase at moderate temperatures. Ionic compounds need thousands of degrees just to melt, let alone vaporize.

3. Ionic compounds are already "oxidized" / stable

Ionic compounds like NaCl have already formed very stable electrostatic networks. There's no chemical "fuel" left in them to react with O₂. Many ionic compounds (especially oxides like Na₂O, MgO, CaO) are essentially already the products of burning — they cannot burn again.

Important nuances

⚠ Not every covalent compound burns

Water (H₂O) is covalent but does not burn — it is a product of combustion, not a fuel.

CO₂ is covalent and is actually used to extinguish fires.

Diamond & quartz (SiO₂) are network covalent solids — extremely resistant to burning at normal temperatures.

The pattern: covalent compounds rich in C–H or C–C bonds tend to burn well; covalent compounds that are already heavily oxidized do not.

Test-ready takeaway

If a question asks "which property is characteristic of covalent compounds?" and "burns easily / flammable" is an option — that's a covalent property. If asked about NaCl or CaCl₂ — those don't burn.

Gap 3 of 4

Using IMF to predict boiling points

The real test of IMF mastery: given two formulas, can you predict which has the higher boiling point and explain why?

The 4-step method

★ Steps to compare boiling points
  1. Draw the Lewis structure for each molecule.
  2. Determine the molecular polarity (polar or nonpolar) — check shape and bond dipoles.
  3. Identify the strongest IMF present in each:
    • Hydrogen bonding — H bonded directly to N, O, or F
    • Dipole–dipole — polar molecule, no H–N/O/F
    • London dispersion — nonpolar molecule (or as a baseline in all molecules)
  4. Compare: stronger IMF → higher boiling point. If both have the same type of IMF, the larger / heavier molecule wins (more electrons → stronger London forces).

Worked comparison: H₂O vs. H₂S

Both have the same shape (bent) and similar formulas. Why does water boil at 100 °C while H₂S boils at −60 °C?

H₂O — water
Polar, bent. H is bonded to O → forms hydrogen bonds.
H₂S — hydrogen sulfide
Polar, bent. H is bonded to S (not N/O/F) → only dipole–dipole.

Conclusion: water has hydrogen bonding, H₂S only has dipole–dipole. Hydrogen bonding is much stronger, so water needs much more energy to vaporize → much higher boiling point (100 °C vs −60 °C). The difference of 160 °C tells you how much stronger H-bonding is than ordinary dipole–dipole.

Worked comparison: CH₄ vs. CH₃OH

Both have one carbon. CH₄ boils at −161 °C; CH₃OH (methanol) boils at 65 °C — a 226 °C gap. Why?

CH₄ — methane
Symmetric, nonpolar. Only London dispersion.
CH₃OH — methanol
Polar; H bonded directly to O → hydrogen bonding.

Conclusion: Methanol can hydrogen-bond; methane cannot even be polar. Methanol's IMF are dramatically stronger, so its boiling point is far higher.

Worked comparison: F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂

All four are nonpolar diatomic molecules. They have only London dispersion forces. Yet their boiling points span over 350 °C. Why?

Conclusion: larger molecules have more electrons. More electrons → stronger temporary dipoles → stronger London dispersion → higher BP. This is why the heaviest halogen (I₂) is a solid while the lightest (F₂) is a gas.

Gap 4 of 4

Are lone pairs part of the chemical bond?

Short answer: No. Lone pairs are not part of the bond, but they matter — and here's exactly what they do.

The clear definition

★ Bond vs. lone pair

A covalent bond is a pair of electrons shared between two atoms. Both atoms "claim" both electrons.

A lone pair is a pair of electrons that belong to one atom only — they are not shared. They sit on a single atom and "stay home."

So lone pairs are not chemical bonds. They are unbonded valence electrons.

Visualizing the difference in H₂O

In water, O has 4 valence electrons used in bonds (2 bonds × 2 electrons) and 4 valence electrons in lone pairs (2 lone pairs × 2 electrons). Total: 6 valence electrons — exactly what oxygen brings to the table.

Why lone pairs still matter

Even though lone pairs aren't bonds, they have huge effects on the molecule:

1. They take up space (VSEPR)

Lone pairs repel other electron groups, just like bonds do. They count as "electron regions" in VSEPR. This is why H₂O is bent (not linear) and NH₃ is pyramidal (not flat). The lone pairs are pushing the bonds away.

2. They make hydrogen bonds possible

A hydrogen bond is the attraction between an H atom (bonded to N/O/F on one molecule) and a lone pair on N/O/F on a neighboring molecule. Without lone pairs, no hydrogen bonding — water would not exist as we know it.

3. They affect polarity

Lone pairs concentrate negative charge on one side of the molecule, contributing to molecular polarity. Compare CH₄ (no lone pairs on C, nonpolar) to NH₃ (one lone pair on N, polar) and H₂O (two lone pairs on O, very polar).

▶ Useful summary

Lone pairs are not bonds — they are owned by one atom only.

But they are crucial for: shape (VSEPR), hydrogen bonding (they're the "acceptor"), and molecular polarity.

Answer Key

Detailed answers and explanations for all 20 exercises

Exercise 01
B — Place one dot on each side first, then pair up
This is Hund's rule (simplified). Electrons repel one another, so they occupy separate sides before pairing up. Pairing too early leads to wrong bond predictions (see Exercise 5).
Exercise 02
N has 5 v.e. → 1 lone pair (top), 3 unpaired electrons (right, bottom, left) → forms 3 bonds.
Place dots one at a time: dots 1–4 fill all four sides, dot 5 must pair up. Result: 1 pair + 3 unpaired = 3 unpaired dots → 3 bonds. This matches the structure of NH₃ and N₂.
Exercise 03
O has 6 v.e. → 2 lone pairs, 2 unpaired electrons → forms 2 bonds.
Dots 1–4 fill the four sides; dots 5 and 6 pair up two of them. Two unpaired dots remain, so oxygen forms 2 bonds. This matches H₂O, where each H bonds with one of those unpaired electrons.
Exercise 04
C: 4 v.e., 0 LP, 4 unpaired, 4 bonds · N: 5, 1, 3, 3 · O: 6, 2, 2, 2 · F: 7, 3, 1, 1
Pattern: as v.e. increases by 1, lone pairs increase by 1 and unpaired electrons (= bonds) decrease by 1. Total dots = bonds + 2(lone pairs) always equals valence electrons.
Exercise 05
The student paired electrons too early. The correct diagram has 4 unpaired dots (one on each side), so carbon forms 4 bonds.
Hund's rule says place one dot per side first. Carbon has 4 v.e., which exactly fills the four sides with no pairing. All 4 are unpaired → 4 bonds, which is why carbon is tetravalent (CH₄, CO₂, all of organic chemistry).
Exercise 06
C — Burns easily / flammable
A and B describe ionic properties; D is also ionic (low melting points are typical for covalent). Flammability is characteristic of most covalent compounds, especially those containing C and H.
Exercise 07
C — H₂O (water)
Water is a covalent compound but it is the product of combustion, not a fuel — it does not burn. CH₄, ethanol, and octane are all classic flammable hydrocarbons.
Exercise 08
Ionic compounds like NaCl have very strong electrostatic bonds and are already stable; they have no C–H bonds to react with O₂. Methane has C–H bonds that release a lot of energy when they break and recombine with oxygen, and CH₄ vaporizes easily so it can mix with air and ignite.
Two key points: (1) the chemical fuel must be present (C–H or C–C bonds), and (2) the compound must be able to vaporize so it can mix with O₂. Ionic compounds fail on both counts.
Exercise 09
CaCl₂: ionic, not flammable · C₃H₈: covalent, flammable · KBr: ionic, not flammable · C₆H₆: covalent, flammable
Quick check: metal + nonmetal = ionic (likely not flammable); compounds with C and H only = covalent and flammable. Propane is a common fuel; benzene is flammable (be careful — it's also toxic).
Exercise 10
CO₂ is covalent, but the rule says most covalent compounds burn — not all. CO₂ is already fully oxidized (it is what carbon looks like after burning), so there is no chemical fuel left. The rule applies most reliably to compounds containing C–H or C–C bonds.
Flammability comes from oxidizable bonds (mainly C–H, C–C). When carbon is already bonded to two oxygens, it has nowhere to go — so CO₂ is the "ash" of combustion, not the fuel.
Exercise 11
Best pair: H₂O and H₂S. Both are covalent (so we are isolating IMF effects), both are bent and have similar size and shape (so molecular weight is not the explanation), but they differ in IMF type — water has hydrogen bonding while H₂S only has dipole–dipole. The dramatic 160 °C boiling-point difference (100 °C vs −60 °C) is therefore caused by IMF type alone, making this the cleanest comparison.
Why other pairs are worse: NaCl is ionic (the comparison would be confounded by bond-type difference, not just IMF). CH₄ and C₂H₆ both have only London dispersion forces (no IMF type difference). HF and any other listed item differ in too many ways at once. The H₂O/H₂S pair is the textbook example of an IMF-only comparison: same shape, same general size, only IMF type changes.
Exercise 12
Sample answer: CH₃OH vs CH₄. CH₃OH has hydrogen bonding (H bonded to O); CH₄ has only London dispersion forces (nonpolar). Therefore CH₃OH has the higher boiling point. Other valid pairs: HF (H-bonding) > HCl (dipole–dipole); HCl (dipole–dipole) > CH₄ (LDF); CH₃OH (H-bonding) > C₂H₆ (LDF), etc.
Any pair works as long as the IMF identification and ranking are correct: H-bonding > dipole–dipole > London dispersion. For molecules with the same IMF type, the larger/heavier wins.
Exercise 13
C — CH₃OH
CH₃OH is the only molecule with H bonded directly to O, so it can form hydrogen bonds — the strongest IMF. CH₃F is polar (dipole–dipole only); CH₄ and C₂H₆ are nonpolar (LDF only).
Exercise 14
F₂ (LDF, smallest) < Cl₂ (LDF, larger) < HCl (dipole–dipole) < HF (hydrogen bonding)
F₂ and Cl₂ are both nonpolar, so they only have LDF; Cl₂ wins because it is bigger (more electrons). HCl is polar with dipole–dipole. HF can H-bond (H to F!), giving the strongest IMF and highest BP. Actual values: F₂ = −188 °C, Cl₂ = −34 °C, HCl = −85 °C, HF = +20 °C.
Exercise 15
In water (H₂O), hydrogen is bonded directly to oxygen, so water molecules form hydrogen bonds with one another — a very strong IMF. In H₂S, hydrogen is bonded to sulfur (not N, O, or F), so only weaker dipole–dipole forces exist between molecules. Hydrogen bonds require much more energy to break, raising water's boiling point dramatically.
This is the textbook example showing how dramatic hydrogen bonding can be — a 160 °C difference between two structurally similar molecules.
Exercise 16

More electrons means stronger temporary dipoles, which means stronger LDF. The much stronger LDF in I₂ gives it a high enough boiling point to be solid at room temperature.
Larger nonpolar molecules always have stronger LDF — this is why the halogens go from gas (F₂, Cl₂) to liquid (Br₂) to solid (I₂) as you go down the group.
Exercise 17
B — A pair of electrons that belongs to only one atom and is not shared
Option A describes a covalent bond — the opposite of a lone pair. Lone pairs sit on a single atom and do not participate in bonding (though they affect shape, polarity, and H-bonding).
Exercise 18
False. A chemical bond requires electrons to be shared between two atoms; lone pairs belong to one atom only and are not shared.
This is a common conceptual confusion. Lone pairs are unbonded valence electrons. They affect molecular shape, polarity, and intermolecular interactions, but they are not bonds.
Exercise 19
NH₃: 3 N–H bonds, 1 lone pair on N, 8 total valence electrons used. (5 from N + 3 × 1 from H = 8 v.e.)
N forms 3 bonds (uses 6 electrons of which 3 are N's) and has 1 leftover lone pair (uses N's other 2 electrons). The 3 H atoms contribute 1 electron each to their bonds. Total = 8 v.e., distributed as 3 bonds + 1 lone pair.
Exercise 20
(1) Shape (VSEPR): lone pairs push bonds together, making H₂O bent and NH₃ pyramidal instead of linear/flat. (2) Hydrogen bonding: lone pairs on N, O, or F act as the "acceptor" for hydrogen bonds — without them, water could not hydrogen-bond. (Other valid answers: lone pairs contribute to molecular polarity; they can act as electron donors in chemical reactions.)
Any two of these effects are acceptable. The key insight: lone pairs are not bonds, but they shape molecular behavior in major ways.
Exercise 21
CH₃OH has hydrogen bonding (H bonded directly to O), while C₂H₆ has only London dispersion forces (nonpolar molecule). The two lone pairs on oxygen in methanol are crucial — they are the "acceptors" that allow neighboring methanol molecules to form hydrogen bonds, which is why methanol's boiling point is so much higher despite similar mass. Both compounds are covalent and contain C–H bonds, so both can burn in oxygen; ethane (C₂H₆) is actually slightly more flammable per mass because it has more C–H content per molecule and no oxygen pulling it toward the oxidized state.
This synthesis question pulls in all 4 gap topics: lone pairs (the O lone pairs enable H-bonding), IMF comparison (H-bond vs LDF), burning as a covalent property (both burn because they have C–H), and the underlying valence-electron logic (O's 6 v.e. give it 2 bonds + 2 lone pairs).
