📖 Key Terms
All vocabulary across teacher notes and assigned textbook chapters.
Solution
Homogeneous mixture of solute dissolved in solvent. Can be gas, liquid, or solid phase.
Solute
Substance being dissolved (smaller quantity). E.g., NaCl in saltwater.
Solvent
Substance doing the dissolving (larger quantity). Water is the "universal solvent."
Aqueous Solution
Solution in which water is the solvent.
Dissolution
Process by which solute dissolves into solvent to form a solution.
Dissociation
Ionic compound separating into its component ions when dissolved. Only ionic compounds dissociate — not molecular ones.
Hydrated
Ion or molecule surrounded by water molecules in solution.
Solubility
Amount of solute that can dissolve in 100g of solvent at a specific temperature to form a saturated solution. Units: g/100g water.
Unsaturated Solution
Contains less solute than the maximum possible; can dissolve more solute.
Saturated Solution
Contains the maximum amount of dissolved solute at a given temperature and pressure. Adding more solute → it settles undissolved.
Supersaturated Solution
Contains more solute than normally possible — unstable. Adding a seed crystal causes rapid crystallization of excess solute.
Solution Equilibrium
State where rate of dissolving = rate of recrystallization; total dissolved solute stays constant.
Recrystallization
Dissolved solute returning to solid state. Used in labs to purify compounds.
Seed Crystal
Small crystal added to a supersaturated solution to initiate crystallization.
Rate of Dissolution
How fast a solute dissolves. Factors: agitation, temperature, surface area.
Agitation
Stirring/mixing. Brings fresh solvent to solute → ↑ rate. Does NOT affect how much dissolves.
Surface Area
Smaller particles have more surface area → more contact with solvent → ↑ rate. Does NOT affect amount.
Concentration
Amount of solute dissolved in a given amount of solvent or solution. Can be expressed many ways.
Dilute vs. Concentrated
Dilute = small amount of solute; Concentrated = large amount. Qualitative terms.
Molarity (M)
Moles of solute per liter of SOLUTION. M = mol/L. Changes with temperature.
Stock Solution
Concentrated solution of accurately known concentration used to make diluted solutions.
Volumetric Flask
Pear-shaped flask calibrated for ONE precise volume; used to make accurately concentrated solutions.
Dilution
Adding solvent to decrease concentration. Moles of solute stay the same: McVc = MdVd.
Electrolyte
Compound that conducts electricity in aqueous solution or when melted — must produce mobile ions.
Nonelectrolyte
Compound that does NOT conduct electricity; dissolves without producing ions. E.g., sugar, ethanol.
Strong Electrolyte
Completely ionized in solution. Includes all ionic compounds, strong acids (HCl, HNO₃, H₂SO₄), strong bases (NaOH, KOH).
Weak Electrolyte
Only partially ionizes in solution. Conducts electricity weakly. E.g., acetic acid, NH₃. Uses ⇌ in equation.
Ionization
A molecular compound breaking into ions when dissolved in water. (Ionic compounds "dissociate"; acids "ionize.")
Miscible
Two liquids that mix completely with no phase separation (e.g., water + vinegar).
Immiscible
Two liquids that separate into phases (e.g., water + oil). Different IMFs.
Alloy
Solid-solid solution. Examples: brass (Cu+Zn), bronze (Cu+Sn), sterling silver (Ag+Cu).
Polar Molecule
Uneven charge distribution (e.g., H₂O, sucrose, NH₃). Dissolves in water.
Nonpolar Molecule
Even charge distribution (e.g., CO, hexane, oil). Does NOT dissolve in water.
Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)
Attractive forces between molecules. "Like dissolves like" = similar IMFs dissolve each other.
Coulombic Attraction
Attraction between opposite charges. Depends on charge magnitude and distance. Governs ionic solubility.
Polyatomic Ion
A charged group of bonded atoms (e.g., NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻, CO₃²⁻). Does NOT further dissociate when parent compound dissolves.
Henry's Law
Gas solubility ∝ partial pressure of gas above liquid. ↑ pressure → ↑ gas dissolves. Explains soda fizzing.
Solubility Curve
Graph of solubility (g/100g water) vs. temperature. Points on curve = saturated; below = unsaturated; above = supersaturated.
Blood as a Solution
Blood is simultaneously: a solution (of salts, glucose, urea), a suspension (of blood cells), and a colloid (of plasma proteins).

§16.2 — Solute-Solvent Combinations
📌
Solutions are not limited to solid-in-liquid. Any state can combine. The largest component is the solvent; everything else is the solute.
Solute StateSolvent StateExample
GasGasAir (N₂ = solvent ~78%; O₂, Ar, CO₂ = solutes)
GasLiquidO₂ dissolved in water; CO₂ in soda
LiquidLiquidEthanol in water; antifreeze (ethylene glycol + water)
SolidLiquidNaCl in water; sugar in tea
SolidSolidAlloys: brass (Cu+Zn), bronze (Cu+Sn), sterling silver (Ag+Cu)
LiquidSolidDental amalgam: mercury + silver/tin/copper powder (~50% Hg)
💡
Antifreeze rule: If a mixture is 60% ethylene glycol and 40% water → ethylene glycol is the solvent (larger quantity); water is the solute.
§16.3 — Rate of Dissolving

🌊 Agitation / Stirring

Keeps fresh solvent in contact with solute. Without stirring, solute's surface becomes saturated, slowing dissolution. Only affects rate — NOT total amount.

🌡️ Temperature

↑ temp → ↑ kinetic energy → more frequent and forceful collisions. Affects both rate AND amount (for solids, higher temp = more dissolves).

🔩 Surface Area

Crushing/grinding creates more contact points. Granulated sugar dissolves faster than a sugar cube. Only affects rate — NOT total amount.

⚠️
Key distinction: Stirring and grinding only speed up how fast dissolving happens — they do NOT change the maximum amount (solubility) that can dissolve. Temperature is the only factor here that affects both.
§16.4 — Saturated and Unsaturated Solutions

💧 What Happens at the Molecular Level

  • As NaCl dissolves, ions break off and enter solution (hydrated)
  • Some dissolved ions collide back with the crystal → recrystallize
  • Eventually dissolving rate = recrystallization rate → equilibrium
  • At equilibrium: solution is saturated

🔬 Recrystallization in Lab

  • Used to purify synthesized compounds
  • Dissolve impure compound in hot solvent → forms solution
  • Cool slowly → target compound precipitates (less soluble when cool)
  • Impurities stay dissolved in solution
🧪
How to tell if a solution is saturated: Add more solute. If it dissolves → unsaturated. If it doesn't → saturated. If there's undissolved solid at the bottom at equilibrium → saturated.
§16.5 — How Temperature Influences Solubility

🏔️ Solids in Water

  • Most: ↑ temperature → ↑ solubility (direct relationship)
  • NaCl: nearly flat curve — very little change with temperature
  • KNO₃: very steep curve — solubility rises dramatically
  • Reason: higher KE → solvent collides more with solid → IMFs weaken → more dissolves

🫧 Gases in Water

  • ↑ temperature → ↓ gas solubility (inverse relationship)
  • Reason: higher KE → gas molecules escape liquid phase more easily
  • Examples: HCl, NH₃, SO₂ all decrease in solubility as temp rises
  • ↑ pressure → ↑ gas solubility (Henry's Law — direct)
  • Soda: sealed under pressure → CO₂ stays dissolved; open → pressure drops → fizz
🔥
Nuclear power plant context (from textbook): Plants near rivers return warmer water to the river, which lowers dissolved oxygen in the water, affecting fish survival — an application of gas solubility and temperature.
📊
Reading solubility curves: At 30°C, NaNO₃ ≈ 95g/100g water; K₂Cr₂O₇ ≈ 16g; NaCl ≈ 35g. NaNO₃ is most soluble. Steeper slope = more soluble compound. If 80g KNO₃ is added to 100g water at 30°C and only ~48g dissolves → solution is saturated with 32g undissolved solid remaining.
§15.7 — Electrolytes and Nonelectrolytes

⚡ Electrolytes

  • Conduct electricity in aqueous solution or when molten
  • Require mobile ions to carry current
  • All ionic compounds are electrolytes — even insoluble ones (conduct in molten state)
  • Example: NaCl, CaCl₂, AgNO₃, KOH
  • Sports drinks contain electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺) to replace those lost in sweat

🚫 Nonelectrolytes

  • Do NOT conduct electricity in solution or molten state
  • Dissolve without producing ions
  • Example: sugar (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁), ethanol (C₂H₅OH), CO, glucose
  • Polar molecules that don't ionize in water are nonelectrolytes
§15.9 — Strong and Weak Electrolytes
TypeIonizationConductivityEquationExamples
Strong Electrolyte~100% ionizedHighSingle arrow → (complete)NaCl, KCl, HCl(aq), HNO₃, H₂SO₄, NaOH, KOH
Weak ElectrolytePartial ionizationLowDouble arrow ⇌ (equilibrium)Acetic acid (CH₃COOH), NH₃, HF, carbonic acid
NonelectrolyteNo ionizationNoneShows dissolution (no ions)Sugar, ethanol, CO, glucose
💡
HCl special case: HCl gas is a molecular compound — it's a nonelectrolyte in its pure form. But when dissolved in water, it completely ionizes: HCl(g) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq), making it a strong electrolyte called hydrochloric acid.
🔋
Car battery connection: Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) in car batteries is a strong electrolyte — completely ionizes to provide the ions needed to conduct electricity and power the vehicle.
§16.9 — Molarity
Molarity Formula
M = moles solute / liters solution
⚠️ Volume = total SOLUTION (not just water). Convert mL ÷ 1000 to get L. Read as "molar" e.g. "1.5 M NH₃" = "1.5 molar ammonia."

Example 1 — Find M

42.23g NH₄Cl (MM=53.50) in 500 mL solution.

mol = 42.23 ÷ 53.50 = 0.7893 mol

M = 0.7893 ÷ 0.5000 L = 1.579 M

Example 2 — Find grams

Make 3.00 L of 0.250 M KMnO₄ (MM=158.04)

mol = 0.250 × 3.00 = 0.750 mol

g = 0.750 × 158.04 = 118.5 g

🎯
Why use molarity? Percent solutions tell you mass, not particle count. Molarity lets you directly compare numbers of molecules/ions for stoichiometry — a 2% NaCl vs 2% CsCl have very different numbers of particles even though the mass percentages match.
§16.11 — Preparing Solutions
STEP 1Calculate moles → convert to grams
STEP 2Weigh out solid on balance
STEP 3Add to volumetric flask half-filled with distilled water
STEP 4Swirl until all dissolved
STEP 5Add water up to calibration line; cap & invert to mix
⚠️
Common mistake: You CANNOT add solid to 1L of water and get 1L of solution — the hydrated ions take up space, making final volume slightly >1L. Always use a volumetric flask calibrated for your target volume.
🧪
Volumetric flasks are ONE-SIZE tools — a 1-liter flask can ONLY be used to make 1 liter of solution, not 500 mL or any other volume.
§16.12 — Dilution
Dilution Equation ⭐ HIGH PRIORITY
Mc × Vc = Md × Vd
c = concentrate (stock), d = dilute (final). Moles of solute are CONSERVED — only volume changes. "Adding water doesn't change the number of solute particles."

Dilution Example 1

Make 1.0 L of 1.0 M HCl from 11.7 M stock:

V꜀ = (1.0 × 1.0) / 11.7 = 0.0855 L = 85.5 mL

Add acid to water (not reverse!)

Dilution Example 2

Make 500 mL of 1.0 M acetic acid from 17.6 M stock:

V꜀ = (1.0 × 0.500) / 17.6 = 0.0284 L = 28.4 mL

Pipette 28.4 mL into 500 mL volumetric flask; fill to line.

AgNO₃ — The Tricky Special Case ⭐

🔬 Why AgNO₃ is Tricky

  • AgNO₃ is ionic → it does dissociate when dissolved
  • Produces: Ag⁺ and NO₃⁻
  • Nitrate (NO₃⁻) is a polyatomic ion — a charged molecule
  • Therefore: NO₃⁻ does NOT further break apart
  • In solution you see: Ag⁺ (hydrated) + NO₃⁻ (hydrated, intact)

📌 Polyatomic Ion Rule

When an ionic compound containing a polyatomic ion dissolves, the polyatomic ion separates as a unit — it does not break into its individual atoms.

Other examples: Na₂SO₄ → 2Na⁺ + SO₄²⁻ (sulfate stays together)

K₂CO₃ → 2K⁺ + CO₃²⁻ (carbonate stays together)

Teacher Slide Key Ideas — Fill-in-the-Blank Answers

🔑 Dissolving Key Ideas

  • Most ionic compounds dissociate into separate ions when dissolved
  • Ionic substance dissolves when ion-water attraction is weaker than ion-ion (wait — attraction must be stronger for water to win) → ion-ion must be weaker than ion-water
  • Many polar molecules dissolve; water attracts to oppositely charged regions
  • Most nonpolar molecules do NOT dissolve
  • Polar molecules dissolve but do NOT dissociate; they become hydrated

🔑 Solubility Key Ideas

  • Rate depends on amount of collisions between solute and solvent
  • More collisions → faster rate of dissolving
  • How much dissolves depends on temperature and solute identity
  • Most solids dissolve more at higher temperatures; gases at lower temperatures (and higher pressures)
  • Solubility = IF something dissolves; collision theory = RATE
Saturation Key Ideas — From Slides

💧 Saturation Summary

  • Unsaturated = can dissolve more solute
  • Saturated = maximum solute at given temperature
  • Supersaturated = more than possible → very unstable state
  • Compounds with smaller ions and lower charges are more soluble
  • Coulombic attraction: lower charge + smaller distance → stronger pull → harder to dissolve

Vapor Pressure & Gas Solubility — From Slides (Don't Skip!)

🌡️ Vapor Pressure & IMFs

  • Higher intermolecular forces → fewer molecules escape as vapor → lower vapor pressure
  • Increasing temperature → more molecules have enough energy to escape liquid → vapor pressure increases
  • Gas solubility: lower temperature → gas molecules have lower KE → stronger attraction to solvent → more stays dissolved
  • Higher pressure: pushes more gas molecules into solution → ↑ solubility

Worked Examples (Teacher Slide Style)

Ex 1 — Find M

10.0g LiCl (MM=42.5 g/mol) in 100 mL solution.

mol = 10.0÷42.5 = 0.235 mol
V = 100mL = 0.100 L
M = 0.235÷0.100 = 2.35 M LiCl

Ex 2 — Find moles

How many moles Cu(NO₃)₂ for 250mL of 1.5M?

moles = M×V = 1.5 × 0.250 = 0.375 mol

Ex 3 — Find volume

What volume of 2.0M KF from 50.0g KF (MM=58.1)?

mol = 50.0÷58.1 = 0.861 mol
V = 0.861÷2.0 = 0.43 L

Ex 4 — Find grams

500mL of 0.5M Cu(NO₃)₂ (MM=187.6 g/mol)?

mol = 0.5 × 0.500 = 0.25 mol
g = 0.25 × 187.6 = 46.9 g

Ex 5 — Dilution

1.0L of 1.0M HCl from 11.7M stock?

11.7 × V꜀ = 1.0 × 1.0
V꜀ = 1.0÷11.7 = 0.0855 L = 85.5 mL

Ex 6 — Dilution

500mL of 1.0M acetic acid from 17.6M stock?

17.6 × V꜀ = 1.0 × 0.500
V꜀ = 0.500÷17.6 = 0.0284 L = 28.4 mL

Ex 7 — Make solution from solid

How to make 500mL of 1.2M NaOH (MM=40.0)?

mol = 1.2 × 0.500 = 0.60 mol
g = 0.60 × 40.0 = 24.0 g

Put 24g NaOH in 500mL volumetric flask; fill to line.

Ex 8 — Make from solid (two-step)

50mL of 0.25M Na₂CO₃ (MM=106 g/mol)?

mol = 0.25 × 0.050 = 0.0125 mol
g = 0.0125 × 106 = 1.33 g
