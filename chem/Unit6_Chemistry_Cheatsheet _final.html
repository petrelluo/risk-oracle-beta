2 Metallic Bond
Structure: Positive metal cations surrounded by a "sea" of freely moving (delocalized) valence electrons.
PropertyReason (Delocalized Electrons)
Conducts heat & electricity Mobile electrons carry charge and thermal energy freely through the metal
Malleable (hammered into shapes) Metal nuclei slide past each other; mobile electrons keep them bonded in new positions
Ductile (stretched into wire) Same electron sea holds atoms together during deformation
Shiny / Lustrous Free electrons absorb & re-emit light at all wavelengths
High MP & BP Strong attraction between cations and electron sea; much energy needed to break
Solid at room temp (except Hg) Strong metallic bonds hold structure rigid
Metallic Bond Strength varies with:
FactorEffect on Bond Strength
↑ Valence electrons contributedStronger bond (more e⁻ in the sea = stronger attraction)
↑ Nuclear charge of metal ionStronger bond (greater pull on the electron sea)
Measure: Metallic bond strength is indicated by the metal's boiling point — a higher boiling point means a stronger metallic bond.

3 Ionic Compound
PropertyReason (Crystal Lattice)
Hard solid at room temp Ions locked in rigid 3-D lattice; strong electrostatic attractions hold positions
Brittle Pressure shifts layers → like charges align → repel and shatter
High MP & BP Large amounts of energy needed to overcome strong ionic bonds in lattice
Conducts electricity when molten or dissolved in H₂O Ions become mobile (free to carry charge) when liquid or dissociated in water
Does NOT conduct as solid Ions fixed in lattice; no mobile charge carriers
Soluble in water (most) Polar water molecules attract and surround ions, pulling them free
Metal transfers e⁻ → cation (+); nonmetal receives → anion (−). Opposite charges attract → ionic bond in a 3-D crystal lattice.

9 Resonance

Resonance: When 2+ equivalent Lewis structures can be drawn, the actual bonding is a blend of all structures — electrons are delocalized over multiple bonds. No single structure is correct; proof is that all bond lengths and strengths are equal.

Example 1: Ozone (O₃)

O=O—O: ¨¨ ¨¨
⟺
:O—O=O ¨¨ ¨¨
→ actual: O–O bonds identical length & strength

Example 2: Carbonate Ion (CO₃²⁻)

Three equivalent structures: C=O on different O each time
Actual: all three C–O bonds are equivalent (bond order = 1⅓)
Electrons are delocalized over all three C–O bonds
Clue for ResonanceKey Evidence
Can draw 2+ valid Lewis structuresAll bond lengths are equal (not what a single Lewis structure would predict)
Molecule seems "stuck" between two structuresBond strengths are equal too

11 Effects on Boiling Point & Vapor Pressure

↑ IMF strength = ↑ BP & MP (direct); ↓ vapor pressure (inverse). Volatile = weak IMFs, easily vaporizes, high vapor pressure.

Property Stronger IMF → Weaker IMF →
Boiling Point ⬆ Higher BP ⬇ Lower BP
Melting Point ⬆ Higher MP ⬇ Lower MP
Vapor Pressure ⬇ Lower VP ⬆ Higher VP (volatile)

Worked Examples:

ComparisonHigher BP & Why
H₂O (100°C) vs. H₂S (−60°C) H₂O: H–O → hydrogen bonding. H₂S: H–S → dipole–dipole only (weaker).
H₂O (100°C) vs. isopropyl alcohol (82.5°C) H₂O: smaller, same H-bonding → stronger attraction.
F₂ (−188°C) vs. I₂ (+184°C) I₂: larger molecule → stronger London dispersion forces.
