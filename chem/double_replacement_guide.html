Unit Objectives

The Unit 8B goals, straight from your teacher's slides. These are the things you should be able to do by quiz day — not just recognize.

  • Describe how to read and write a chemical equation.
  • Identify if components in a reaction are elements or compounds; for compounds, identify whether they are ionically or covalently bonded.
  • Recognize the patterns of reactants and products to identify the 6 types of chemical reactions.
  • Explain how balanced chemical equations illustrate the conservation of matter.
  • Relate reactivity to predicting whether a single replacement reaction will occur.
  • Predict the formation of a precipitate in a double replacement reaction.
§ 01 · Chapter 11.1

Word Equations

A word equation is a chemical reaction described entirely in words. It names the reactants and products without any formulas. It's the first stage of translation from English sentence → chemical formula equation → balanced equation.

The structure

reactants  →  products

The arrow is read as "yields" or "produces." A plus sign between substances is read as "reacts with" (on the reactant side) or "and" (on the product side).

Example translations

Sentence: Hydrogen gas reacts with oxygen gas to form liquid water.
Word equation: hydrogen + oxygen → water
Sentence: Solid magnesium sulfide reacts with hydrochloric acid to form hydrogen sulfide gas and magnesium chloride solution.
Word equation: magnesium sulfide + hydrochloric acid → hydrogen sulfide + magnesium chloride
Study tip

Pay attention to state descriptors in the sentence — "solid," "aqueous solution," "gas bubbles." These tell you the phase labels (s), (aq), (g), (l) that you'll need later.

§ 02 · Chapter 11.2

Writing Chemical Equations

Once you have the word equation, the next step is to translate each name into a correct chemical formula. The result — before balancing — is called the skeleton equation.

Step-by-step translation

  1. Write the word equation.
  2. Replace each name with the correct chemical formula (use ionic charges to build correct ratios).
  3. Include physical state symbols in parentheses after each formula: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous (dissolved in water).
  4. Indicate reaction conditions above the arrow: Δ for heat, catalyst name (like Pt), or electricity.

Worked example (from slides)

Hydrogen gas reacts with oxygen gas when heated to produce liquid water.
Skeleton equation: H₂(g) + O₂(g) → H₂O(l) + Energy

More skeleton equation examples

MgS(s) + HCl(aq) → H₂S(g) + MgCl₂(aq)
N₂O₅(g) —[Pt, Δ]→ N₂(g) + O₂(g)
FeCl₃(aq) —[electricity]→ Cl₂(g) + Fe(s)
H₂S(aq) + NaOH(aq) → Na₂S(aq) + H₂O(l)
Phase label rules of thumb

Acids and bases are usually aqueous. Ionic compound solubility is determined using the solubility table. Gases escape from solution; precipitates drop out as solids. The reaction type is a strong hint about which phases to expect.

§ 03 · Chapter 11.3

Balancing Equations

A balanced chemical equation has the same number of atoms of each element on both sides of the arrow. This is a direct expression of the law of conservation of mass — atoms are neither created nor destroyed.

What you can & cannot change

  • You CAN change coefficients — the numbers in front of a formula. 2 H₂O means two molecules of water.
  • You CANNOT change subscripts — the small numbers inside a formula. Changing H₂O to H₂O₂ changes it from water into hydrogen peroxide, a completely different substance.

Balanced example

2 H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2 H₂O(l)
Left side: 2 × 2 = 4 H atoms  ·  1 × 2 = 2 O atoms
Right side: 2 × 2 = 4 H atoms  ·  2 × 1 = 2 O atoms ✓

A systematic approach

  1. Write the correct formulas and skeleton equation.
  2. Count atoms of each element on both sides.
  3. Balance elements that appear in only one reactant and one product first.
  4. Balance hydrogen and oxygen last (they appear in many compounds).
  5. Use the smallest whole-number ratio of coefficients. If fractions appear, multiply through.
  6. Double-check by recounting every element.
Common trap

Polyatomic ions (NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻, OH⁻, CO₃²⁻, PO₄³⁻) often travel together unchanged through a reaction. Balance them as a unit rather than as individual atoms — it's faster and less error-prone.

§ 04 · Chapter 11.4

Combination (Synthesis) Reactions

Two or more reactants combine to form a single product. The telltale sign: many reactants → one product.

A + B → AB

Common patterns

Binary compound formation (metal + nonmetal)

2 Al + 3 F₂ → 2 AlF₃
2 Cs + Cl₂ → 2 CsCl
4 Li + O₂ → 2 Li₂O
6 K + N₂ → 2 K₃N

Synthesis of a base (metal oxide + water)

Na₂O + H₂O → 2 NaOH
CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂

Synthesis of an acid (nonmetal oxide + water)

CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃
Prediction shortcut

If you see two reactants and only one product, it's a combination reaction. The product's formula is determined by the ionic charges of the reactants (for metal + nonmetal).

§ 05 · Chapter 11.5

Decomposition Reactions

A single compound breaks apart into two or more simpler substances. The reverse of a combination reaction. Almost always requires an energy input — heat (Δ) or electricity.

AB → A + B

Common patterns

Binary compound decomposition

MgCl₂ —[Δ]→ Mg + Cl₂
CO₂ → C + O₂
2 FeCl₃ —[Δ]→ 2 Fe + 3 Cl₂

Metal carbonates decompose into a metal oxide + CO₂

MCO₃ → MO + CO₂
CaCO₃ —[Δ]→ CaO + CO₂

Metal chlorates decompose into a metal chloride + O₂

MClO₃ → MCl + O₂
2 KClO₃ —[Δ]→ 2 KCl + 3 O₂
2 NaClO₃ —[Δ]→ 2 NaCl + 3 O₂

Classic fun one

2 H₂O₂ —[yeast catalyst]→ 2 H₂O + O₂ (the "elephant toothpaste" reaction)
Identifier

One reactant → two or more products. Look for Δ (heat) or "electricity" over the arrow.

§ 06 · Chapter 11.6

Combustion Reactions

A substance reacts rapidly with oxygen gas, releasing heat and light. When the fuel is a hydrocarbon (contains only C and H, sometimes with O), the products are always carbon dioxide and water.

hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

Worked examples

CH₄ + 2 O₂ → CO₂ + 2 H₂O (methane, natural gas)
2 C₂H₆ + 7 O₂ → 4 CO₂ + 6 H₂O (ethane)
C₃H₈ + 5 O₂ → 3 CO₂ + 4 H₂O (propane)
2 C₈H₁₈ + 25 O₂ → 16 CO₂ + 18 H₂O (octane, gasoline)

Balancing combustion — the sequence

  1. Balance carbon atoms first (set CO₂ coefficient equal to C in hydrocarbon).
  2. Balance hydrogen atoms next (set H₂O coefficient equal to H/2 in hydrocarbon).
  3. Balance oxygen atoms last (count O on product side, divide by 2 for the O₂ coefficient).
  4. If the O₂ coefficient is a fraction, multiply the whole equation by 2.
Incomplete combustion

When oxygen is limited, combustion can produce CO (carbon monoxide) or even pure C (soot) instead of CO₂. For quiz purposes, assume complete combustion unless stated otherwise.

§ 07 · Chapter 11.7

Single Replacement Reactions

One element replaces another element in a compound. The pattern: a lone element swaps with a similar element (metal with metal, halogen with halogen) in an aqueous compound.

A + BC → AC + B    (A replaces B)

Reactants: an element plus an aqueous compound. Rule: the reaction only occurs if the lone element is more reactive than the similar element it's trying to replace. Reactivity is determined using the Activity Series (next section).

Metal replacing metal

Zn(s) + CuCl₂(aq) → ZnCl₂(aq) + Cu(s) ✓ Zn > Cu in activity
Ag(s) + HBr(aq) → No reaction   (Ag is below H — less reactive)

Halogen replacing halogen

Cl₂(g) + 2 NaBr(aq) → 2 NaCl(aq) + Br₂(l) ✓ Cl > Br in activity
Cl₂(g) + 2 NaF(aq) → No reaction   (F > Cl — F is more reactive)
The rule in one sentence

The lone element must be more reactive than the element it replaces. Metals replace less reactive metals; halogens replace less reactive halogens. Otherwise: no reaction.

§ 08 · Chapter 11.8

Activity Series

The Activity Series is a ranking of elements by reactivity, used to predict whether a single replacement reaction will happen. An element can only replace an element below it on the list.

Metals

Mg · Al · Zn
Cr · Fe · Cd · Ni
Sn · Pb

Cu · Hg
Ag · Pt · Au
Most reactive ↑

Halogens

F₂
Cl₂
Br₂
I₂

How to use the series

  1. Identify the lone element (metal or halogen).
  2. Identify the similar element inside the aqueous compound.
  3. Locate both on the activity series.
  4. If the lone element is higher on the list → reaction occurs, element swap takes place.
  5. If the lone element is lower (or equal) → no reaction.

Why hydrogen appears on the metal series

Hydrogen is included because metals above it can replace hydrogen from acids (producing H₂ gas), while metals below it cannot.

Mg + 2 HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂(g)  ✓ Mg > H
Cu + HCl → No reaction  (Cu < H)

§ 09 · Chapter 11.9

Double Replacement Reactions

Also called double displacement or metathesis. Two aqueous ionic compounds exchange partners — the cation of one compound pairs with the anion of the other, and vice versa.

AB + CD → AD + CB

A and C are positively-charged cations. B and D are negatively-charged anions. They simply swap partners.

How does the strength of attraction between ions impact double replacement reactions?

The core concept

When two ionic solutions are mixed, the ions are already floating freely, each surrounded by water molecules (ion-dipole interactions). A reaction only occurs if the new bonds between swapped ions would be stronger than those ion-dipole attractions to water. If they're not, the ions just stay dissolved — nothing happens.

Key idea · from the slides

When a reaction between two aqueous compounds forms a precipitate, molecular liquid, or molecular gas, it's because the new ionic or covalent bonds are stronger than the original ion-dipole interactions in solution.

§ 10 · The Main Event

How to Predict a Double Replacement

A double replacement reaction will occur only if one of the products is a precipitate, water (or weak acid), or a gas. Otherwise, the ions stay dissolved and there is no reaction.

The three driving forces

1 · Precipitate (s)

CaBr₂(aq) + 2 AgNO₃(aq) →
Ca(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2 AgBr(s)

An insoluble ionic compound forms and drops out of solution. Use the solubility rules to check.

2 · Water or weak acid (l)

HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) →
NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)

Often a neutralization — an acid reacts with a base to form water.

3 · Gas (g)

Na₂S(aq) + 2 HCl(aq) →
2 NaCl(aq) + H₂S(g)

A molecular gas bubbles out of solution — H₂S, CO₂, HCN, NH₃, etc.

Step-by-step prediction procedure

  1. Identify the ions. Break each reactant into its cation and anion. Example: AgNO₃ → Ag⁺ + NO₃⁻; NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻.
  2. Swap partners. The cation of compound 1 pairs with the anion of compound 2, and vice versa. Use ionic charges to write correct formulas — don't just copy subscripts.
  3. Check each product. Is either product insoluble (precipitate)? Water or weak acid? A gas? If yes to any → reaction occurs. If no to all → no reaction.
  4. Write and balance the complete equation with phase labels.

Solubility rules (Table 11.3 from your slides)

Memorize this table. It's the difference between "reaction" and "no reaction" on precipitate problems.

Compound typeSolubility
Salts of alkali metals (Li⁺, Na⁺, K⁺…) and ammonium (NH₄⁺)Soluble — always
Nitrates (NO₃⁻) and chlorates (ClO₃⁻)Soluble — always
Sulfates (SO₄²⁻)Soluble, except with Pb²⁺, Ag⁺, Hg₂²⁺, Ba²⁺, Sr²⁺, Ca²⁺
Chlorides, bromides, iodides (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻)Soluble, except with Ag⁺, Pb²⁺, Hg₂²⁺

Memory mnemonic
  • "Always friendly" — Group 1 ions, ammonium, nitrates, chlorates: these dissolve with anyone.
  • "Always grumpy" — carbonates, phosphates, chromates, sulfides, hydroxides: these usually don't dissolve.
  • "It depends" — sulfates and halides: check the exception list.

Special cases to recognize

Acid + carbonate

Na₂CO₃ + 2 HCl →
2 NaCl + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l)

Produces CO₂ gas + water. Intermediate H₂CO₃ is unstable and decomposes.

Acid + sulfide

MgS + 2 HCl →
MgCl₂ + H₂S(g)

Produces H₂S gas (rotten-egg smell).

Acid + base

HCl + NaOH →
NaCl + H₂O(l)

Classic neutralization. Always produces water.

Ammonium + hydroxide

NH₄Cl + NaOH →
NaCl + NH₃(g) + H₂O(l)

Produces ammonia gas and water. NH₄OH is unstable.

The six reaction types at a glance

Synthesis

A + B → AB

Two or more → one product.

Decomposition

AB → A + B

One → two or more. Needs heat/electricity.

Combustion

CₓHᵧ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

Hydrocarbon burned in oxygen.

Single replacement

A + BC → AC + B

Predicted with Activity Series.

Double replacement

AB + CD → AD + CB

Predicted with solubility rules + water/gas check.

Acid-base (neutralization)

HX + MOH → MX + H₂O

A special type of double replacement.

§ 11 · Self-check

Practice Problems

Click any card to reveal the answer. Try predicting each reaction before peeking — does it occur? If yes, what drives it?

Double replacement predictions

Problem 01
KBr(aq) + AgNO₃(aq) → ?
Reaction ✓
Products: KNO₃(aq) + AgBr(s). The AgBr precipitates — silver halides are insoluble (Ag⁺ exception on the halides row).
Problem 02
HNO₃(aq) + KOH(aq) → ?
Reaction ✓
Products: KNO₃(aq) + H₂O(l). Neutralization — H⁺ and OH⁻ combine to form water.
Problem 03
NaNO₃(aq) + KCl(aq) → ?
No reaction ✗
All four possible products (Na⁺, K⁺, NO₃⁻, Cl⁻ pairings) are soluble. No precipitate, no gas, no water. Ions stay dissolved.
Problem 04
Na₂CO₃(aq) + 2 HCl(aq) → ?
Reaction ✓
Products: 2 NaCl(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l). Acid + carbonate — always produces CO₂ gas plus water.
Problem 05
Pb(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2 KI(aq) → ?
Reaction ✓
Products: 2 KNO₃(aq) + PbI₂(s). Brilliant yellow precipitate — Pb²⁺ with halides is insoluble.
Problem 06
(NH₄)₂SO₄(aq) + Ba(NO₃)₂(aq) → ?
Reaction ✓
Products: 2 NH₄NO₃(aq) + BaSO₄(s). BaSO₄ precipitates — Ba²⁺ is on the sulfate exception list.

Single replacement predictions

Problem 07
Zn(s) + CuCl₂(aq) → ?
Reaction ✓
Products: ZnCl₂(aq) + Cu(s). Zn is above Cu on the activity series — Zn replaces Cu.
Problem 08
Ag(s) + HBr(aq) → ?
No reaction ✗
Ag is below H on the activity series — Ag cannot replace H.
Problem 09
Cl₂(g) + 2 NaBr(aq) → ?
Reaction ✓
Products: 2 NaCl(aq) + Br₂(l). Cl is above Br on the halogen activity series.
Problem 10
Cl₂(g) + 2 NaF(aq) → ?
No reaction ✗
F is above Cl — fluorine is more reactive than chlorine, so Cl cannot replace F.

Reaction type identification

Problem 11
2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O
Combination (synthesis) — two reactants combine into one product. Also qualifies as combustion of H₂.
Problem 12
2 KClO₃ → 2 KCl + 3 O₂
Decomposition — metal chlorate breaking into a metal chloride and O₂ gas.
Problem 13
C₃H₈ + 5 O₂ → 3 CO₂ + 4 H₂O
Combustion — hydrocarbon + O₂ producing CO₂ and H₂O.
Problem 14
Mg + 2 HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂
Single replacement — Mg replaces H from the acid. Mg is above H on the activity series.
Problem 15
AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃
Double replacement — two ionic compounds exchange partners. AgCl is the precipitate.
Problem 16
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
Decomposition — metal carbonate breaking into metal oxide + CO₂. Requires heat.
