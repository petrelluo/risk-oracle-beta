Electronegativity & Predicting Bond Type

What Is Electronegativity?

Electronegativity (EN) is the ability of an atom to attract shared electrons toward itself in a bond. It is a periodic trend — not directly measurable, but scaled relative to fluorine.

Periodic Trend
  • Across a period (left → right): EN increases (more protons pull harder)
  • Down a group (top → bottom): EN decreases (electrons farther from nucleus)
  • Highest EN: Fluorine (F) = 4.0  |  Lowest EN: Cesium / Francium ≈ 0.7–0.8
  • Noble gases typically not assigned EN values

Using Electronegativity Difference (ΔEN) to Predict Bond Type

Calculate: ΔEN = |ENatom 1 – ENatom 2|

ΔEN RangeBond TypeDescription
0 to <0.4Nonpolar CovalentElectrons shared equally
0.4 – 2.0Polar CovalentElectrons shared unequally
≥ 2.0IonicElectron effectively transferred
Important Caveat Different textbooks may use slightly different cutoff values — the boundary between polar covalent and ionic is a "gray area." The ONLY way to know bond type for certain is to examine the physical properties of the substance.

Overview of Bond Types

🔴 Ionic Bond

Electron transfer from metal → nonmetal

  • Metal loses e⁻ → forms cation (+)
  • Nonmetal gains e⁻ → forms anion (−)
  • Opposite charges attract → ionic bond
  • ΔEN ≥ 2.0

🔵 Covalent Bond

Electron sharing between two nonmetals

  • Polar covalent: unequal sharing (ΔEN 0.4–2.0)
  • Nonpolar covalent: equal sharing (ΔEN < 0.4)
  • Can form single, double, or triple bonds

🟣 Metallic Bond

Delocalized electrons among metal cations

  • "Sea of electrons" surrounding metal cations
  • Electrons mobile throughout entire structure
  • Between metal + metal (or metal + nonmetal in alloys)

🟢 Two Ways to Predict Bond Type

  1. Element types: Metal + Nonmetal = usually ionic; Nonmetal + Nonmetal = usually covalent; Metal + Metal = metallic
  2. ΔEN: Use the cutoff table above

⚠ Only physical properties confirm bond type with certainty!

Ionic Bonds & Ionic Compounds (CK-12 8.6, 8.9)

Formation of Ionic Compounds

When a metal transfers one or more electrons to a nonmetal:

  • Metals have low ionization energies → easily lose electrons → form cations
  • Nonmetals have high electronegativity → easily gain electrons → form anions
  • Both achieve a full octet (noble-gas configuration) → lower potential energy → more stable
Crystal Lattice Structure

Cations and anions self-assemble into a 3-D repeating structure called a crystal lattice. Each ion is surrounded by ions of opposite charge. The formula unit is the lowest whole-number ratio of ions (e.g., NaCl = 1:1 ratio). Ionic compounds do NOT form discrete "molecules."

Physical Properties of Ionic Compounds

PropertyDescription & Explanation
Physical state at RTCrystalline solid (strong ionic attractions hold lattice together)
HardnessHard but brittle — layers of ions shatter when shifted (like charges repel when layers slip)
Melting & Boiling PointsHigh — large amounts of energy needed to overcome strong ionic bonds
Electrical conductivityPoor conductor as solid (ions fixed in lattice); conducts when molten or dissolved in water (ions free to move)
Solubility in waterGenerally soluble (some to varying degrees)
ChemThink Simulation Observations When Na loses an e⁻ to Cl: Na shrinks (lost outer shell electron) and Cl grows (gained extra electron). The resulting Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions spontaneously self-assemble into a repeating crystal lattice. The ratio of ions in the lattice equals the chemical formula.

The Octet Rule (CK-12 8.2)

Most atoms bond to achieve 8 electrons in their valence shell (like a noble gas). Atoms bond not to follow rules — but to become more stable and lower in energy.

Exceptions to the Octet Rule
  • H: needs only 2 electrons (duet rule)
  • Be: often forms only 4 electrons (e.g., BeH₂ — linear)
  • B & Al: often form only 6 electrons (e.g., BCl₃ — trigonal planar)
  • Period 3+ nonmetals (P, S, Cl, etc.): can expand octet (e.g., PF₅ has 10 e⁻, SF₆ has 12 e⁻)

Drawing Lewis Structures — Step-by-Step

The 5 Steps
  1. Count valence electrons: Add all valence e⁻ from all atoms. For anions, add extra e⁻ (CO₃²⁻ adds 2). For cations, subtract.
  2. Draw skeleton: Place the least electronegative atom in the center. C is always central. H is never central. Halogens are always terminal (outer).
  3. Place one bond (2 e⁻) between each bonded pair of atoms.
  4. Complete octets on outer atoms first (except H — max 2 e⁻).
  5. Check remaining electrons: If valence e⁻ are used up but central atom lacks an octet → convert lone pairs to double or triple bonds. If electrons remain unused → place on central atom as lone pairs.
Bonding Tips (from teacher's notes) C always forms 4 bonds. H always forms 1 bond. Halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) almost always form 1 bond and never form multiple bonds. O prefers 2 bonds. N prefers 3 bonds (both O and N are flexible).

Bond lengths and strengths are equal across all resonance bonds — the Lewis model cannot perfectly describe them. Models are not perfect; they are attempts to model reality.

Covalent Bonds (CK-12 9.2)

Covalent Bond Basics

A covalent bond forms when two atoms share electrons. This sharing allows both atoms to achieve a full valence shell at lower potential energy (more stable). Covalent bonds form primarily between nonmetals.

Why Covalent? (Simulation Key Ideas)
  • Each atom is attracted to the electrons of the other atom's nucleus
  • Rather than fully transferring, atoms "fight over" the electrons — covalent bonding is shared custody
  • The bond forms at the point of lowest potential energy (maximum stability)
  • Nonmetals have high EN → good at attracting electrons → share rather than lose them
Bond TypeElectron Pairs SharedExampleRelative Strength

Double Bond ( = )2 pairs (4 e⁻)O=O, C=OMedium
Triple Bond ( ≡ )3 pairs (6 e⁻)N≡N, H–C≡NStrongest / Shortest

VSEPR Theory & Molecular Geometry

VSEPR — Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion

Electron regions (bonding pairs AND lone pairs) repel each other and arrange to be as far apart as possible. This determines the molecule's 3-D shape.

Electron regions = number of bond areas + number of lone pairs on central atom

Electron RegionsLone PairsMolecular GeometryBond AngleExample
20Linear180°CO₂, BeCl₂, HCN
30Trigonal Planar120°BF₃, BCl₃, CH₂O
31Bent~117°SO₂
40Tetrahedral109.5°CH₄, CCl₄, CBr₄, CF₄
41Trigonal Pyramidal~107°NH₃
42Bent~104.5°H₂O, SF₂
50Trigonal Bipyramidal90° / 120°PF₅, PCl₅
60Octahedral90°SF₆
Effect of Lone Pairs Lone pairs take up more space than bonding pairs, compressing bond angles. That is why NH₃ (1 lone pair) has a smaller angle than CH₄ (no lone pairs), and H₂O (2 lone pairs) has an even smaller angle than NH₃.
Key Examples from Teacher's Notes
  • NH₃: 4 electron regions (3 bonds + 1 lone pair) → trigonal pyramidal (~107°) → POLAR
  • H₂O: 4 electron regions (2 bonds + 2 lone pairs) → bent (~104.5°) → POLAR
  • BeCl₂: 2 electron regions (2 bonds, 0 lone pairs) → linear (180°) → NONPOLAR (octet exception)
  • BCl₃: 3 electron regions (3 bonds, 0 lone pairs) → trigonal planar (120°) → NONPOLAR (octet exception)

Molecular Polarity

Polar vs. Nonpolar Molecules

A polar molecule has an uneven distribution of electrons → net dipole moment. A nonpolar molecule has even distribution → dipoles cancel to zero.

Steps to Determine Molecular Polarity
  1. Draw the Lewis structure
  2. Determine molecular geometry (VSEPR)
  3. Identify polar bonds (atoms with different EN — the more electronegative atom attracts electrons more)
  4. Draw dipole arrows pointing toward the more electronegative atom
  5. If molecule is symmetrical AND all surrounding atoms are identical → dipoles cancel → NONPOLAR
  6. If arrows point to one side (asymmetric structure or different atoms) → net dipole → POLAR
Important Distinction A molecule can have polar bonds but still be nonpolar overall if the dipoles cancel out due to symmetry. Example: CO₂ has polar C=O bonds, but because it is linear and symmetric, the two dipoles cancel → nonpolar molecule.

Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

Three Types of Intermolecular Forces

IMFs are attractions between molecules (not within). They are generally much weaker than ionic or covalent bonds. IMFs determine physical state, boiling point, melting point, and vapor pressure of molecular compounds.

Van der Waals Forces = collective name for London Dispersion + Dipole-Dipole forces (named after Dutch chemist Johannes van der Waals, 1837–1923). Hydrogen bonding is a special, stronger case separate from van der Waals.
TypeStrengthWhich Molecules?Key Detail
London Dispersion Forces (LDF)WeakestALL molecules (nonpolar & polar)Temporary dipoles caused by moving electrons. Strength ↑ with more electrons (larger molecules)
Dipole-Dipole (DD)MediumPolar moleculesAttraction between permanent dipoles on neighboring polar molecules. Methane (CH₄) vs octane (C₈H₁₈): both nonpolar, but octane has far more electrons → much stronger LDF
Hydrogen Bonding (HB)Strongest IMFMolecules with H bonded directly to F, O, or NSpecial, much stronger type of dipole-dipole. H is very small → very polarized. Examples: H₂O, HF, NH₃
Strength Order (weakest → strongest): LDF < Dipole-Dipole < Hydrogen Bonding <<< Covalent Bond < Ionic Bond ≈ Metallic Bond
Classifying IMFs — Practice
  • CH₄ → nonpolar → LDF only
  • I₂ → nonpolar but large/many electrons → strong LDF (solid at RT!)
  • HCl → polar, H bonded to Cl (not F/O/N) → Dipole-Dipole
  • CH₂Cl₂ → polar → Dipole-Dipole
  • H₂O → polar, H bonded to O → Hydrogen Bonding
  • NH₃ → polar, H bonded to N → Hydrogen Bonding
  • HF → polar, H bonded to F → Hydrogen Bonding
  • CO₂ → nonpolar (linear, symmetric) → LDF only

IMFs & Physical Properties (CK-12 9.2)

How IMF Strength Affects Physical Properties

Boiling & Melting Points

↑ IMF strength → more energy needed to pull molecules apart → higher BP and MP

↑ IMF = direct (positive) relationship with BP/MP

Vapor Pressure

↑ IMF strength → fewer molecules can escape as vapor → lower vapor pressure

↑ IMF = inverse (indirect) relationship with vapor pressure

Volatility A substance that easily vaporizes at normal temperatures is called volatile. Volatile substances have weak IMFs and high vapor pressures. Example: rubbing alcohol feels cold on skin because it evaporates quickly.
Classic Comparisons (must know for quiz)
ComparisonExplanation
H₂O (BP 100°C) vs H₂S (BP −60°C)H₂O: H bonded to O → hydrogen bonding. H₂S: H bonded to S (not F/O/N) → only dipole-dipole. HB >> DD → water has much higher BP
H₂O (100°C) vs isopropyl alcohol (82.5°C)Both have HB, but water has a more extensive H-bonding network. Water has stronger net IMF → higher BP
F₂ (−188°C) < Cl₂ (−34°C) < Br₂ (59°C) < I₂ (184°C)All nonpolar → LDF only. Larger molecules → more electrons → stronger LDF → higher BP. F₂ is most volatile (weakest LDF)
CH₄ vs C₈H₁₈ (octane)Both nonpolar → LDF only. Octane has far more electrons → much stronger LDF → much higher BP

Physical Properties of Molecular Compounds

PropertyMolecular Compounds
State at room temperatureSolid, liquid, or gas (depends on IMF strength)
Melting & boiling pointsRelatively low (IMFs much weaker than ionic bonds)
Electrical conductivityPoor (no free ions or electrons)
SolubilityPolar molecules dissolve in polar solvents (water); nonpolar in nonpolar solvents ("like dissolves like")

Metallic Bonding (CK-12 8.10)

The "Sea of Electrons" Model

In metallic bonding, the valence electrons are delocalized — they move freely among all metal cations. The coulombic attraction between positive metal cations and the mobile electron sea forms the metallic bond.

All Metallic Properties Come from Mobile Electrons
PropertyWhy?
Electrical conductivityMobile electrons carry electrical charge through the metal
Heat conductivitySame mobile electrons transfer kinetic energy (heat)
Malleability (hammered into shapes)Metal cations slide past each other while held by the electron sea — no fixed directional bonds to break
Ductility (stretched into wire)Same reason as malleability
Luster (shininess)Mobile electrons absorb and re-emit photons of light
High melting & boiling pointsStrong metallic bonds require much energy to break
Metallic Bond Strength Bond strength varies with: (1) number of valence electrons and (2) nuclear charge. A practical measure of metallic bond strength is the boiling point of the metal.

Metal Alloys (CK-12 8.12)

What Is an Alloy?

An alloy is a solution (mixture) formed from a metal and another substance (metal or nonmetal). Alloys are homogeneous — atoms intermix throughout. Formation of an alloy changes the physical properties of both metals involved.

Beneficial Properties of Alloys (vs. pure metals)
  • Often lower melting points than either component metal
  • Higher strength than pure metals
  • Greater durability
  • Better corrosion resistance
AlloyComponentsNotable Properties / Uses
BrassCopper + ZincMusical instruments, plumbing fixtures
BronzeCopper + TinStatues, coins, historical tools
SteelIron + Carbon (+ others)Construction, tools — much stronger than iron
Sterling SilverSilver + CopperJewelry — harder and more durable than pure silver
18K GoldGold + Copper/Silver/otherJewelry — more durable than pure (24K) gold

Overall Bond Strength Order (strongest → weakest)

Ionic ≈ Metallic > Covalent (triple > double > single) >> Hydrogen Bonding > Dipole-Dipole > London Dispersion Forces

Intramolecular vs. Intermolecular
  • Intramolecular bonds (within a molecule): ionic bonds, covalent bonds, metallic bonds — these are strong
  • Intermolecular forces (between molecules): LDF, dipole-dipole, hydrogen bonding — these are much weaker
  • This is why molecular compounds have lower BP/MP than ionic or metallic solids — you only need to overcome the weaker IMFs to boil them, not break actual bonds

38
BCl₃: B has 3 bonds, 0 lone pairs → 3 electron regions → trigonal planar. NH₃: N has 3 bonds + 1 lone pair → 4 electron regions → pyramidal. The lone pair is the key difference.
39
BCl₃: 6e⁻ on B (exception), trigonal planar, nonpolar. BeH₂: 4e⁻ on Be (exception), linear, nonpolar. PF₅: 10e⁻ on P (expanded, period 3), trigonal bipyramidal, nonpolar.
40
All are nonpolar → LDF only. Larger molecules have more electrons → stronger LDF → higher BP. F₂ has fewest electrons → weakest LDF → lowest BP → most volatile.
41
Both have HB, but water (H₂O, small molecule) has a more extensive HB network. Isopropyl alcohol has a large nonpolar carbon chain that dilutes HB effect. ∴ Water has stronger net IMF → higher BP.
42
(a) BP: ↑IMF → ↑BP (direct). (b) Vapor pressure: ↑IMF → ↓VP (indirect/inverse). (c) Volatility: ↑IMF → less volatile; volatile substances have weak IMF.
43
Similar: both involve polar molecules with partial charges; both are attractions between δ+ and δ−. Differ: HB requires H bonded specifically to F/O/N and is stronger than regular DD. Example DD: HCl, CH₂O. Example HB: H₂O, NH₃, HF.
44
Both nonpolar → LDF only. Octane has 8 carbons and 18 hydrogens = many more electrons than methane (1C, 4H). More electrons = larger temporary dipoles = stronger LDF = much higher BP (octane BP ~126°C vs methane BP −161°C).
45
Intramolecular = within a molecule (covalent bonds, ionic bonds). Intermolecular = between molecules (LDF, DD, HB). Intramolecular bonds are much stronger; IMFs are weaker, which is why molecular compounds have lower BP than ionic or metallic solids.
