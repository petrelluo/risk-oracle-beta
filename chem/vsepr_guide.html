1 What is VSEPR?

VSEPR = Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion. The core idea:

Electron pairs (both bonding and lone pairs) around a central atom repel each other and arrange as far apart as possible in 3D space.

This arrangement determines the shape of the molecule. Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs because they are closer to the nucleus and occupy more space — so lone pairs compress bond angles.

2 How to Determine Shape (Step-by-Step)

1

Draw the Lewis dot structure.

2

Count electron regions around the central atom: each single/double/triple bond = 1 region; each lone pair = 1 region.

3

Find electron geometry — the 3D arrangement of ALL electron regions.

4

Find molecular shape — same as electron geometry but lone pairs are invisible; only atom positions count.

5

Estimate bond angles — lone pairs compress angles below the ideal.

4 Polarity

Bond polarity: electronegativity difference between bonded atoms creates polar bonds (δ+ and δ−).

Δ EN < 0.5 → nonpolar bond  |  Δ EN 0.5–1.7 → polar covalent  |  Δ EN > 1.7 → ionic

Molecular polarity: polar bonds + asymmetric shape = polar molecule. Polar bonds + symmetric shape = dipoles cancel = nonpolar.

✓ Nonpolar

No polar bonds (H₂, N₂), OR polar bonds but symmetric shape cancels them (CO₂ linear, CCl₄ tetrahedral, BF₃ trigonal planar).

⚡ Polar

Polar bonds AND asymmetric shape — net dipole exists (H₂O, NH₃, HCl, CHCl₃). Also any diatomic with two different atoms.

5 Intermolecular Forces (IMF)

IMFs act between molecules and control boiling point, melting point, solubility, and viscosity. Stronger IMF = higher boiling point.

London Dispersion (LDF)

All molecules. Temporary dipoles in electron clouds. Stronger with more electrons (larger molecules). Explains why Xe boils higher than He.

Dipole-Dipole

Polar molecules only. Permanent dipoles align (+end attracts −end). Stronger than LDF alone for similar-sized molecules.

Hydrogen Bonding

H bonded directly to N, O, or F only. The bare H proton is strongly attracted to lone pairs on adjacent molecules. Strongest type; explains water's anomalous properties.

1. London Dispersion Forces (LDF) — In Every Molecule

Even in perfectly nonpolar molecules like He or H₂, electrons are in constant motion. At any instant, electrons may be unevenly distributed, creating a temporary (instantaneous) dipole. This induces a dipole in a neighboring molecule, and the two temporary dipoles attract each other.

What affects LDF strength?

Polarizability — how easily the electron cloud can be distorted. More electrons = larger, "squishier" electron cloud = stronger LDF. This is why:

  • Noble gas boiling points increase from He → Ne → Ar → Kr → Xe
  • Cl₂ is a gas but Br₂ is a liquid and I₂ is a solid (at room temperature)
  • Longer carbon chains have higher boiling points
Noble Gas Boiling Points — LDF increases with electron count

Each step down the group adds 8+ electrons → stronger LDF → higher boiling point.

2. Dipole-Dipole Forces — Polar Molecules Align

In polar molecules, the permanent partial charges (δ+ and δ−) create an electric dipole. When polar molecules are near each other, they orient so that δ+ regions face δ− regions — this is dipole-dipole attraction.

Dipole-dipole forces are stronger than LDF for molecules of similar size. This explains why polar molecules (like HCl, SO₂) have higher boiling points than nonpolar molecules of similar molecular weight.

HCl vs Ar — Same Molecular Weight (~40), Different IMF

3. Hydrogen Bonding — Nature's Strongest Molecular Force

Hydrogen bonding occurs when H is bonded directly to N, O, or F — the three most electronegative elements. The H–N/O/F bond is so polar that the H becomes almost a bare proton, strongly attracted to lone pairs on adjacent molecules.

H-bond requires: H bonded to N, O, or F — AND — a lone pair on a neighboring N, O, or F to accept it.

The H₂O Anomaly — Why Water is Extraordinary

Compare the boiling points of Group 16 hydrides. Without H-bonding, you'd expect H₂O to boil around −80°C (extrapolating the H₂S → H₂Se → H₂Te trend). Instead it boils at +100°C — 180°C higher than expected!

Group 16 Hydride Boiling Points — H₂O Anomaly from H-bonding

H₂S, H₂Se, H₂Te follow the normal LDF trend. H₂O is a dramatic outlier due to its exceptionally strong hydrogen bonding network.

The Hydrogen Bond Network in Liquid Water

Each water molecule can form up to 4 hydrogen bonds — 2 as donor (via its 2 O–H bonds) and 2 as acceptor (via its 2 lone pairs on O). This creates an extensive 3D network that gives water many unique properties.

4. Properties Explained by IMF

💧 Surface Tension

Water molecules at the surface are only pulled inward and sideways (not outward into air) — creating an unbalanced net inward force. This pulls the surface taut, like a stretched membrane. Water has one of the highest surface tensions of any liquid, enabling water striders to walk on it and water to form spherical droplets.

Cause: Strong H-bonding network. Removing a molecule from the surface costs energy.

🌡️ High Boiling Point

Water boils at 100°C — much higher than similar-sized molecules (H₂S boils at −60°C). To boil, you must overcome the IMF holding molecules together. The strong H-bonding network requires a lot of energy to disrupt, keeping water liquid at room temperature.

Cause: Each water molecule can form up to 4 H-bonds simultaneously.

🧊 Ice Floats (Density Anomaly)

Liquid water is denser than solid ice — ice floats. Most solids are denser than their liquid. In ice, water molecules lock into a rigid hexagonal lattice with fixed H-bonds, creating more empty space than liquid water (where H-bonds constantly break and reform). This protects aquatic life in winter.

Cause: H-bond network in ice is more open/ordered than in liquid water.

🌿 Capillary Action

Water can climb up narrow tubes (like plant stems) against gravity. Two forces: adhesion (water H-bonding to polar surface of glass/cellulose) and cohesion (water H-bonding to itself). Adhesion pulls water up the walls; cohesion pulls the rest of the water up with it. Essential for water transport in plants.

Cause: Both H-bonding to surface AND between water molecules.

🌊 High Specific Heat

Water absorbs a lot of heat before its temperature rises (4.18 J/g·°C vs 0.9 for aluminum). Heating water requires first breaking/weakening H-bonds before kinetic energy increases. This buffers temperature changes — why oceans moderate climate, why the body uses sweat to cool down efficiently.

Cause: Energy goes into breaking H-bonds, not raising temperature.

🔗 Viscosity

Viscosity = resistance to flow. Stronger IMF between molecules means they don't slide past each other easily. Glycerol (many O–H groups → many H-bonds) is much more viscous than water (2 H-bonds per molecule). Oil is viscous due to strong LDF from large, flexible hydrocarbon chains.

Cause: Molecules must break IMF to flow past each other.

Notice: CCl₄ has only LDF yet boils at +77°C — much higher than CH₄ (also LDF-only) because CCl₄ has 4× more electrons → much larger, more polarizable electron cloud → much stronger LDF.
