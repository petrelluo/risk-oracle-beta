Concentration & Dilution

The concentration of a solution measures the amount of solute dissolved in a given quantity of solvent.

M = mol / Lmolarity
M₁V₁ = M₂V₂dilution formula
C = m / Vgeneral conc.

Dilution Procedure — Example: 85.5 mL stock → 1.0 L

1
Fill a 1.0 L volumetric flask ¼ with distilled water first — adding water before acid prevents dangerous exothermic splashing.
2
Dispense 85.5 mL of the concentrated stock solution from a buret into the flask.
3
Add distilled water up to the 1.0 L fill line; stopper and invert to mix thoroughly.
Solubility Rules (higher rule takes priority)
✔ Soluble
Na⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺All salts — no exceptions
NO₃⁻, ClO₃⁻, ClO₄⁻, CH₃CO₂⁻All salts — no exceptions
Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻EXCEPT Ag⁺, Hg₂²⁺, Pb²⁺
SO₄²⁻EXCEPT Ca²⁺, Ba²⁺, Pb²⁺, Ag⁺, Sr²⁺
F⁻EXCEPT Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺, Sr²⁺, Ba²⁺, Pb²⁺
✖ Insoluble
CO₃²⁻, PO₄³⁻, C₂O₄²⁻, CrO₄²⁻, S²⁻All salts
OH⁻, O²⁻Most metal salts
Common insoluble examples:  CaSO₄  ·  CaCO₃  ·  AgCl  ·  K₂SO₄
Electrolytes in Aqueous Solution

⚡ Strong Electrolytes

100% dissociation — all dissolved units become free ions
7 Strong Acids (memorize)
HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃
H₂SO₄, HClO₄, HClO₃
Strong Bases
Group 1 hydroxides (NaOH, KOH…)
+ Ba(OH)₂, Sr(OH)₂
Soluble Ionic Salts
NaCl, KNO₃, CuSO₄, NH₄Cl…

〰 Weak Electrolytes

Partial dissociation — equilibrium between molecular & ionic forms
Weak Acids
CH₃COOH (acetic), HF
HNO₂, H₂CO₃, H₃PO₄, HCN…
Weak Bases
NH₃ (ammonia)
amines (CH₃NH₂…)
Slightly Soluble Salts
PbSO₄, CaF₂
(low ion concentrations)

○ Non-Electrolytes

No ionization at all — dissolve as intact molecules, zero conductivity
Examples
Sucrose, glucose, ethanol
glycerol, acetone, urea
— most organic molecules
Note
These often dissolve well in water (polar) — they just don't ionize.
⚠ Classic confusion — dissolves ≠ electrolyte: Sugar dissolves beautifully in water but is a non-electrolyte (produces no ions). Conversely, AgCl barely dissolves — but every unit that does dissolves dissociates 100% into Ag⁺ and Cl⁻. Solubility and degree of ionization are completely independent.
Making a Solution from a Solid — 4 Steps
1
Calculate moles needed → convert to grams using molar mass
2
Weigh out the correct mass of solid on a balance
3
Add solid to a volumetric flask half-filled with distilled water; swirl until fully dissolved
4
Add distilled water up to the calibration mark on the flask neck; cap and invert to mix
