The Core Question

Why can you bend a copper wire with your fingers — but a salt crystal snaps when you drop it?

It all comes down to what happens at the atomic level when force is applied.

The type of bonding between atoms/ions determines whether a material deforms gracefully or fractures violently.

🔨

Gold leaf · Aluminium foil · Copper sheets

Tendency to fracture suddenly without significant deformation when stress is applied. No warning — just snap.

METALLIC BONDING

Structure of a Metal

Positive ion cores locked in a lattice — surrounded by a mobile "sea" of free electrons

Positive Ion Cores

Metal atoms release their outer electrons, becoming positive ions (cations). These ions arrange themselves in a regular, repeating 3D lattice — rows and layers stacked neatly like cannonballs.

The Sea of Electrons

The released electrons are delocalised — they don't belong to any one atom. They move freely throughout the entire structure, forming an "electron sea" that holds all the positive ions together.

🔑 Key Insight: The metallic bond is the electrostatic attraction between the positive ion lattice and the surrounding sea of free electrons. Neither is "in charge" — it's a communal, non-directional bond.

Why is the sea of electrons so special?

🔄

Non-directional

Unlike covalent bonds (fixed angle) or ionic bonds (fixed + & − pattern), the metallic bond has no preferred direction. Electrons flow to wherever they're needed.

💧

Fluid redistribution

When layers move, the electron sea instantly redistributes around the new positions. No bonds need to break and reform — the bond is maintained throughout.

⚡

Electrical conductivity

The same free electrons that enable deformation also carry electrical current — which is why all metals are good electrical conductors.

MECHANISM

Why Metals Are Malleable & Ductile

Step by step: what happens when you hammer a metal or pull it into a wire

🔨 Malleability

Ability to be hammered into sheets. Force is applied from above — layers spread outward. Gold can be beaten to 0.1 µm thick (100× thinner than a human hair).

🧵 Ductility

Ability to be stretched into wires. Tension is applied in one direction — layers slide and elongate. 1g of gold can be drawn into 2.4 km of wire.

Step-by-step mechanism: Layers Sliding

1

Initial state — regular lattice

Metal ions sit in neat rows. The electron sea flows uniformly around and between all ions, holding them in place with equal force in all directions. The lattice is in equilibrium.

2

Force applied — stress on the lattice

A hammer blow or tension applies stress. This force acts along a slip plane — a layer of ions is pushed to slide relative to the layer below it.

3

Layers slide — the electron sea flows with them

The top layer shifts. Crucially, the free electrons instantly redistribute around the ions in their new positions. The ions never face each other directly — electrons are always between them, maintaining attraction.

4

New stable configuration — NO fracture

The ions settle into a new equilibrium. The lattice structure is preserved — just shifted. The metal is permanently deformed but completely intact. Metallic bonds were never broken, just temporarily rearranged.

IONIC BONDING

Structure of an Ionic Crystal

Alternating positive and negative ions locked in a rigid 3D lattice by electrostatic forces

Cations (Positive)

Metal atoms completely transfer electrons to non-metal atoms, forming positive ions (e.g. Na⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺). These have fixed positions in the lattice.

Anions (Negative)

Non-metal atoms gain electrons to become negative ions (e.g. Cl⁻, O²⁻, F⁻). They alternate with cations throughout the crystal.

🔑 Key: Every ion is surrounded by ions of opposite charge. Na⁺ is surrounded by 6 Cl⁻, each Cl⁻ by 6 Na⁺. The pattern must stay alternating — disrupting it brings like charges together, which is catastrophic.

Why is the ionic lattice so rigid?

⚡

Strong electrostatic forces

Coulomb's law: force ∝ q₁×q₂/r². Opposite charges attract powerfully. In NaCl, each Na⁺ is attracted to 6 Cl⁻ simultaneously — from all six sides.

🎯

Directional constraint

Unlike metallic bonds, ionic bonds require a specific spatial arrangement. Opposite charges must alternate — the pattern cannot be violated without triggering catastrophic repulsion.

🚫

No free electrons

Electrons are fully transferred — none are free to move. There is no "sea" to redistribute when ions shift. This absence is what makes deformation impossible.

MECHANISM

Why Ionic Crystals Are Brittle

The same strong forces that hold the lattice together instantly tear it apart when disrupted

The Fatal Shift

In an ionic crystal, the rigid alternating pattern is everything. When a force causes layers to shift by even one ion spacing, ions of the same charge end up next to each other. Like charges violently repel — this repulsion is as strong as the original attraction was — and the crystal shatters along that plane.

Step-by-step mechanism: The Fracture

1

Initial state — perfectly alternating lattice

Every + ion is surrounded by − ions and vice versa. All electrostatic forces are balanced — attraction in every direction. The crystal is in stable, low-energy equilibrium.

2

Force applied — stress builds at a slip plane

An impact or applied stress concentrates at a crystallographic plane. The upper half of the lattice begins to shift relative to the lower half along this slip plane.

3

⚠️ Critical shift — like charges align!

After shifting just one ion width, the pattern flips: Na⁺ now sits next to Na⁺, and Cl⁻ next to Cl⁻. The electrostatic repulsion between these identical ions is enormous — the same Coulomb force that used to attract now repels.

4

💥 Fracture — instantaneous and complete

The repulsive forces shoot the two halves apart. The crystal fractures cleanly along the slip plane — a flat, crystallographic cleavage face. No deformation, no warning. The crystal has a characteristic cleavage angle matching its crystal structure.

The ONE key difference that explains everything

METAL — Free electrons present

Electron sea = shock absorber

When layers shift under force, the electron sea instantly flows to surround the ions in their new positions. No bonds break. No repulsion develops. The metal simply takes a new shape. This is why it can be hammered flat, bent, or drawn into wires.

IONIC — No free electrons

No buffer = instant catastrophe

When layers shift, there is nothing to smooth the transition. Like charges collide. The repulsion is as powerful as the original attraction — but now acting outward. The lattice tears itself apart along the cleavage plane.
