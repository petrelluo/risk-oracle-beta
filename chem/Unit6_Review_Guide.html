6.1

Types of Chemical Bonds

Goal: predict bond type from element type & electronegativity

Why do atoms bond at all?

When atoms bond, their valence shells become completely (or more nearly) filled with electrons. This makes the resulting substance more stable and lower in energy. Stability is the driving force behind all chemical bonding.

The four types of bonding

There are four main types of chemical bonds covered in this unit:

  • Ionic bonding — electrons are transferred from a metal to a nonmetal, forming oppositely charged ions held together by electrostatic attraction.
  • Nonpolar covalent bonding — electrons are shared equally between two atoms (identical or very similar electronegativity).
  • Polar covalent bonding — electrons are shared unequally; the more electronegative atom pulls the electrons closer to itself.
  • Metallic bonding — metal atoms release their valence electrons into a shared "sea," forming a lattice of cations in a mobile electron cloud.
Vocabulary
Chemical bondA force of attraction between atoms or ions that holds them together.
Ionic bondingBonding between a metal that transfers an electron to a nonmetal, forming ions.
Covalent bondingBonding between two nonmetals that share electrons.
Polar covalent bondA covalent bond where electrons are shared unequally due to electronegativity differences.
Nonpolar covalent bondA covalent bond where electrons are shared equally.

Electronegativity trend review (CK-12 Ch. 6.21)

Electronegativity measures how strongly an atom attracts electrons toward itself in a bond. The trend on the periodic table is:

  • Increases left → right across a period (more protons, stronger pull on electrons)
  • Decreases top → bottom down a group (electrons farther from nucleus, weaker pull)
  • Fluorine (F) is the most electronegative element of all — always the highest.

In a polar covalent bond, the more electronegative atom gets a partial negative charge (δ−) and the less electronegative atom gets a partial positive charge (δ+). This charge separation creates a dipole.

Bond strength hierarchy

In terms of strength, intramolecular (within-molecule) bonds rank as:

Ionic > Metallic > Covalent

Stronger bonds mean higher melting and boiling points.

6.2

Formation of Ionic Compounds

Goal: describe how an ionic compound forms

How ionic bonds form

An ionic bond forms when a metal atom transfers one or more valence electrons to a nonmetal atom. This transfer completes the octet of both atoms, making them more stable.

  • The metal atom loses electrons → becomes a positively charged cation (and shrinks in size).
  • The nonmetal atom gains electrons → becomes a negatively charged anion (and grows in size).
  • The opposite charges attract strongly: this electrostatic attraction is the ionic bond.
Classic Example: NaCl
Sodium (Na, a metal) has 1 valence electron. Chlorine (Cl, a nonmetal) needs 1 electron to complete its octet. Na transfers its 1 valence electron to Cl. Na becomes Na⁺; Cl becomes Cl⁻. These oppositely charged ions attract each other, forming an ionic bond.

Crystal lattice and formula unit (CK-12 Ch. 8.6)

Ionic compounds do not form individual molecules. Instead, billions of cations and anions self-assemble into a highly ordered, three-dimensional repeating structure called a crystal lattice. In this structure, each cation is surrounded by anions and each anion is surrounded by cations — maximizing attraction and minimizing repulsion.

The formula unit is the lowest whole-number ratio of ions in the ionic compound. For NaCl, the formula unit is 1 Na⁺ : 1 Cl⁻. For MgCl₂, the formula unit is 1 Mg²⁺ : 2 Cl⁻.

Vocabulary
Ionic compoundA hard, brittle solid formed from electron transfer; conducts when molten or dissolved in water.
Crystal latticeThe 3-dimensional repeating structure of cations and anions held together by ionic bonds.
Formula unitThe lowest whole-number ratio of ions in an ionic compound (NOT a discrete molecule).
CationA positively charged ion (metal lost electrons); smaller than the original atom.
AnionA negatively charged ion (nonmetal gained electrons); larger than the original atom.
6.3

Properties of Ionic Compounds

Goal: relate properties to the crystal lattice structure

All physical properties of ionic compounds trace back to the crystal lattice — the strong, ordered arrangement of positive and negative ions throughout the solid.

PropertyExplanation (WHY)
Hard solid at room temperature Ions are locked in rigid fixed positions in the crystal lattice.
Brittle (shatters when struck) When pressure is applied, the ionic layers slide so that like charges (+ and +) align. These like charges repel strongly, causing the crystal to crack and shatter.
High melting and boiling points The strong electrostatic attractions between ions throughout the entire lattice require a large amount of energy to overcome.
Conducts electricity when molten or dissolved in water In solid form, ions are locked in place and cannot move — no charge carriers. When melted or dissolved, ions become mobile and can carry electric current.
Does NOT conduct electricity as a solid Ions are not free to move in the solid state.
Soluble in water (often) Water molecules (which are polar) surround individual ions and pull them away from the lattice ("like dissolves like").
Special Case: Acids
Acids are molecular compounds (not ionic), yet many conduct electricity when dissolved in water — because they release H⁺ ions when dissolved. Acids are their own category; don't confuse them with ionic compounds.
Key Idea
If you're told a substance is a solid at room temperature but does not conduct electricity when dissolved in water, it is not ionic — it's likely covalent/molecular. Physical properties are the only reliable way to classify bond type.
9.2

Covalent Bonds

Goal: describe covalent bonding and its properties

What is a covalent bond? (CK-12 Ch. 9.2)

A covalent bond forms when two nonmetal atoms share electrons to complete their octets. Unlike ionic bonds (where electrons are fully transferred), both atoms in a covalent bond "own" a share of the bonding electrons.

  • A single bond = 2 shared electrons (1 pair)
  • A double bond = 4 shared electrons (2 pairs)
  • A triple bond = 6 shared electrons (3 pairs)

Double and triple bonds are shorter and stronger than single bonds. A triple bond is the strongest and shortest covalent bond.

Properties of covalent (molecular) compounds

Because covalent compounds have weaker intermolecular forces (between molecules) compared to ionic or metallic bonds:

  • They can be solid, liquid, or gas at room temperature.
  • They have relatively low melting and boiling points.
  • They are poor electrical conductors (no ions or free electrons).
  • Polar molecular compounds tend to be soluble in water; nonpolar ones are not ("like dissolves like").
6.5

Electron Dot Diagrams & Lewis Structures

Goal: draw Lewis structures for molecular compounds

What are electron dot diagrams? (CK-12 Ch. 8.1)

An electron dot diagram (Lewis dot structure) shows the valence electrons of an atom as dots placed around the element's symbol. The number of dots equals the number of valence electrons (group number for main group elements).

In a Lewis structure for a molecule, bonds are shown as lines (one line = one pair of shared electrons) and lone pairs (non-bonding electrons) are shown as pairs of dots.

Rules for drawing Lewis structures

  1. Count total valence electrons. Add up all valence electrons from every atom in the molecule. For ions, add one electron per negative charge or subtract one per positive charge.
  2. Draw the skeleton. Carbon (C) is always the central atom when present. Hydrogen (H) is never the central atom. Otherwise, the least electronegative atom is central.
  3. Place one bond between each atom and the central atom. Each bond uses 2 valence electrons.
  4. Complete the octets on all outer (terminal) atoms by placing lone pairs. (H only needs 2 electrons — the duet rule.)
  5. Check remaining valence electrons.
    • If you run out of electrons before all atoms have an octet, form double or triple bonds by moving lone pairs from outer atoms to bonding regions.
    • If unused electrons remain, place them on the central atom as lone pairs.

Bonding preferences to know

  • C — typically forms 4 bonds (no lone pairs on C when fully bonded)
  • N — typically forms 3 bonds + 1 lone pair
  • O — typically forms 2 bonds + 2 lone pairs
  • F, Cl, Br, I (halogens) — typically form 1 bond + 3 lone pairs
  • H — always forms exactly 1 bond (needs only 2 electrons total)

The Octet Rule (CK-12 Ch. 8.2)

The octet rule states that atoms tend to bond until they have 8 electrons in their valence shell (like a noble gas). This drives both ionic and covalent bonding. However, there are important exceptions:

ExceptionElement(s)Electron countExample
Duet ruleH2 electrons (not 8)H₂, HCl, H₂O
Incomplete octetBe4 electronsBeH₂ (2 bonds, no lone pairs)
Incomplete octetB, Al6 electronsBCl₃, AlBr₃ (3 bonds, no lone pairs)
Expanded octetP, S, and any nonmetal in Period 3 or beyond10 or 12 electronsPCl₅ (10e), SF₆ (12e)
Key Idea
Atoms don't actually "follow rules" — they bond to achieve stability (lower energy). The octet rule is a useful guideline, not an absolute law. Atoms in Period 3 and beyond have access to d orbitals, which is why they can hold more than 8 electrons around the central atom.

Resonance (CK-12 Ch. 8.2)

Sometimes more than one valid Lewis structure can be drawn for the same molecule, with double bonds in different positions. This is called resonance. The actual structure is a hybrid — a blend of all resonance structures — and cannot be accurately represented by a single Lewis structure. Electrons are delocalized (spread out) across multiple bonds.

How to recognize resonance: if you can draw two or more equally valid Lewis structures that differ only in where a double bond (or lone pair) is placed, the molecule has resonance.

  • Ozone (O₃): Two resonance structures exist (O=O–O ↔ O–O=O). The actual molecule has both O–O bonds equal in length — neither is a pure single or double bond.
  • Carbonate ion (CO₃²⁻): Three resonance structures exist. All three C–O bonds are equal in length (~1.33 bond order).

Proof of resonance: If bond lengths and strengths are equal for bonds that look different in any one Lewis structure, the molecule has resonance.

6.4

Metallic Bonding & Metal Alloys

Goal: explain metallic properties using the sea of electrons model

What is metallic bonding? (CK-12 Ch. 8.10)

In metals, the valence electrons are delocalized — they are not bound to any one atom but are free to move throughout the entire metal sample. This creates a model where:

  • Positive metal cations sit in fixed, ordered positions.
  • A "sea" of mobile valence electrons surrounds them on all sides.
  • The bond is the coulombic attraction between the positively charged cations and the negatively charged electron sea.
Vocabulary
Metallic bondingBonding formed by the coulombic attraction between delocalized valence electrons and positive metal cations.
MalleabilityAbility to be hammered or bent into shapes without breaking or cracking.
DuctilityAbility to be stretched into a wire without breaking or cracking.
AlloyA solution (mixture) formed from a metal and one or more other substances (metals or nonmetals).

Properties of metals — explained by the sea of electrons

PropertyExplanation using delocalized electrons
Conducts electricity (solid and liquid)Delocalized electrons are mobile and carry electrical charge through the metal in any direction.
Conducts heatMobile electrons rapidly transfer kinetic energy throughout the metal.
MalleableWhen pressure is applied, metal cation layers slide past one another. The electron sea reforms the metallic bond in the new arrangement, so the metal bends instead of shattering (unlike ionic solids).
DuctileSame reason as malleability — the electron sea maintains the bond as layers shift.
Lustrous (shiny)The mobile electrons at the surface reflect light.
High melting & boiling points (generally)The strong attraction between the cations and the electron sea throughout the entire structure requires a large amount of energy to break.
Not soluble in waterWater cannot disrupt the metallic lattice because the metallic bonds are not electrostatic attractions between discrete ions.

Metallic bond strength

Not all metallic bonds are equally strong. Bond strength varies with two factors:

  • Number of valence electrons contributed: More electrons in the sea → stronger attraction to the cations → stronger bond.
  • Nuclear charge of the metal cation: Higher nuclear charge → greater pull on the electron sea → stronger bond.

How to measure metallic bond strength: Look at the boiling point of the metal. A higher boiling point means more energy is required to separate the atoms, which means a stronger metallic bond.

Alloys (CK-12 Ch. 8.12)

An alloy is a mixture (solid solution) of a metal with one or more other elements (usually other metals, but sometimes nonmetals). Alloys are made because they typically have better properties than the pure metals they are made from.

Benefits of alloys over pure metals:

  • Higher strength and hardness
  • Greater durability
  • Better corrosion resistance
  • Lower melting point (useful for solder)

Two types of alloys

  • Substitutional alloy: Atoms of the added element are similar in size to the host metal atoms and substitute (replace) them in the crystal lattice. Example: brass (Zn atoms replace some Cu atoms) and bronze (Sn atoms replace some Cu atoms).
  • Interstitial alloy: Small atoms of the added element fit into the spaces (interstices) between the larger host metal atoms. Example: steel (small C atoms fit into the gaps between Fe atoms), which makes the structure harder to deform.

6.6

VSEPR Theory & Molecular Geometry

Goal: use Lewis structures and VSEPR to predict 3D shape

What is VSEPR theory?

VSEPR (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion) theory states that the electron pairs around a central atom repel each other and arrange themselves as far apart as possible. This repulsion determines the 3D shape (geometry) of the molecule.

Vocabulary
Electron regionAny area around an atom that contains electron density — either a bond (single, double, or triple all count as ONE region) or a lone pair.
Molecular geometryThe 3-dimensional shape of a molecule based on the positions of its atoms (not counting lone pairs as "positions").
Critical Rule
Count electron regions = bonding regions + lone pairs. A double bond and a triple bond each count as just one electron region. The electron regions determine the basic geometry; lone pairs compress the bond angles.

Why lone pairs compress bond angles

Lone pairs take up more space than bonding pairs because they are held by only one nucleus (not shared between two). This means lone pairs repel bonding pairs more strongly, compressing the bond angles below the ideal value.

  • NH₃: 4 electron regions (3 bonds + 1 lone pair) → trigonal pyramidal geometry → bond angle is slightly less than 109.5° (about 107°)
  • H₂O: 4 electron regions (2 bonds + 2 lone pairs) → bent geometry → bond angle is even more compressed, about 104.5°
Water is Special: Always Draw H₂O as Bent
H₂O could theoretically look linear if you ignore lone pairs, but always draw water as bent. The two lone pairs on oxygen push the H–O–H angle down to about 104.5°, making water a bent molecule. This bent shape and the polarity it creates are responsible for many of water's unique properties, including the fact that ice floats.
6.7

Molecular Polarity

Goal: determine if a molecule is polar based on geometry and element types

Bond polarity vs. molecular polarity

A bond can be polar (if the two bonded atoms have different electronegativities) while the molecule as a whole may be nonpolar — if the bond dipoles cancel due to symmetry.

  • Polar molecule: has an uneven distribution of electrons — a net dipole. Results from asymmetric structure or different atoms on different sides.
  • Nonpolar molecule: bond dipoles cancel out because of symmetry, resulting in a net dipole of zero.
Vocabulary
DipoleOpposite charges separated by a distance; shown with an arrow pointing from δ+ to δ−.
Polar moleculeA molecule with an uneven distribution of electrons — has a net dipole (δ+ on one end, δ− on the other).
Nonpolar moleculeA molecule with an even distribution of electrons — dipoles cancel (net dipole = 0).

5-step process to determine molecular polarity

  1. Draw the correct Lewis structure.
  2. Determine the molecular geometry using VSEPR (count all electron regions).
  3. Consider the electronegativity of all atoms bonded to the central atom.
  4. Draw arrows (dipoles) pointing from the less electronegative atom toward the more electronegative atom for each bond.
  5. If the molecule is symmetrical (same atoms on all sides, dipoles cancel) → NONPOLAR. If asymmetrical (different atoms or lone pairs unbalance the dipoles) → POLAR.

And compare CH₄ (nonpolar, all H) vs. CH₃Cl (polar, one H replaced by Cl). Symmetry of both structure and atom types must be considered.
6.8

Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

Goal: identify the type of IMF for a given molecular compound

Intramolecular bonds vs. intermolecular forces

Intramolecular bonds are the bonds within a molecule (ionic, covalent, metallic — the actual chemical bonds). These are strong.

Intermolecular forces (IMFs) are the attractive forces between molecules. These are generally much weaker than intramolecular bonds, but they control physical properties like boiling point, melting point, and vapor pressure.

The collective term for all intermolecular forces is Van der Waals forces.

Vocabulary
IMF (intermolecular force)The force of attraction between molecules.
Hydrogen bondingAttraction between a hydrogen bonded to F, O, or N and a lone pair on a neighboring molecule. Strongest IMF.
Dipole-dipoleAttraction between two polar molecules (between permanent dipoles). Medium strength.
London dispersion forcesAttraction between temporary dipoles caused by the movement of electrons; present in all molecules. Weakest IMF.

The three types of IMF in detail

1. Hydrogen Bonding (strongest IMF)

Hydrogen bonding is a special, extra-strong type of dipole-dipole interaction. It occurs only when hydrogen is covalently bonded directly to a highly electronegative atom — specifically F, O, or N. The partially positive H (δ+) is attracted to a lone pair on a neighboring F, O, or N atom.

  • Molecules with H bonded to F, O, or N: H₂O, HF, NH₃, alcohols (–OH groups), etc.
  • Hydrogen bonding is why water has an unusually high boiling point (100°C) compared to similar-sized molecules.
  • It's also why ice floats: the HB network in ice creates an open structure that is less dense than liquid water.

2. Dipole-Dipole Interactions (medium strength)

Dipole-dipole forces occur between any two polar molecules. The δ+ end of one molecule is attracted to the δ− end of a neighboring molecule.

  • Any polar molecule has dipole-dipole forces: HCl, H₂S, SO₂, CH₂Cl₂, etc.
  • Weaker than hydrogen bonding, but stronger than London dispersion.
  • Note: Hydrogen bonding is simply a very strong and special case of dipole-dipole interaction. A molecule with hydrogen bonding also has dipole-dipole forces — HB is the dominant one.

3. London Dispersion Forces (weakest IMF — but universal)

London dispersion forces (LDF) exist in all molecules — polar and nonpolar alike. They arise from temporary (instantaneous) dipoles: at any given moment, electrons in a molecule are not perfectly evenly distributed. This creates a temporary dipole that induces a dipole in a neighboring molecule, and the two temporary dipoles attract each other.

  • Present in: CH₄, I₂, noble gases, N₂, and all other molecules including polar ones.
  • London forces increase with the number of electrons in the molecule and with the size/mass of the molecule. Larger molecules have more electrons and stronger LDF.
  • Example: I₂ has a much higher boiling point than F₂ because I₂ has far more electrons and therefore much stronger London forces.

IMF identification rules — summary

Polar molecule (but no H–F, H–O, or H–N bond)London dispersion + dipole-dipole
Molecule has H bonded directly to F, O, or NLondon dispersion + dipole-dipole + hydrogen bonding
Key Idea
Molecules can have multiple types of IMF simultaneously — you list all that apply. For example, water (H₂O) has all three: London dispersion, dipole-dipole, AND hydrogen bonding. When ranking, use the strongest IMF present to determine properties.

6.9

IMFs & Physical Properties

Goal: explain and compare boiling points and vapor pressure using IMF strength
Vocabulary
Vapor pressureThe pressure of the vapor above a liquid in a closed container; a measure of how easily a liquid evaporates.
Boiling pointThe temperature at which the vapor pressure of a liquid meets or exceeds the external (atmospheric) pressure.
VolatileA substance that easily vaporizes at normal temperatures (has a high vapor pressure and low boiling point).

How IMF strength affects physical properties

The stronger the intermolecular forces between molecules, the more energy is required to separate them — whether melting (solid → liquid) or boiling (liquid → gas).

Property↑ Stronger IMF →↑ Weaker IMF →Relationship
Boiling pointHigher BPLower BPDirect (↑IMF = ↑BP)
Melting pointHigher MPLower MPDirect (↑IMF = ↑MP)
Vapor pressureLower VP (less volatile)Higher VP (more volatile)Inverse (↑IMF = ↓VP)

Summary relationship:

  • ↑ IMF = ↓ vapor pressure → inverse relationship
  • ↑ IMF = ↑ melting & boiling points → direct relationship

How to rank boiling points

Use this 3-step approach:

  1. Identify the strongest IMF for each molecule. (HB > dipole-dipole > London dispersion)
  2. Compare IMF types: molecules with stronger IMF types have higher boiling points, regardless of size.
  3. If molecules have the same IMF type, the larger/heavier molecule (more electrons) has stronger London dispersion forces and therefore a higher boiling point.
Worked Examples from Class

Example 1: H₂O vs. H₂S
H₂O boils at 100°C; H₂S boils at −60°C. Why? H₂O has H bonded to O → hydrogen bonding (the strongest IMF). H₂S has H bonded to S (not F/O/N) → only dipole-dipole. Hydrogen bonding is much stronger than dipole-dipole, so water requires far more energy to boil.

Example 2: H₂O vs. isopropyl alcohol (rubbing alcohol)
Water boils at 100°C; isopropyl alcohol boils at 82.5°C. Both have hydrogen bonding (both have O–H groups). But water has a higher boiling point because water molecules form more HB per molecule (2 lone pairs on O available for bonding), whereas isopropyl alcohol's larger nonpolar hydrocarbon part weakens the overall HB network and increases London dispersion in a different way. The key takeaway: water's HB network is unusually strong for its molecular size.

Example 3: F₂ vs. Cl₂ vs. Br₂ vs. I₂ (the halogens)
Boiling points: F₂ (−188°C) < Cl₂ (−34°C) < Br₂ (59°C) < I₂ (184°C). All are nonpolar — they only have London dispersion forces. As you go down the group: more electrons, larger atoms → stronger London forces → higher boiling point. I₂ has the most electrons and the highest boiling point.

Example 4: CH₂O vs. CBr₄ vs. SF₂
To compare boiling points when molecules are similar in size, look at which IMF is stronger. SF₂ (polar, has dipole-dipole) would have a higher boiling point than CBr₄ (nonpolar, London dispersion only) even though CBr₄ has more electrons, because the IMF type matters first.

Why molecular compounds have low melting/boiling points

When a molecular compound melts or boils, you are breaking the intermolecular forces between molecules — not the covalent bonds within the molecules. Because IMFs are much weaker than covalent bonds, relatively little energy is needed. This is why molecular compounds often exist as liquids or gases at room temperature.

Molecular properties summary

Compared to ionic and metallic compounds, molecular (covalent) compounds generally have:

  • Lower melting and boiling points
  • Existence as solids, liquids, or gases at room temperature
  • Poor electrical conductivity
  • Variable solubility in water (polar molecules dissolve; nonpolar do not)
