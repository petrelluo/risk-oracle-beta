Introduction: The Ultimate Lego Set

A chemical reaction is the amazing process where matter transforms. It happens all around us, every single day! Before we dive into the math of chemistry, let's look at a few real-world examples:

🔥 A Campfire

Wood combusts with oxygen in the air to release heat, light, carbon dioxide gas, and water vapor.

🚲 Rusting Metal

An old bicycle left in the rain. The iron (Fe) reacts with oxygen (O₂) to form a crumbly new substance: Iron Oxide (rust).

🧁 Baking a Cake

Baking powder reacts with heat to create trapped carbon dioxide gas bubbles, making the cake rise and become fluffy.

To understand how these transformations work, you must understand the golden rule of chemistry: The Law of Conservation of Mass.

Think of atoms like Lego blocks. When a chemical reaction happens, you do not get any brand new Legos out of nowhere, and none of your old Legos magically disappear. You simply take apart your starting creations (the Reactants) and use those exact same blocks to build completely new things (the Products).

Below is a graph showing the combustion of Methane (CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O). Notice how the chemical substances change completely, but the actual count of every single atom stays exactly the same!

The graph visualizes that no atoms are lost or gained. 1 Carbon, 4 Hydrogens, and 4 Oxygens go in, and that exact same number comes out, just rearranged into entirely new molecules.

Step 1: The Cast of Characters (Chemical Formulas)

Before writing a sentence, you need to know the words. In chemistry, the "words" are chemical formulas.

  • Symbols: 1- or 2-letter symbols from the Periodic Table (e.g., H for Hydrogen, Na for Sodium).
  • Subscripts (The small, low numbers): These tell you exactly how many atoms of that specific element are glued together in a single molecule. Example: In H2O, the subscript 2 means there are 2 Hydrogen atoms bonded to 1 Oxygen atom.
CRITICAL RULE: Charge Neutrality

When you are constructing the formula for an ionic compound (a metal + a nonmetal), you must ensure the molecule has a net charge of zero. You do this by adjusting the subscripts, NOT the coefficients.

Example: Magnesium is Mg2+. Chlorine is Cl-. To make them neutral, you need two negative Chlorines to balance the positive two of Magnesium. Therefore, the correct formula is MgCl2.

Step 2: The Stage Directions (State Symbols)

Chemicals behave differently depending on their physical state. We indicate this using small letters in parentheses right after the chemical formula:

  • (s) = Solid (like ice, metals, or salt crystals)
  • (l) = Liquid (pure liquids like liquid water or liquid bromine)
  • (g) = Gas (like steam, oxygen, or carbon dioxide)
  • (aq) = Aqueous (meaning the chemical is dissolved in water, like saltwater)

Step 3: Writing the "Skeleton" Equation

A chemical equation is divided into two halves, separated by an arrow.

  • Reactants (Left Side): Your starting ingredients. Separate multiple reactants with a plus sign (+).
  • The Arrow (→): Means "yields" or "reacts to produce".
  • Products (Right Side): The final result of the reaction.

Skeleton Equation Example (Unbalanced): H2(g) + O2(g) → H2O(l)

Step 4: The Law of Conservation (Balancing)

Because of the Law of Conservation of Mass we learned in the introduction, if you start a reaction with 2 oxygen atoms, you MUST end with 2 oxygen atoms.

  • Coefficients (The big numbers in front): These tell you how many entire molecules you have. This is the ONLY number you are allowed to change to balance an equation.
  • If you place a 2 in front of water (2H2O), it multiplies everything inside the molecule by 2 (giving you 4 Hydrogens and 2 Oxygens).
The Fully Balanced Equation:
2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)

Step 5: Classifying Chemical Reactions

Just like movies belong to genres (comedy, action, horror), chemical reactions belong to specific categories. Knowing these patterns helps you predict what products will form. Here are the five main types:

1. Synthesis (Combination)

A + B → AB

Two or more simple substances combine to form a single, more complex product. They "synthesize" something new.

Example: 2Na(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2NaCl(s) (Sodium and chlorine combine to make table salt)

2. Decomposition

AB → A + B

The exact opposite of synthesis. One complex compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances. This usually requires heat or electricity.

Example: 2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) (Water breaking down into hydrogen and oxygen gas)

3. Single-Replacement

A + BC → AC + B

One active element steps in and "tags out" a less active element in a compound, taking its place.

Example: Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂(g) (Zinc replaces the Hydrogen in the acid)

4. Double-Replacement

AB + CD → AD + CB

Two ionic compounds trade partners. The positive ions (A and C) switch places to form two entirely new compounds.

Example: AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq) (Silver and Sodium swap their negative partners)

5. Combustion

CₓHₗ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

A substance (usually a hydrocarbon fuel) reacts rapidly with Oxygen gas (O₂), releasing heat and light. If it's a hydrocarbon, the products are almost always Carbon Dioxide and Water.

Example: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(g) (Burning methane gas)
