📚 Key Terms Glossary
All vocabulary you need to know for this quiz.
Solution
A homogeneous mixture of a solute dissolved in a solvent.
Solute
The substance being dissolved (present in smaller amount).
Solvent
The substance doing the dissolving (usually water).
Aqueous Solution
A solution in which water is the solvent.
Dissolving
Process by which solute particles separate and become surrounded by solvent molecules.
Dissociation
Ionic compound separating into its component ions when dissolved. Occurs only in ionic compounds, NOT molecular compounds.
Hydrated
A dissolved ion or molecule that is surrounded by water molecules.
Solubility
The maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature.
Unsaturated Solution
Contains less solute than the maximum possible; can dissolve more solute.
Saturated Solution
Contains the maximum amount of dissolved solute at a given temperature and pressure.
Supersaturated Solution
Contains more solute than should theoretically be possible at a given temperature; very unstable.
Solution Equilibrium
State where rate of dissolving = rate of recrystallization; total dissolved solute stays constant.
Recrystallization
Process of dissolved solute returning to the solid state.
Seed Crystal
A small crystal of solute added to a supersaturated solution to initiate crystallization.
Rate of Dissolution
How quickly a solute dissolves. Affected by: agitation, temperature, and surface area.
Concentration
Amount of solute dissolved in a given amount of solvent or solution.
Dilute Solution
Contains a small amount of solute relative to the solvent.
Concentrated Solution
Contains a large amount of solute relative to the solvent.
Molarity (M)
Moles of solute per liter of SOLUTION. M = mol/L. Changes with temperature.
Molality (m)
Moles of solute per kilogram of SOLVENT. m = mol/kg. Does NOT change with temperature.
Stock Solution
A concentrated solution of known concentration used to make diluted solutions.
Volumetric Flask
A pear-shaped flask with a long neck calibrated for precise volume; used for making accurate solutions.
Miscible
Two liquids that mix in all proportions with no phase separation (e.g., water and vinegar).
Immiscible
Two liquids that do NOT mix and separate into phases (e.g., water and oil).
Coulombic Attraction
Attraction between oppositely charged particles. Depends on magnitude of charges and distance between them.
Electrolyte
A substance that produces ions when dissolved in water, conducting electricity. Ionic compounds are electrolytes.
Polar Molecule
A molecule with an uneven distribution of charge due to differences in electronegativity (e.g., water, sucrose).
Nonpolar Molecule
A molecule with an even distribution of charge; generally does NOT dissolve in water (e.g., oil, CO).
Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)
Attractive forces between molecules. "Like dissolves like" means similar IMFs lead to dissolution.
Agitation
Stirring or mixing. Brings fresh solvent into contact with solute, increasing dissolution rate.
Polyatomic Ion
A charged group of atoms bonded together (e.g., NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻). Does NOT further dissociate when the ionic compound dissolves.
Henry's Law
The solubility of a gas increases as pressure above the solution increases (gas solubility ∝ pressure).

Topic 8.2 — The Dissolving Process ⭐ TEACHER PRIORITY

⚠️
This is directly from the teacher's self-prepared guided notes. Expect questions on illustrating dissolved substances, dissociation vs hydration, and identifying polar/nonpolar molecules.

🧂 Ionic Compounds Dissolving

  • Ionic compounds dissociate — they break into separate ions
  • Each ion becomes hydrated (surrounded by water molecules)
  • The partial negative end of water (O) attracts cations (+)
  • The partial positive end (H) attracts anions (−)
  • Example: NaCl → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)
  • Example: CaCl₂ → Ca²⁺(aq) + 2 Cl⁻(aq)
  • 2 units of CaCl₂ → 2 Ca²⁺ + 4 Cl⁻ in solution
  • Na₂S → 2Na⁺ + S²⁻ per formula unit

🍬 Molecular Compounds Dissolving

  • Polar molecules dissolve in water but do NOT dissociate
  • Individual molecules separate from each other and become hydrated
  • The molecules remain intact (no bond breaking)
  • Example: Sucrose (sugar) — polar because of –OH groups
  • Example: NH₃ — polar, dissolves; individual molecules hydrated
  • Nonpolar molecules (like CO) do NOT dissolve in water
  • CO in beaker diagram → molecules stay SEPARATE, NOT dissociated
💡
Tricky AgNO₃ Question: AgNO₃ dissolves to give Ag⁺ and NO₃⁻. Even though it's ionic, the nitrate ion (NO₃⁻) is a polyatomic ion — a charged molecule. It does NOT further dissociate when AgNO₃ dissolves!

Chapters 16.2 — Rate of Dissolution

🌊 Agitation (Stirring)

Brings fresh solvent molecules into contact with solute. Without stirring, the area around the solute becomes saturated, slowing dissolving. Does NOT change how much dissolves — only how fast.

🌡️ Temperature

Higher temperature → more kinetic energy → molecules move faster → more frequent and forceful collisions with solute → faster rate. For solids, also increases how much dissolves.

🔩 Surface Area

Crushing or grinding creates smaller particles with greater surface area → more contact points between solute and solvent → faster dissolving. Does NOT change how much dissolves.

Chapters 16.3 & 16.5 — Saturation States

📊 Reading Solubility Curves

  • X-axis = temperature (°C), Y-axis = grams of solute per 100g water
  • Points ON the curve = saturated solution
  • Points BELOW the curve = unsaturated
  • Points ABOVE the curve = supersaturated
  • Steeper slope = more soluble compound
  • NaCl has nearly flat slope (solubility barely changes with temp)
  • NaNO₃ most soluble at 30°C (~95g/100g water)

🔮 Supersaturated Solutions

  • Contains more dissolved solute than should be possible at that temperature
  • Very unstable state
  • Adding a seed crystal initiates rapid crystallization
  • After crystallization, solution becomes saturated
  • Example: honey that has crystallized; hot-pack hand warmers

Chapter 16.4 — Temperature & Solubility

🏔️ Solid/Liquid Solutes

As temperature increases, kinetic energy increases → solvent collides more often and with more force against the solid solute → IMFs between solute particles weaken → MORE solid dissolves. Direct relationship.

🫧 Gaseous Solutes

As temperature increases, gas molecules have more energy to escape the solution → LESS gas dissolves. As pressure above solution increases, more gas is forced into solution → MORE dissolves (Henry's Law). Inverse with temp, direct with pressure.

Chapters 16.9 — Molarity

Molarity Formula
M = molessolute / literssolution
⚠️ Volume = total SOLUTION, not just water. 1 mL = 0.001 L. Divide mL by 1000 to get L.
💡
Step-by-step problem strategy: (1) Convert grams → moles using molar mass. (2) Convert mL → L. (3) Divide moles by liters. Always check units.

Making Solutions — Dilution Equation ⭐

Dilution Equation
McVc = MdVd
c = concentrated (stock), d = diluted. Moles don't change during dilution — only volume increases.
STEP 1Identify Mc, Md, Vd (given)
STEP 2Solve for Vc = (Md × Vd) / Mc
STEP 3Pipette Vc mL of stock into volumetric flask
STEP 4Fill to volume line with water

Worked Examples

Example 1 — Find Molarity from grams

10.0g LiCl (MM = 42.5 g/mol) dissolved in 100 mL solution.

Step 1: moles = 10.0g ÷ 42.5 g/mol = 0.235 mol
Step 2: V = 100 mL = 0.100 L
Step 3: M = 0.235 mol ÷ 0.100 L = 2.35 M LiCl

Example 2 — Find moles from M and V

How many moles of Cu(NO₃)₂ are needed for 250 mL of 1.5 M solution?

moles = M × V = 1.5 M × 0.25 L = 0.375 mol

Example 3 — Find volume from moles and M

What volume of 2.0 M KF from 50.0g KF (MM = 58.1 g/mol)?

moles KF = 50.0 g ÷ 58.1 g/mol = 0.861 mol
V = 0.861 mol ÷ 2.0 M = 0.43 L

Example 4 — Dilution Equation

How to make 1.0 L of 1.0 M HCl from 11.7 M stock?

M꜀V꜀ = M_dV_d → 11.7 × V꜀ = 1.0 × 1.0
V꜀ = 1.0 ÷ 11.7 = 0.0855 L = 85.5 mL

Example 5 — Grams from molarity + volume

How many grams of Cu(NO₃)₂ (MM=187.6 g/mol) to make 500 mL of 0.5 M?

moles = 0.5 M × 0.500 L = 0.25 mol
grams = 0.25 mol × 187.6 g/mol = 46.9 g

Example 6 — Molality

28.60g of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆, MM=180.18 g/mol) dissolved in 250g of water.

moles = 28.60 g ÷ 180.18 g/mol = 0.1587 mol
m = 0.1587 mol ÷ 0.250 kg = 0.635 m
