Ch. 15.7 & 15.9 — Water as Solvent

Why Water Is the "Universal Solvent"

Water is a polar covalent molecule with a bent shape (~105° bond angle). The highly electronegative oxygen pulls electron density away from the hydrogen atoms, giving O a partial negative charge (δ−) and each H a partial positive charge (δ+). This polarity allows water to:

  • Attract and surround ionic compounds — the δ− oxygen sides face cations; the δ+ hydrogen sides face anions (hydration).
  • Interact with other polar covalent molecules through dipole–dipole attractions.
  • Form strong hydrogen bonds (H bonded to F, O, or N in a neighboring molecule).
  • Give water an unusually low vapor pressure and high surface tension compared to molecules of similar size.

Solubility Rule

"Like Dissolves Like" — IMF Matching

  • Substances dissolve in solvents with similar intermolecular forces (IMFs).
  • Polar solutes dissolve in polar solvents; nonpolar solutes dissolve in nonpolar solvents.
  • A substance is considered soluble if >0.1 mol dissolves per liter of water.
  • For ionic solubility: ions with smaller size or lower charge tend to be more soluble (weaker coulombic attraction = easier to separate).
Ch. 16.2 — Rate of Dissolving

3 Factors That Affect Rate (not solubility!)

  • Agitation (stirring) — keeps fresh solvent in contact with solute, increasing collision frequency.
  • Temperature — molecules move faster, collide more often and more energetically.
  • Surface area — smaller/crushed particles expose more surface for collisions (e.g., powdered sugar vs. a sugar cube).

🔑 Collision Theory governs the RATE at which a solute dissolves. Solubility determines HOW MUCH can dissolve.

Ch. 16.3 & 16.5 — Solution Types

Unsaturated, Saturated & Supersaturated

  • Unsaturated — can still dissolve more solute at that temperature.
  • Saturated — contains the maximum amount of dissolved solute at a given temperature. Rate of dissolving = rate of crystallization (dynamic equilibrium).
  • Supersaturated — contains more solute than theoretically possible; highly unstable. Any disturbance (adding a seed crystal, stirring) causes rapid crystallization (e.g., rock candy).
Ch. 16.4 — Temperature & Solubility

How Temperature Affects Solubility

  • For most solid solutes: solubility increases with temperature (steeper solubility curve = more sensitive to temperature).
  • For gases dissolved in liquids: solubility decreases as temperature rises (why warm soda goes flat).
  • Solubility curves show grams of solute that dissolve in 100 g of water at various temperatures. Points above the curve = supersaturated; on the curve = saturated; below = unsaturated.
  • NaCl is unusual — its solubility changes very little with temperature (nearly flat curve).
Ch. 16.9 — Preparing Solutions

Making Accurate Solutions (Molarity)

  • A volumetric flask — pear-shaped, calibrated to a precise volume — is used to make accurate solutions.
  • Method 1: Weigh solute → dissolve in a small amount of water → transfer to volumetric flask → dilute to the calibration line.
  • Method 2: Dilution from a concentrated stock solution using: McVc = MdVd
  • ⚠️ Always add concentrated acid to water, not water to acid (to avoid dangerous exothermic splashing).

Ch. 16.12 — Colligative Properties

Vapor Pressure Lowering (Raoult's Law)

Colligative properties depend only on the number of solute particles — not their chemical identity. They include:

  • Vapor pressure lowering — adding a nonvolatile solute reduces vapor pressure because solute particles occupy the surface, reducing the rate at which solvent evaporates. The more solute, the lower the vapor pressure.
  • Boiling point elevation — lower vapor pressure means a higher temperature is needed to reach atmospheric pressure → solution boils higher than pure solvent.
  • Freezing point depression — solute particles disrupt the crystal lattice formation → solution freezes lower than pure solvent.

Electrolytes (ionic compounds) produce MORE particles per formula unit → greater colligative effect than nonelectrolytes at the same molality.

⚡ Quick Key Ideas to Remember

  • 1Ionic compounds dissociate into separate ions; polar molecular compounds remain intact (only hydrated).
  • 2Rate (stirring, temp, surface area) ≠ Solubility (how much dissolves).
  • 3Supersaturated solutions are unstable and will crystallize with any disturbance.
  • 4Gas solubility decreases with temperature; solid solubility usually increases.
  • 5Use molality (not molarity) for colligative property calculations.
  • 6All colligative properties are driven by the number of dissolved particles.
  • 7Volumetric flasks ensure precise solution concentrations. Always dilute: McVc = MdVd.

Key Formulas

Everything you need for calculations

Molarity
M = mol solute ÷ L solution
Units: mol/L  |  Symbol: M (uppercase)  |  Used for most concentration problems
Molality
m = mol solute ÷ kg solvent
Units: mol/kg  |  Symbol: m (lowercase italic)  |  Used for colligative properties
Dilution Formula
McVc = MdVd
c = concentrated stock  |  d = diluted solution  |  V can be in mL or L (keep consistent)
Percent by Mass
% m/m = (mass solute ÷ mass solution) × 100
Also written: mass solute ÷ (mass solute + mass solvent) × 100
Boiling Point Elevation (Colligative)
ΔTb = Kb × m
Kb(water) = 0.51°C/m  |  m = molality  |  Solution BP = 100°C + ΔTb
Freezing Point Depression (Colligative)
ΔTf = Kf × m
Kf(water) = 1.86°C/m  |  Solution FP = 0°C − ΔTf

Worked Example: Dilution

How is 1.0 L of a 1.0 M HCl solution made from an 11.7 M stock?

  1. Set up: McVc = MdVd → 11.7 × Vc = 1.0 × 1.0 L
  2. Solve: Vc = 1.0/11.7 = 0.0855 L = 85.5 mL
  3. Procedure: Fill volumetric flask ¼ with water, add 85.5 mL stock via buret, dilute to 1.0 L fill line.

Worked Example: Molality

Find molality of a solution made by dissolving 28.60 g glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆, MW = 180.18) in 250 g water.

  1. mol glucose = 28.60 ÷ 180.18 = 0.1588 mol
  2. kg solvent = 250 g ÷ 1000 = 0.250 kg
  3. m = 0.1588 mol ÷ 0.250 kg = 0.635 m

Key Terms

Definitions you must know for the test

Solute
The substance being dissolved (present in smaller amount).
Solvent
The substance doing the dissolving (present in larger amount). Usually water in aqueous solutions.
Aqueous solution
A homogeneous mixture in which water is the solvent.
Hydration
Water molecules surrounding and stabilizing dissolved ions or molecules.
Dissociation
The separation of an ionic compound into its individual ions when dissolved in water.
Solubility
The maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature.
Unsaturated solution
A solution that can still dissolve more solute at the current temperature.
Saturated solution
A solution containing the maximum dissolved solute at a given temperature; in dynamic equilibrium with undissolved solute.
Supersaturated solution
An unstable solution containing more dissolved solute than should be possible at the current temperature.
Miscible
Two liquids that mix in all proportions without phase separation (e.g., water and vinegar).
Immiscible
Two liquids that do not mix and form separate layers (e.g., water and oil).
Molarity (M)
Moles of solute per liter of solution. M = mol/L. Changes with temperature.
Molality (m)
Moles of solute per kilogram of solvent. m = mol/kg. Does not change with temperature.
Colligative property
A property of a solution that depends only on the number of dissolved particles, not their identity (e.g., vapor pressure lowering, BP elevation, FP depression).
Vapor pressure lowering
Reduction in vapor pressure of a solvent caused by the presence of a nonvolatile solute (Raoult's Law).
Volumetric flask
A pear-shaped flask calibrated to contain a precise volume; used to prepare accurate solutions.
Electrolyte
A substance that dissociates into ions in solution, making the solution electrically conductive.
Coulombic attraction
The attraction between oppositely charged particles; depends on magnitude of charge and distance between charges.
