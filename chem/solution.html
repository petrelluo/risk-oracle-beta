1. Classifying Mixtures & The Tyndall Effect

A mixture's classification depends heavily on particle size and how it behaves over time.

  • Solutions (Homogeneous): Particles are completely dissolved (atomic/molecular size). They never settle, cannot be filtered, and light passes straight through them. (Example: Saltwater, air, brass alloys).
  • Suspensions (Heterogeneous): Large particles that temporarily float but will settle to the bottom if left alone. (Example: Muddy water, snow globes).
  • Colloids (Heterogeneous): Medium-sized particles that do not settle. Because the particles are larger than molecules, they scatter light. This scattering is known as the Tyndall Effect. (Example: Fog, milk, mayonnaise).

The Golden Rule: "Like dissolves like." Polar solvents (water) dissolve polar/ionic solutes. Nonpolar solvents (oil) dissolve nonpolar solutes. Mixing polar and nonpolar creates an immiscible mixture (they separate).

2. Energetics of the Dissolving Process

When a solute dissolves (solvation/hydration), three distinct energy steps occur. The overall Heat of Solution (ΔHsoln) determines if the liquid gets hot or cold.

  1. Step 1 (Endothermic): Energy is required to break the bonds holding the solute together.
  2. Step 2 (Endothermic): Energy is required to spread out the solvent molecules to make room.
  3. Step 3 (Exothermic): Energy is released when the solvent and solute attract each other and form new bonds.
Concept Check: Hot vs. Cold Packs
Exothermic (Hot Pack): If Step 3 releases MORE energy than Steps 1 & 2 required, the overall reaction releases heat into the water, making the solution feel hot.
Endothermic (Cold Pack): If Steps 1 & 2 require MORE energy than Step 3 releases, the reaction steals heat from the water, making the solution feel cold.

3. Dissociation & Electrolytes

Substances behave differently regarding electricity when dissolved based on whether they form charged ions.

  • Strong Electrolytes: 100% dissociate into ions. Excellent conductors. (Ionic salts, strong acids).
  • Nonelectrolytes: Dissolve, but stay entirely intact as neutral molecules. Do not conduct. (Sugars, alcohols).
Sample Problem: Writing Dissociation Equations

Question: Write the dissociation equation for Aluminum Chloride (AlCl3) and determine how many total moles of ions are produced per mole of solid.

1. Identify the ions: Aluminum is Al3+ and Chloride is Cl-.
2. Balance the equation: The subscript 3 becomes a coefficient.
3. Equation: AlCl3(s) → Al3+(aq) + 3Cl-(aq)
4. Result: 1 mole of Al3+ + 3 moles of Cl- = 4 total moles of ions.

4. Solubility & Environmental Factors

Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can fit into a solvent. Unsaturated means it can hold more; Saturated means it is exactly full; Supersaturated is a tricked, unstable state holding too much.

Factor Effect on Solid Solutes Effect on Gas Solutes
Increase Temperature Increases solubility (dissolves more) Decreases solubility (gas escapes)
Increase Pressure No effect Increases solubility (Henry's Law)

5. Concentration Math: Molarity, Molality, and Dilution

Concentration measures how "crowded" the solute is.

Sample Problem 1: Molarity (M)

Formula: M = moles of solute / Liters of solution

Question: What is the molarity of 58.44 g of NaCl dissolved in 2.0 L of water? (Molar mass of NaCl = 58.44 g/mol)

Step 1 (Convert grams to moles): 58.44 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = 1.0 mole NaCl.
Step 2 (Apply formula): M = 1.0 mol / 2.0 L
Answer: 0.50 M
Sample Problem 2: Molality (m)

Formula: m = moles of solute / kg of solvent (Used because it doesn't change with temperature!).

Question: You dissolve 0.50 moles of sugar into 250 grams of water. What is the molality?

Step 1 (Convert grams to kg): 250 g ÷ 1000 = 0.25 kg water.
Step 2 (Apply formula): m = 0.50 mol / 0.25 kg
Answer: 2.0 m
Sample Problem 3: Dilutions

Formula: M1V1 = M2V2

Question: You have 100 mL of a 6.0 M stock solution of HCl. You add water until the final volume is 300 mL. What is the new concentration?

Step 1 (Plug in values): (6.0 M)(100 mL) = (M2)(300 mL)
Step 2 (Solve for M2): 600 = 300 × M2
Answer: M2 = 2.0 M
