Part 1 · Ranking Electronegativity

📖 Key Concept
Electronegativity is the ability of an atom to attract electrons toward itself in a chemical bond. The higher the value, the more strongly an atom pulls electrons.

The Two Golden Rules

⚡ Rule 1 — Across a Period (left → right)
Electronegativity increases as you move left to right across a row (period). More protons = stronger pull on electrons.
⚡ Rule 2 — Down a Group (top → bottom)
Electronegativity decreases as you move down a column (group). Electrons are farther from the nucleus and more shielded.
💡 Quick Memory Trick
Think of a "staircase going up and to the right" on the periodic table — that's where electronegativity is highest. Fluorine (F) is always #1. Noble gases are excluded (they don't bond).

Worked Example 1 — Ranking 4 elements

Rank the following from lowest to highest electronegativity: Na, Cl, O, F

✏️ STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
1
Find each element on the periodic table and note its position (period and group).
2
Na → Period 3, Group 1 (far left) — very low EN.
Cl → Period 3, Group 17 (right side, same row as Na) — much higher EN than Na.
O → Period 2, Group 16 — above S/Cl, so higher than Cl.
F → Period 2, Group 17 (top-right corner) — highest of all.
3
Apply the rules: left→right increases; bottom→top increases.
Lowest → Highest: Na < Cl < O < F

Worked Example 2 — Comparing within a group

Rank F, Cl, Br, I from highest to lowest electronegativity.

✏️ STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
1
All four are in Group 17 (halogens) — same column.
2
Going down the group, EN decreases. F is at the top, I is at the bottom.
Highest → Lowest: F > Cl > Br > I

Part 2 · Ranking Boiling Points

📖 Key Concept
To boil a liquid, you must break the intermolecular forces (IMFs) between molecules. Stronger IMFs → more energy needed → higher boiling point.

Step 1 — Know the 3 Types of IMFs (weakest to strongest)

IMF TypeWhat molecules have it?Relative Strength
London Dispersion (LD) ALL molecules; dominant in nonpolar molecules Weakest (but grows with molecule size)
Dipole-Dipole (DD) Polar molecules (uneven electron distribution) Moderate
Hydrogen Bonding (HB) Molecules with H bonded to F, O, or N Strongest
💡 The Big Rule
HB > DD > LD in terms of boiling point. But if comparing two molecules with the same IMF type, the larger/heavier molecule will have the higher boiling point (stronger LD forces).

Step 2 — How to Rank Boiling Points (the method)

🔧 The 3-Question Method
Ask these in order for each molecule:
1. Does it have H bonded to F, O, or N? → If yes: Hydrogen Bonding (highest BP)
2. Is the molecule polar? → If yes: Dipole-Dipole (medium BP)
3. Neither? → London Dispersion only (lowest BP, unless molecule is very large)

Worked Example 3 — Ranking three small molecules

Rank from lowest to highest boiling point: CH₄, HCl, H₂O

✏️ STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
1
CH₄ (methane): H is bonded to C, NOT to F/O/N. No dipole (symmetric, nonpolar). → London Dispersion only. Very small molecule. Lowest BP.
2
HCl: H is bonded to Cl (not F, O, or N). But Cl is very electronegative → molecule IS polar. → Dipole-Dipole. Medium BP.
3
H₂O: H is bonded to O — that's one of the magic three (F, O, N)! → Hydrogen Bonding. Highest BP.
Lowest → Highest BP: CH₄ (-161°C) < HCl (-85°C) < H₂O (100°C)

Worked Example 4 — Same IMF type, different sizes

Rank from lowest to highest boiling point: CH₄, C₄H₁₀, C₈H₁₈

✏️ STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
1
All three are nonpolar hydrocarbons → London Dispersion only for all.
2
Since they share the same IMF type, bigger molecule = more electrons = stronger LD = higher BP.
3
CH₄ (1 carbon) < C₄H₁₀ (4 carbons, butane) < C₈H₁₈ (8 carbons, octane).
Lowest → Highest BP: CH₄ < C₄H₁₀ < C₈H₁₈

📌 Summary to share with students
Electronegativity: Increases → and ↑ on the periodic table. F = highest always.
Boiling Point: Determined by IMF strength. HB > DD > LD. For the same IMF type, bigger molecule = higher BP.
Key link: High electronegativity difference between bonded atoms → polar bond → may lead to stronger IMF → higher BP.
