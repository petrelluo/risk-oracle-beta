⚛️ Chemical Bonding

Ionic, Metallic, and Covalent Study Guide

Step 1: Predicting the Bond Type

The Prime Directive of Bonding: Atoms bond to achieve a stable, full outer electron shell (a "perfect octet"). By bonding, they reach a state of lower, more stable potential energy.

You can predict which bond will form based on the Electronegativity (EN) difference between the two atoms. EN is how strongly an atom pulls on shared electrons.

The Quick Rule:
Metal + Nonmetal = Ionic Bond
Metal + Metal = Metallic Bond
Nonmetal + Nonmetal = Covalent Bond

Step 2: Ionic Bonding (The Transfer)

An ionic bond is the strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions. It occurs when a metal completely transfers its electron(s) to a nonmetal.

The Electron Transfer

Metals have very low ionization energy, making it easy to pull their valence electrons away. Nonmetals have high electronegativity and readily accept those electrons to complete their shell.

The Radius Shift

When electrons are transferred, physical sizes change dramatically. Metals lose their outer shell and shrink (becoming positive cations). Nonmetals gain electrons which repel each other, causing the cloud to grow (becoming negative anions).

The Ionic Crystal Lattice

Ionic compounds spontaneously assemble into a massive, repeating 3D grid called a Crystal Lattice. The chemical formula (like MgCl₂) simply represents the lowest ratio of these ions that results in a neutral zero charge.

Macroscopic Properties

  • High Melting Points: The electrostatic bonds are extremely strong.
  • Brittle: If struck, the lattice shifts. Positive ions align with positive ions, violently repelling and shattering the crystal.
  • Conductivity: They do not conduct as solids. They ONLY conduct when melted or dissolved in water, freeing the ions.

Step 3: Metallic Bonding (Sea of Electrons)

Instead of trading electrons, tightly packed metal atoms share them in a massive, communal pool. Because they have a weak hold on outer electrons, valence electrons detach and flow freely, creating a "Sea of Delocalized Electrons" surrounding positively charged nuclei.

Macroscopic Properties

  • Excellent Conductors: Because the "sea of electrons" is highly mobile, metals conduct electricity easily, even as solids.
  • Malleable & Ductile: You can hammer them or pull them into wires. The fluid electrons allow the metal nuclei to slide past each other without breaking the bonds.
  • Alloys: Mixing metals (like Copper + Zinc = Brass) creates a distorted lattice, making the metal much harder and stronger.

Step 4: Covalent Bonding (The Sharing)

When two nonmetals interact, neither wants to give up an electron (they both have high electronegativity). Instead, their electron clouds overlap and they share valence electrons to complete their octets.

Macroscopic Properties

  • Low Melting/Boiling Points: While the bond inside the molecule is strong, the forces between separate molecules are weak.
  • Poor Conductors: Because electrons are locked tightly in the shared bond, there are no free-moving charges. They do not conduct electricity.
