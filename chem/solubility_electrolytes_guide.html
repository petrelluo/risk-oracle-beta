The master rule: "like dissolves like"

The single idea that everything else follows from

A solvent can only dissolve a solute if the forces between solvent and solute are comparable to or stronger than the forces holding the solute together and the forces holding the solvent together.

Polar / ionic solutes

Dissolve in polar solvents like water. Water's partial charges attract ions and polar molecules strongly enough to pull them apart.

NaCl, KNO₃, sugar, ethanol ✓
Nonpolar solutes

Dissolve in nonpolar solvents like hexane or benzene. Matching van der Waals forces make it favorable.

Fats in hexane, wax in oil ✓   — not in water ✗
Why water is special: Water is bent and polar with a large dipole moment, plus two H-bond donors and two H-bond acceptors. This gives it an unusually strong ability to stabilize cations (through its oxygen's lone pairs) and anions (through its H-bond donor H atoms). Almost nothing matches water as a solvent for ionic and polar compounds.
"Like dissolves like" is a tendency, not a law. Some ionic compounds are insoluble in water because their lattice energy is too high (BaSO₄, AgCl). Some polar organics are barely soluble because their nonpolar portion is too large (long-chain alcohols). Always verify with the specific solubility rules below.

How ionic compounds dissolve

Ion–dipole forces vs lattice energy

Ionic solids are held together by strong electrostatic attraction between alternating + and − ions in a crystal lattice. For one to dissolve, water must pull those ions away one by one.

The mechanism

step 1
Water orients
O (δ−) faces cations, H (δ+) faces anions at the crystal surface
→
step 2
Ion-dipole forces act
Must overcome lattice energy to pull ions free from crystal
→
step 3
Hydration shell forms
Released ions surrounded by water — releases hydration energy
Dissolves when: Hydration energy released ≥ lattice energy needed to break the crystal.
Insoluble when: Lattice energy is so high that even full hydration can't compensate (BaSO₄, AgCl, CaCO₃).

What makes hydration stronger?

Higher charge

Al³⁺ is hydrated far more strongly than Na⁺. More charge means stronger ion-dipole attraction to water's dipole.

Smaller ionic radius

Smaller ions concentrate their charge into less space → stronger electric field → tighter, more stable hydration shell.

High charge cuts both ways

High charge density also means high lattice energy — which is why Al₂O₃ is insoluble despite Al³⁺ being strongly hydrated.

The lattice vs hydration tug-of-war: Two ions with ±2 charges attract each other four times more strongly than ±1 pairs (Coulomb's law: force ∝ q₁q₂/r²). That's why most 2−2 and 3−2 salts (BaSO₄, Ca₃(PO₄)₂) are insoluble — their lattice energy overwhelms any hydration benefit.

How covalent compounds dissolve

Two very different sub-cases: polar vs nonpolar

Polar covalent — dissolves in water

Molecules have a dipole or can H-bond with water. New solvent–solute attractions compensate for partially breaking water's H-bond network.

  • Ethanol (C₂H₅OH) — OH group H-bonds with water
  • Glucose — multiple OH groups
  • HF, HCl (gases) — polar, dissolve readily
  • Acetone — C=O dipole interacts with water
vs
Nonpolar covalent — does NOT dissolve in water

No charge, no dipole. Water would have to break its own H-bond network to cage these molecules — energetically costly with no compensating attraction.

  • Oils, fats — long nonpolar C–C / C–H chains
  • Waxes, benzene, hexane
  • I₂, Br₂ — nonpolar; dissolve in CCl₄, not water
  • CO₂ — slight solubility only because it reacts to form H₂CO₃
The "oil and water" explanation: Water molecules near a nonpolar solute lose some H-bonding freedom. To minimize this penalty, water forms a highly ordered "cage" around the nonpolar molecule — which actually decreases entropy. The system avoids this by pushing nonpolar molecules together instead. That's the hydrophobic effect, and it's why oil droplets merge into larger ones.

The partially-miscible gray zone

!
Long-chain alcohols have a polar OH head (dissolves) and a nonpolar carbon tail (doesn't). As chain length increases, solubility drops sharply: methanol ∞ miscible → ethanol ∞ → propanol ∞ → butanol 7.7 g/100 mL → pentanol 2.2 g/100 mL → hexanol 0.6 g/100 mL. The nonpolar tail eventually overwhelms the polar head.

Electrolytes vs non-electrolytes

Does the dissolved compound produce free ions?

An electrolyte dissolves to produce free ions that can carry electric current. The key question is whether the solute ionizes in water — not merely whether it dissolves.

Strong electrolytes

100% dissociation. All dissolved units become free ions.

The 7 strong acids (memorize)
HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄, HClO₃
Strong bases
All group 1 hydroxides (NaOH, KOH…) + Ba(OH)₂, Sr(OH)₂
Soluble ionic salts
NaCl, KNO₃, CuSO₄, NH₄Cl…
Weak electrolytes

Partial dissociation. An equilibrium exists between the molecular and ionic forms.

Weak acids
CH₃COOH (acetic), HF, HNO₂, H₂CO₃, H₃PO₄, HCN…
Weak bases
NH₃ (ammonia), amines (CH₃NH₂…)
Slightly soluble salts
PbSO₄, CaF₂ — low ion concentrations
Non-electrolytes

No ionization at all. Dissolve as intact molecules — zero free ions, zero conductivity.

Examples
Sucrose, glucose, ethanol, glycerol, acetone, urea — most organic molecules
These often dissolve well in water (polar), they just don't ionize.
Classic confusion — dissolves ≠ electrolyte: Sugar dissolves beautifully in water but is a non-electrolyte (produces no ions). Conversely, AgCl barely dissolves — but every unit that does dissolves dissociates 100% into Ag⁺ and Cl⁻. Solubility and degree of ionization are completely independent.
HF is a weak acid — not a strong acid, even though all other hydrogen halides (HCl, HBr, HI) are. The H–F bond is unusually short and strong, so HF only partially ionizes: HF ⇌ H⁺ + F⁻ with Kₐ ≈ 6.8 × 10⁻⁴. This makes HF a weak electrolyte despite being a hydrogen halide.

Insoluble ionic compounds: still electrolytes?

Two independent properties that are often conflated

Short answer: technically yes — but practically, it doesn't matter.
Every ionic compound that dissolves dissociates 100% into ions. Insoluble ionic compounds are therefore strong electrolytes by definition. But because so little dissolves, the actual ion concentration is negligibly small — far too low to conduct meaningful current.

Solubility and electrolyte strength are two completely separate properties. You need to know both to understand a compound's behavior:

property 1
Degree of dissociation

What fraction of the dissolved compound breaks into ions?
For ionic compounds: always 100%. That's what being ionic means — in water, the ion-dipole forces pull them apart as individual ions, never as intact formula units.

This is what "strong / weak / non-electrolyte" describes.

property 2
Solubility

How much of the compound enters the solution in the first place?
This ranges from completely miscible down to essentially zero, and is determined by the lattice energy vs hydration energy competition.

This is what "soluble / slightly soluble / insoluble" describes.

The precise language: Chemists say an insoluble ionic compound is a "strong electrolyte with very low solubility" — keeping the two properties explicitly separate. Never call it a non-electrolyte just because it doesn't dissolve much. The electrolyte strength describes the ionization behavior; the solubility describes the quantity that enters solution. Both statements are needed.

Practical consequence

i
BaSO₄ is used as an X-ray contrast agent precisely because it is an insoluble strong electrolyte. It passes through the GI tract without dissolving (low solubility → low ion concentration → not toxic), while still being dense enough to block X-rays. If it were a non-electrolyte, it would dissolve as molecules and be absorbed; if it were merely "soluble," Ba²⁺ ions would enter the bloodstream and be highly toxic.
i
When chemists write net ionic equations, they show insoluble ionic compounds as intact formula units (e.g., AgCl(s)) rather than as separated ions. This is because so little dissolves that representing it as Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) would be misleading — the ion concentrations are negligible even though dissociation is technically complete for the tiny amount that does dissolve.

Key exceptions and tricky cases

The ones most likely to appear on exams and cause confusion

!
AgCl, AgBr, AgI — all insoluble. Silver halides are used in photography precisely because they are insoluble, light-sensitive solids. But Ag⁺ + NO₃⁻ = AgNO₃ is highly soluble. The anion changes everything — the same cation can form both soluble and insoluble salts.
!
BaSO₄ — insoluble, even though BaCl₂ and Ba(NO₃)₂ are soluble. BaSO₄'s enormous lattice energy (Ba²⁺ × SO₄²⁻, double charges) overwhelms hydration. Used as an X-ray contrast agent because it passes through the body undissolved — and undissociated in meaningful quantities.
!
Ca(OH)₂ — slightly soluble, not insoluble. Dissolves enough (~1.5 g/L) to make limewater — a dilute but functional basic solution. Often listed inconsistently across textbooks; what matters is that it is not freely soluble like NaOH, and any dissolved portion dissociates completely (strong electrolyte).
!
PbCl₂ — soluble in hot water, insoluble in cold. Most ionic compounds become more soluble as temperature rises. PbCl₂ demonstrates this dramatically — it precipitates on cooling. This is the opposite behavior from gases, which become less soluble on heating.
!
H₂SO₄ — the second proton is weak. Sulfuric acid fully donates its first H⁺ (H₂SO₄ → H⁺ + HSO₄⁻, strong step) but only partially donates the second (HSO₄⁻ ⇌ H⁺ + SO₄²⁻, Kₐ₂ ≈ 0.012). It is a diprotic acid with one strong step and one weak step.
!
NH₃ — weak base, not a non-electrolyte. Ammonia dissolves extremely well in water (700 volumes of gas per volume of water) but is a weak electrolyte — only a small fraction reacts: NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻ (Kb ≈ 1.8 × 10⁻⁵). Highly soluble + weak electrolyte.
i
CO₂ — dissolves slightly, then reacts. CO₂ is nonpolar, yet has some water solubility (~1.7 g/L at 25°C) because it reacts: CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃. Carbonic acid then partially ionizes. This is why carbonated drinks are acidic and why CO₂ emissions drive ocean acidification.
i
Hg₂²⁺ (mercury(I)) — almost always insoluble. The dimeric Hg₂²⁺ ion forms insoluble chloride, bromide, iodide, sulfate, carbonate, phosphate, and hydroxide. Hg₂(NO₃)₂ and Hg₂(ClO₄)₂ are among the few soluble mercury(I) salts.

The thermodynamic "why"

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS — when and why dissolution is spontaneous

Dissolution is spontaneous when Gibbs free energy decreases: ΔG < 0, which means ΔH − TΔS < 0.

Enthalpy of solution (ΔH_soln)

Sum of three steps (Hess's law):

+Lattice energy — endothermic, breaking the crystal
+Expanding solvent — endothermic, parting water molecules
−Hydration energy — exothermic, ions surrounded by water
ΔH_soln can be + or −. NaCl ≈ +3.9 kJ/mol (endothermic). LiCl ≈ −37 kJ/mol (exothermic).
Entropy of solution (ΔS_soln)

Mixing almost always increases entropy — ions/molecules go from an ordered solid to dispersed in solution.

ΔS_soln is almost always positive for dissolution of solids.

Exception: highly charged ions create such an ordered hydration shell that local entropy decreases (electrostriction). But overall solution entropy still usually increases.
Why are some things insoluble if entropy always favors dissolving? The TΔS term is usually too small to overcome a very large positive ΔH (huge lattice energy, poor hydration). When ΔH ≫ TΔS, ΔG stays positive — dissolution is non-spontaneous. BaSO₄ has ΔG_soln ≈ +59 kJ/mol; entropy simply cannot overcome that.
Temperature effect: Raising T magnifies the TΔS term. That's why most solids get more soluble at higher temperatures (entropy wins more at higher T). Gases show the opposite — dissolving a gas is exothermic (ΔH < 0), so raising T shifts equilibrium away from dissolution (Le Chatelier's principle).

Quick decision map — put it all together

ionic compound → check solubility rules → if soluble, it's a strong electrolyte (100% dissociation). If insoluble, it's still technically a strong electrolyte but with negligible ion concentration.
polar covalent → likely dissolves in water → check ionization: strong acid / weak acid / weak base / non-electrolyte (e.g. sugar, ethanol)
nonpolar covalent → does not dissolve in water → dissolves in nonpolar solvents → non-electrolyte
exception zone → HF (polar, dissolves, but weak acid) · long-chain alcohols (partial) · CO₂ (reacts) · BaSO₄ (ionic, insoluble, technically strong electrolyte) · H₂SO₄ (diprotic: 1 strong + 1 weak step)
The two properties to always state separately:
(1) Solubility — how much enters solution. Determined by lattice energy vs hydration energy.
(2) Electrolyte strength — what fraction of what dissolved becomes ions. For ionic compounds: always 100% (strong). For covalent acids/bases: depends on Kₐ or Kb. For sugars and organics: 0% (non-electrolyte).
