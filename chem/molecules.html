🔷 Part 1: VSEPR Theory — Predicting Molecular Shapes

What Is VSEPR?

VSEPR (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion) states one simple rule:

Electron domains around a central atom push as far apart as possible to minimize repulsion. The resulting arrangement of atoms gives the molecule its shape.

An electron domain is any group of electrons — a single bond, double bond, triple bond, or lone pair all count as one domain.

The 3-Step VSEPR Method

  1. Draw the Lewis Structure — find how many bonding pairs and lone pairs surround the central atom.
  2. Count total electron domains (bonding + lone pairs). This gives the electron geometry.
  3. Identify the molecular shape by looking only at where the atoms are (ignore lone pairs for the name, but they still push things around).
Key distinction:
Electron geometry = all domains (including lone pairs)
Molecular geometry / shape = only the atoms (ignores lone pairs)
Lone pair rule: Lone pairs are "fatter" clouds and repel more strongly than bonding pairs. They compress bond angles slightly below the ideal geometry.

Ideal tetrahedral = 109.5° → NH₃ (1 lone pair) = 107° → H₂O (2 lone pairs) = 104.5°

⚡ Part 2: Polarity & Dipole Moments

From Bond Polarity → Molecular Polarity

A polar bond forms when two atoms share electrons unequally (different electronegativities). Each polar bond creates a bond dipole — an arrow pointing toward the more electronegative atom.

Molecular polarity depends on TWO things:
1. Are there polar bonds? (individual bond dipoles)
2. Do those dipoles cancel out? → determined by shape

If the dipoles point in exactly opposite directions and cancel → nonpolar.
If they don't cancel → polar (the molecule has a net dipole moment, δ+ and δ− ends).

NONPOLAR
CO₂: linear shape causes the two C=O dipoles to point in exactly opposite directions — they cancel perfectly.
POLAR
H₂O: bent shape means the two O–H dipoles both point toward O — they add together, creating a strong net dipole moment.
🔗 Part 3: Intermolecular Forces (IMF)

What Are IMFs and Why Do They Matter?

Intermolecular forces (IMF) are the attractions between molecules — not the bonds inside them. They are much weaker than covalent or ionic bonds, but they control:

  • Boiling point and melting point
  • Viscosity (how thick a liquid is)
  • Surface tension
  • Solubility ("like dissolves like")
Rule: Stronger IMFs → higher boiling point (more energy needed to separate molecules into gas).
☁️

1. London Dispersion Forces (LDF)

Also called van der Waals forces — present in ALL molecules

Even nonpolar molecules have momentary, random shifts in electron distribution. This creates an instantaneous dipole that induces a temporary dipole in a neighboring molecule, causing a weak attraction.

Strength depends on: the number of electrons (larger molecules / more electrons = stronger LDF = higher boiling point).
Example: F₂ (bp −188°C) < Cl₂ (bp −34°C) < Br₂ (bp 59°C) — all nonpolar halogens, increasing bp due to more electrons.
↔️

2. Dipole–Dipole Forces

Only in polar molecules

Polar molecules have a permanent δ+ end and a δ− end. They align so that the positive end of one molecule is attracted to the negative end of the next.

Example: HCl molecules (bp −85°C) — stronger than LDF-only molecules of similar size. Acetone is held together by dipole–dipole forces.

💧

3. Hydrogen Bonding

Special case — only when H is bonded to N, O, or F

H bonded to a highly electronegative atom (N, O, F) becomes extremely positive (δ+). It is attracted to a lone pair on a neighboring N, O, or F. This is the strongest IMF between neutral molecules.

The big three: N–H ···N, O–H ···O, F–H ···F  (memorize these!)
Why water is special: Each H₂O can form up to 4 hydrogen bonds (2 as donor, 2 as acceptor) — this is why water has a surprisingly high boiling point (100°C) for its tiny size.
⚡

4. Ion–Dipole Forces

Between an ion and a polar molecule — the strongest IMF

When an ionic compound dissolves in a polar solvent (like water), the ions are surrounded by polar molecules oriented toward them. This is the force that makes dissolving possible!

Example: NaCl dissolving in water — Na⁺ ions are surrounded by water molecules with their δ− (O side) facing the ion; Cl⁻ ions are surrounded by water with their δ+ (H side) facing the ion.

Quick IMF Decision Tree

  1. Is it ionic? → Ion–Dipole if mixing with polar solvent. Done.
  2. Does it contain N–H, O–H, or F–H bonds? → Hydrogen Bonding (+ LDF). Done.
  3. Is it polar? (asymmetric shape) → Dipole–Dipole (+ LDF). Done.
  4. None of the above? → London Dispersion Forces only.
