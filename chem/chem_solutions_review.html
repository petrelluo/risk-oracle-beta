⭐ Topic 8.2 — The Dissolving Process (Teacher's Guided Notes — HIGH PRIORITY)
🔥
Teacher Priority: This is directly from Dr. Suzette's own guided notes. Expect questions on ionic vs. molecular dissolving, the salt-vs-sugar comparison, ion counting, AgNO₃ special case, and drawing/describing beaker diagrams.

🧂 Ionic Compounds — How They Dissolve

  • Ionic compounds dissociate — they break apart into separate ions
  • Each ion is then hydrated (surrounded by water molecules)
  • Water's δ− (oxygen end) surrounds cations (+)
  • Water's δ+ (hydrogen end) surrounds anions (−)
  • Condition: ion-water attraction must be stronger than ion-ion attraction

🍬 Polar Molecular Compounds — How They Dissolve

  • Polar molecules dissolve because their charged regions attract water's dipoles
  • They do NOT dissociate — molecules stay intact
  • Molecules separate from each other and become hydrated
  • Most nonpolar molecules do NOT dissolve in water
  • Rule: "Like dissolves like" — polar in polar, nonpolar in nonpolar

⭐ AgNO₃ — The Polyatomic Ion Special Case

⚠️ What Makes AgNO₃ Tricky

  • AgNO₃ is ionic → does dissociate into Ag⁺ and NO₃⁻
  • BUT — NO₃⁻ (nitrate) is a polyatomic ion = a charged molecule
  • Therefore NO₃⁻ does NOT further break apart
  • In the beaker: Ag⁺ (hydrated) and NO₃⁻ (hydrated, intact)

📌 Polyatomic Ion Rule

When an ionic compound with a polyatomic ion dissolves, the polyatomic ion separates as a unit — its atoms do NOT split further.

Other examples:
Na₂SO₄ → 2Na⁺ + SO₄²⁻
K₂CO₃ → 2K⁺ + CO₃²⁻
Ca(NO₃)₂ → Ca²⁺ + 2NO₃⁻

⭐ Teacher's Key Ideas — Fill-in-Blank Answers

Completed Key Ideas from Guided Notes

  • Most ionic compounds dissociate into separate ions when dissolved in water.
  • For an ionic substance to dissolve, the ion-ion attraction must be weaker than the attraction to water's dipoles.
  • Many polar molecules dissolve in water because their charged regions attract the oppositely charged regions on water. Most nonpolar molecules do not dissolve.
  • When polar molecules dissolve, they remain intact and do not dissociate, but they separate and become hydrated by water.

§16.3 — Rate of Dissolving (also in Teacher Slides)
🎯
Dr. Suzette's slides make a key distinction: Solubility = whether/how much dissolves. Collision Theory = the RATE at which it dissolves. These are different!

🌊 Agitation (Stirring)

Brings fresh solvent into contact with solute. Increases rate only — does NOT change how much dissolves (solubility).

🌡️ Temperature

↑ temp → ↑ kinetic energy → more frequent, harder collisions. Increases both rate AND amount (for solid solutes).

🔩 Surface Area

Grinding/crushing creates more contact points. Increases rate only — total solubility unchanged. More surface = more collisions.

⚠️
Stirring and surface area affect only rate. Temperature affects both rate AND (for solids) how much dissolves. This is a common quiz trap.
§16.4 — Saturated & Unsaturated Solutions (also in Teacher Slides)

💧 Unsaturated

Contains less solute than the maximum possible. Can dissolve more solute.

→ Test: add more solute → it dissolves = unsaturated

⚖️ Saturated

Contains the maximum dissolved solute at that temperature. Rate of dissolving = rate of recrystallization (solution equilibrium).

→ Test: add more → it settles undissolved = saturated

🔴 Supersaturated

Contains more dissolved solute than should be possible. Extremely unstable. Adding a seed crystal → rapid crystallization.

→ Created by cooling a hot saturated solution slowly

🔬
Solution equilibrium: At saturation, dissolving and recrystallization happen at equal rates simultaneously — it is a dynamic equilibrium, not static. Recrystallization (dissolved solute returning to solid) is used in labs to purify compounds.
§16.5 — Temperature & Solubility (also in Teacher Slides)

🏔️ Solid Solutes in Water

  • ↑ temperature → ↑ solubility (direct relationship, most solids)
  • NaCl: nearly flat curve — barely changes with temp
  • KNO₃: steep curve — dramatically more soluble at higher temp
  • Why: higher KE → more/harder collisions → more dissolves

🫧 Gas Solutes in Water

  • ↑ temperature → ↓ solubility (inverse relationship)
  • Higher KE → gas molecules escape liquid more easily
  • ↑ pressure → ↑ gas solubility (direct) — Henry's Law
  • Example: sealed soda under pressure → CO₂ stays dissolved; open it → fizz

⭐ Coulombic Attraction & Ionic Solubility (Teacher Slides)

⚡ What is Coulombic Attraction?

  • Attraction between opposite charges
  • Two factors: magnitude of charges & distance between charges
  • Lower charge → weaker attraction → more soluble
  • Smaller ion → closer distance → stronger attraction → less soluble when ion-ion wins, but smaller ions hydrate better

📏 Ion Properties & Solubility

  • Lower charge → MORE soluble (Na⁺, K⁺ more soluble than Ca²⁺)
  • Smaller ion → MORE soluble (NaNO₃ more soluble than KNO₃ because Na⁺ < K⁺)
  • Threshold: >0.1 mol/L in 1L water = "soluble"
§15.7 & §15.9 — Electrolytes & Nonelectrolytes (also in Teacher Slides)
💡
Dr. Suzette's slides ask: "Why do we call dissolved ionic compounds electrolytes?" — because they break into ions that can carry electrical current through the solution.
TypeIonizationConductivityArrowExamples
Strong Electrolyte~100% ionizedHigh→ (complete)NaCl, KCl, HCl(aq), HNO₃, H₂SO₄, NaOH, KOH; all soluble ionic compounds
Weak ElectrolytePartial ionizationLow⇌ (equilibrium)Acetic acid (CH₃COOH), NH₃, HF, carbonic acid
NonelectrolyteNo ionizationNoneNo ions formedSugar, ethanol, CO, glucose
🔋
HCl special case (§15.9): Pure HCl gas = nonelectrolyte. Dissolved in water → completely ionizes: HCl(g) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → becomes a strong electrolyte. Car batteries use H₂SO₄ — a strong electrolyte that provides ions for conductivity.
🏃
Sports drinks (§15.7): Contain electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻) lost through sweat. These ions conduct electrical signals needed for muscle contraction and nerve function. All ionic compounds are electrolytes — even insoluble ones conduct in the molten state.
§16.9 — Molarity (also in Teacher Slides)
Molarity Formula
M = molessolute / literssolution
M in mol/L · Convert mL ÷ 1000 to get L · Volume = total SOLUTION, not just water

Example 1 — Find M (from teacher slides)

10.0g LiCl (MM=42.5) in 100 mL solution:

mol = 10.0 ÷ 42.5 = 0.235 mol

M = 0.235 ÷ 0.100 L = 2.35 M

Example 2 — Find moles

How many moles for 250 mL of 1.5 M Cu(NO₃)₂?

moles = M × V = 1.5 × 0.250 = 0.375 mol

Example 3 — Find volume

Volume of 2.0 M KF from 50.0g KF (MM=58.1)?

mol = 50.0 ÷ 58.1 = 0.861 mol

V = 0.861 ÷ 2.0 = 0.43 L

Example 4 — Find grams

500 mL of 0.5 M Cu(NO₃)₂ (MM=187.6)?

mol = 0.5 × 0.500 = 0.25 mol

g = 0.25 × 187.6 = 46.9 g

🎯
Why molarity? Percent solutions tell you mass, not particles. Molarity lets you directly relate volume to moles of particles — essential for chemical reactions (stoichiometry).
§16.11 — Preparing Solutions (also in Teacher Slides)

4 Steps to Make a Solution from a Solid

  1. Calculate moles needed → convert to grams (using molar mass)
  2. Weigh out the correct mass of solid
  3. Add solid to a volumetric flask half-filled with distilled water; swirl until dissolved
  4. Add distilled water up to the calibration mark on the flask neck; cap and invert to mix

🧪 Volumetric Flask

  • Pear-shaped flask calibrated for ONE exact volume
  • A 1L flask can ONLY make 1L — cannot make 500 mL
  • You cannot add solid to 1L of water → hydrated ions change volume
  • Always fill to the calibration line, not estimated

⚗️ Teacher Examples (from slides)

  • 500 mL of 1.2 M NaOH: mol=0.60, g=24.0g NaOH → place in 500 mL flask, fill to line
  • 50 mL of 0.25 M Na₂CO₃ (MM=106): mol=0.0125, g=1.33g → place in 50 mL flask, fill to line
§16.12 — Dilution (also in Teacher Slides)
Dilution Equation ⭐ HIGH PRIORITY
Mc × Vc = Md × Vd
c = concentrated (stock solution) · d = dilute (final solution) · Moles of solute are CONSERVED — only volume increases

Example — From teacher slides

Make 1.0 L of 1.0 M HCl from 11.7 M stock:

11.7 × Vc = 1.0 × 1.0 → Vc = 0.0855 L = 85.5 mL

⚠️ ALWAYS add acid to water — never water to acid!

Example 2

Make 500 mL of 1.0 M acetic acid from 17.6 M stock:

17.6 × Vc = 1.0 × 0.500 → Vc = 0.0284 L = 28.4 mL

⭐ Bonus: Blood as a Solution + Miscibility (Teacher Slides)

🩸 Blood is Three Things at Once

  • Solution — of salts, glucose, urea, and small compounds
  • Suspension — of blood cells and proteins
  • Colloid — of plasma proteins

🔄 Miscible vs Immiscible

  • Miscible: mix completely, no phase separation (water + vinegar)
  • Immiscible: separate into phases (water + oil)
  • Why: different IMFs — like dissolves like
  • Dilute = less solute · Concentrated = more solute
⭐ Concentration — Types of Measurements (Teacher's Quiz List)
🎯
Three concentration types on the teacher's quiz list: (1) Molarity M = mol/L  ·  (2) % Composition = g solute / g solution × 100  ·  (3) g solute / 100g water (used on solubility graphs). Know how they differ!

① Molarity (M)

M = moles solute / L solution

Used for chemical reactions (stoichiometry). Based on volume of solution.

Example: 0.5 mol NaCl in 2.0 L = 0.25 M

② % Composition (mass %)

% = (g solute / g solution) × 100

g solution = g solute + g solvent. Used in Kool-Aid Lab.

Example: 25g NaCl in 225g water → solution = 250g → % = (25/250)×100 = 10%

③ g solute / 100g water

= g solute ÷ g water × 100

Used on solubility graphs. Note: denominator = water only (NOT total solution).

Example: 25g NaCl in 225g water = 25/225 × 100 = 11.1 g/100g water

⚠️
Key difference: % composition uses g of total SOLUTION (solute + solvent) in denominator. Solubility graphs use g of WATER only. These give different numbers for the same solution! (10% ≠ 11.1 g/100g water above.)

% Composition — Worked Examples

  • Kool-Aid type: 6g Kool-Aid powder dissolved in 294g water → g solution = 300g → % = 6/300 × 100 = 2.0%
  • Find grams from %: Make 200g of 15% sugar solution → g sugar = 0.15 × 200 = 30g sugar
  • Find % given grams: 40g KNO₃ in 160g water → g solution = 200g → % = 40/200 × 100 = 20%

📚 Key Terms
All vocabulary from teacher materials and assigned CK-12 chapters.
Solution
Homogeneous mixture of solute dissolved in solvent. Can be any state (gas, liquid, solid).
Solute
The substance being dissolved (present in smaller amount). Example: NaCl in saltwater.
Solvent
The substance doing the dissolving (larger amount). Water = "universal solvent."
Aqueous Solution
Any solution where water is the solvent.
Dissolving / Dissolution
Process where solute particles separate and spread through solvent.
Dissociation ⭐
Ionic compound separating into component ions. ONLY occurs in ionic compounds — NOT molecular ones.
Hydrated ⭐
Ion or molecule surrounded (enclosed) by water molecules in solution.
Solubility
Maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in 100g of solvent at a specific temperature.
Unsaturated
Solution that can dissolve more solute at that temperature.
Saturated
Contains the maximum dissolved solute; at solution equilibrium.
Supersaturated
Contains more dissolved solute than should be possible — highly unstable.
Solution Equilibrium
Dynamic state where rate of dissolving equals rate of recrystallization.
Recrystallization
Dissolved solute returning to solid state. Used in labs to purify compounds.
Seed Crystal
Small crystal added to a supersaturated solution to trigger rapid crystallization.
Rate of Dissolution
How fast a solute dissolves. Affected by: agitation, temperature, surface area.
Agitation
Stirring/mixing — increases dissolution rate only, not solubility.
Surface Area
More surface → more solvent contact → faster rate (not more dissolved).
Concentration
Amount of solute in a given amount of solvent or solution.
Dilute vs Concentrated
Dilute = less solute; Concentrated = more solute. Qualitative terms.
Molarity (M) ⭐
Moles of solute per liter of SOLUTION. M = mol/L. Changes with temperature.
Stock Solution
Concentrated solution of known concentration used to make diluted solutions.
Volumetric Flask
Pear-shaped flask calibrated for ONE exact volume; used for precise solution preparation.
Dilution ⭐
Adding solvent to decrease concentration. Moles conserved: M꜀V꜀ = M_dV_d.
Electrolyte
Compound that conducts electricity in aqueous solution — produces mobile ions.
Nonelectrolyte
Compound that does NOT conduct electricity; dissolves without producing ions (e.g., sugar, ethanol).
Strong Electrolyte
Completely ionizes. Includes all ionic compounds, strong acids (HCl, HNO₃, H₂SO₄), strong bases (NaOH, KOH).
Weak Electrolyte
Only partially ionizes. Uses ⇌ in equation. Examples: acetic acid, NH₃, HF.
Ionization
Molecular compound breaking into ions when dissolved (especially acids).
Miscible ⭐
Two liquids that mix completely with no phase separation (e.g., water + vinegar).
Immiscible ⭐
Liquids that separate into phases (e.g., water + oil) due to different IMFs.
Alloy
Solid-solid solution. Examples: brass (Cu+Zn), bronze (Cu+Sn), sterling silver (Ag+Cu).
Polar Molecule ⭐
Uneven charge distribution (e.g., H₂O, sucrose, NH₃). Dissolves in water.
Nonpolar Molecule ⭐
Even charge distribution (e.g., CO, oil, hexane). Does NOT dissolve in water.
Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)
Attractive forces between molecules. "Like dissolves like" = similar IMFs dissolve each other.
Coulombic Attraction ⭐
Attraction between opposite charges. Governs ionic solubility. Depends on charge magnitude and distance.
Polyatomic Ion ⭐
Charged group of bonded atoms (e.g., NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻). Does NOT further dissociate when parent compound dissolves.
Henry's Law
Gas solubility ∝ partial pressure above liquid. ↑ pressure → ↑ gas dissolves. Explains soda fizzing.
Solubility Curve ⭐
Graph of solubility (g/100g water) vs. temperature. Read: below=unsaturated, on=saturated, above=supersaturated.
Blood (teacher slide)
Simultaneously: a solution (salts, glucose), a suspension (blood cells), and a colloid (plasma proteins).
