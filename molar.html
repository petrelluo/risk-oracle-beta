From the "Chemistry Dozen" to Liquid Concentration & Dilution

1. The Chemistry Dozen (Avogadro's Number)

In the kitchen, we use a Dozen to group 12 eggs. In the lab, atoms are so small we need a much bigger group. We call this the Mole.

  • A Dozen: 12 items
  • A Mole: 602,200,000,000,000,000,000,000 items (6.022 x 10²³)

This scale shows that a Mole is like a trillion, trillion times bigger than a dozen. It's the only way to get enough atoms to actually see them!

2. Molar Mass: The Identity Weight

One mole of Carbon and one mole of Gold both have the same count of atoms, but Gold atoms are much "fatter."

Grams = Moles × Molar Mass

Example: Weighing Water

Problem: You have 2.0 moles of pure water (H₂O). How many grams is this?
Step 1: Find the Molar Mass H₂O = (1.01 × 2) + 16.00 = 18.02 g/mol
Step 2: Multiply Moles by Molar Mass 2.0 mol × 18.02 g/mol = 36.04 Grams

4. Molarity: The Particle Crowd

Molarity measures how many moles are "crowded" into 1 Liter. If you add more water, the room gets bigger and the crowd thins out.

Molarity (M) = Moles ÷ Liters

Example: The Salty Mix

Problem: You dissolve 0.5 moles of table salt (NaCl) into 2.0 Liters of water. What is the Molarity?
Step 1: Divide Moles by Volume 0.5 mol ÷ 2.0 L = 0.25 M (Concentration)

5. Dilution: The "Watering Down" Rule

Dilution is adding water to a strong solution to make it weaker. The Secret: The amount of solute (the moles) never changes, only the water changes!

Notice the seesaw effect: As Volume goes UP, Molarity goes DOWN proportionally.

M₁ × V₁ = M₂ × V₂

Example: Watering it Down

Problem: You have 0.1 Liters of a very strong 5.0 M Stock Solution. You add water until the total volume reaches 1.0 Liter. What is the new Molarity?
Step 1: Set up M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ 5.0 M × 0.1 L = M₂ × 1.0 L
Step 2: Solve for M₂ (New Molarity) 0.5 = M₂ × 1.0
New Molarity = 0.5 M
