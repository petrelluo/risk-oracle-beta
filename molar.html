From the "Chemistry Dozen" to Liquid Concentration & Dilution

1. The Chemistry Dozen (Avogadro's Number)

In the kitchen, we use a Dozen to group 12 eggs. In the lab, atoms are so small we need a much bigger group. We call this the Mole.

  • A Dozen: 12 items
  • A Mole: 602,200,000,000,000,000,000,000 items (6.022 x 10²³)

Example A: Moles to Particles (Simple)

Problem: You have 0.5 moles of pure Gold (Au). How many atoms is this?
Step 1: Multiply Moles by Avogadro's Number 0.5 mol × (6.022 x 10²³) = 3.011 x 10²³ Atoms of Gold

Example B: The Molecule/Ion Trick

Problem: You have 2.0 moles of Calcium Chloride (CaCl₂). How many individual Chloride ions (Cl⁻) do you have?
Step 1: Find total Molecules first 2.0 mol × (6.022 x 10²³) = 1.204 x 10²⁴ Molecules of CaCl₂
Step 2: Multiply by the number of ions inside Every 1 molecule of CaCl₂ contains 2 Chloride ions.
(1.204 x 10²⁴) × 2 = 2.408 x 10²⁴ Chloride Ions

2. Molar Mass: The Identity Weight

One mole of Carbon and one mole of Gold both have the same count of atoms, but Gold atoms are much "fatter."

Grams = Moles × Molar Mass

Example: Weighing Water

Problem: You have 2.0 moles of pure water (H₂O). How many grams is this?
Step 1: Find the Molar Mass (from Periodic Table) H₂O = (1.01 × 2) + 16.00 = 18.02 g/mol
Step 2: Multiply Moles by Molar Mass 2.0 mol × 18.02 g/mol = 36.04 Grams

4. Molarity: The Particle Crowd

Molarity measures how many moles are "crowded" into 1 Liter. If you add more water, the room gets bigger and the crowd thins out.

Molarity (M) = Moles ÷ Liters

Example: The Salty Mix

Problem: You dissolve 0.5 moles of table salt (NaCl) into 2.0 Liters of water. What is the Molarity?
Step 1: Divide Moles by Volume 0.5 mol ÷ 2.0 L = 0.25 M (Concentration)

5. Dilution: The "Watering Down" Rule

Dilution is adding water to a strong solution to make it weaker. The Secret: The amount of solute (the moles) never changes, only the water changes! Notice how the color gets lighter as the particles spread out into a larger volume.

M₁ × V₁ = M₂ × V₂

Example: Watering it Down

Problem: You have 0.1 Liters of a very strong 5.0 M Stock Solution. You add water until the total volume reaches 1.0 Liter. What is the new Molarity?
Step 1: Set up M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ (5.0 M) × (0.1 L) = (M₂) × (1.0 L)
Step 2: Solve for M₂ (New Molarity) 0.5 = M₂ × 1.0
New Molarity = 0.5 M

6. Step-by-Step Conversion Lab (Full Map Challenges)

Challenge 1: Mass to Particles (The Jewelry Shop)

Problem: You have a 19.7 gram gold (Au) ring. How many gold atoms are in it?
Step 1: Go to the Hub (Grams → Moles) Divide by Molar Mass of Gold (197 g/mol).
19.7 g ÷ 197 g/mol = 0.1 Moles
Step 2: Go to Particles (Moles → Atoms) Multiply by Avogadro's Number.
0.1 mol × (6.022 x 10²³) = 6.022 x 10²² Atoms of Gold

Challenge 2: Molarity to Mass (The Medicine Cabinet)

Problem: You need to make 0.5 Liters of a 2.0 M Sugar (C₆H₁₂O₆) solution. How many grams of sugar powder do you need to weigh out?
Step 1: Find the Moles needed (Molarity → Moles) Multiply Molarity by Liters.
2.0 M × 0.5 L = 1.0 Mole
Step 2: Convert Moles to Grams (Moles → Mass) Multiply by Molar Mass of Sugar (180 g/mol).
1.0 mol × 180 g/mol = 180 Grams of Sugar

Challenge 3: Particles to Molarity (The Water Treatment Lab)

Problem: A 2.0 Liter sample of water contains 3.011 x 10²³ molecules of a pollutant. What is the Molarity of the pollutant?
Step 1: Go to the Hub (Particles → Moles) Divide by Avogadro's Number.
(3.011 x 10²³) ÷ (6.022 x 10²³) = 0.5 Moles
Step 2: Find Molarity (Moles → Concentration) Divide Moles by Volume.
0.5 mol ÷ 2.0 L = 0.25 M
