1. Polyatomic Ions

These are tightly bound groups of atoms that behave as a unit and carry a charge. When a formula requires more than one of a polyatomic ion, place it in parentheses with the subscript outside (e.g., Al₂(SO₄)₃).

2. Binary Ionic Compounds

Formed by a metal (cation) and a nonmetal (anion). Name the metal normally, and change the ending of the nonmetal to "-ide". To write the formula, determine the typical charges based on their periodic table group and balance them to zero.

Charge Rules: Group 1 is +1, Group 2 is +2, Group 15 is -3, Group 16 is -2, Halogens (Group 17) are -1.

3. Transition Metals (Stock System)

Transition metals (and some post-transition metals like Tin and Lead) can form ions with different charges. A Roman numeral indicates the exact positive charge of the metal ion.

Example: Iron(III) oxide means Fe has a +3 charge, resulting in Fe₂O₃.

Exceptions (No Roman Numeral Needed): Zinc (Zn²⁺), Silver (Ag⁺), and Cadmium (Cd²⁺) always have the same charge.

4. Covalent (Molecular) Compounds

Formed by two nonmetals sharing electrons. Greek prefixes are used to specify the exact number of atoms of each element. The second element ends in "-ide". (Note: Never use 'mono-' on the first element).
